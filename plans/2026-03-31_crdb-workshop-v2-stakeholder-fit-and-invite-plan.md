## 2. Stakeholder fit analysis

### 2.1 What the workshop design is trying to do

The approved narrative anchors CRDB workshop v2 on Strategy 2 Service Bundle Demonstration packaged as a Strategy 1 style steering decision package with a mandatory NCAIF website blueprint annex. The preliminary plan already positions the workshop as:

- validation and convergence rather than initial scoping
- locking demand backbone and service tiers
- testing baseline rules and access logic
- shaping governance roles and operating rhythm

This matches the strongest cross-agency demand clusters in the Chapter 2 synthesis:

- authoritative baselines and trusted reference point
- planning-grade and engineering-ready datasets
- machine-readable and GIS-ready access
- tiered service design and trustworthy communication
- access, publishing, and governance pathways

### 2.2 How interview-based needs map onto sessions

- **FTI** single source of truth, poor government UX, and business interruption evidence
  - Sessions 2 and 3 provide a place to test how baselines and service tiers will actually support private-sector planning and budget justification.
  - Session 5 synthesis can surface explicit recommendations on economic loss translation and how NCAIF products help firms defend investments.
- **UDDC** double vulnerability, budget defence, and high-resolution NbS design
  - Sessions 2 and 3 are well aligned with UDDC needs for planning-grade downscaled hazard inputs and socio-economic overlays.
  - The website blueprint annex can include a sector/local planning view where UDDC-type users can discover both maps and raw data.
- **BMA** operations, interoperability, and urban heat/flood services
  - Sessions 2 and 4 can draw out operator versus decision-maker service layers that respond directly to BMA requirements for role-specific services.
  - Baseline and access-rail discussion speaks to BMA frustrations about manual website checking and weak APIs.
- **DPT** multi-scale planning mandate and engineering-ready inputs
  - Session 3 is strongly aligned with DPT demands for climate-adjusted rainfall and GIS-ready datasets.
  - Governance and rhythm mapping in Session 4 can make explicit who maintains the datasets DPT relies on and at what frequency.

Across these interviews the current session structure has a good conceptual fit. The main risk is not design misalignment but **under-representation of some actors in the room** for each decision type (private sector, finance/insurance, sub-national authorities, NFCS steering).

### 2.3 Coverage and gaps by stakeholder category

From the synthesis note the recurring agencies and roles fall into the following categories:

- National economic and planning: NESDC, OTP, TBA, NXPO
- National data system owners and integrators: DGA, NSO, DCCE
- Hazard and climate service providers: TMD and allied technical units (implied via NFCS quote bank and consultation references)
- Local and sectoral implementers: DLA/LAOs, BMA, UDDC, DPT, MSDHS, sector line ministries
- Disaster risk management: DDPM
- Private sector: FTI and potentially sector associations

Workshop v2 as currently framed:

- is strong on data system owners and technical implementers (CRDB, DCCE, BTR, CRI and similar)
- has explicit design intent to include local implementers (LAOs, BMA) and sector planners
- implicitly needs NFCS steering bodies but does not yet spell out their presence in the invite logic
- recognises private sector insight in the evidence base but does not yet guarantee private sector seats in the room

Gaps that matter for NFCS-safe steering and deliverability:

- **NFCS ownership layer**: NFCS steering committee, inter-ministerial oversight or equivalent is not yet clearly represented in stakeholder lists, though it is central in WMO governance wording.
- **Financial and economic decision makers**: NESDC, TBA, budget and financing actors must be present if baseline and service bundle decisions are to be fiscally credible.
- **Disaster risk operations**: DDPM and provincial DRR units are key for translating service bundles into preparedness and response workflows.
- **Representative non-Bangkok local authorities**: DLA plus at least one secondary-city municipality are needed so the workshop does not over-fit to BMA.

## 3. TOR expectation of at least 15 organizations

### 3.1 Is 15 organizations sensible

The Chapter 2 synthesis already references more than 15 distinct organizations across clusters. Within the NFCS governance framing WMO recommends inter-ministerial oversight, NMHS leadership, and sector user interface platforms. A workshop that aims to lock Human Settlement service bundles, baseline rules, and governance rhythms will inherently span:

- economic planning and budgeting
- climate and hazard service provision
- data infrastructure and catalog governance
- local implementation and operations
- disaster risk management
- private sector demand and co-investment

Under that logic a floor of 15 organizations is sensible. Fewer organizations would either under-represent critical functions (governance, finance, disaster operations) or compress too many roles into single institutions in ways that contradict the WMO governance patterns.

### 3.2 Is 15 organizations achievable

Evidence from the interviews shows that many of these agencies are already engaged in CRDB work (NESDC, NXPO, DGA, NSO, DLA, MSDHS, DDPM, BMA, DPT, FTI, UDDC and others). The preliminary workshop note also anticipates a large participant group (~80 people) and already categorises roles into data providers, users, governance actors, and facilitators.

Given this starting point, reaching or slightly exceeding 15 organizations is operationally achievable if:

- invitations are anchored on the existing interview list and NFCS consultation participants
- each major function has at least one institutional representative
- the design maintains breakouts where participants can work in functionally coherent groups rather than an undifferentiated plenary

The main operational risk is **coordination and calendar friction**, not lack of candidates.

## 4. Recommended stakeholder additions

### 4.1 Within already engaged ecosystems

Prioritise ensuring attendance from the following organizations based on explicit needs in the synthesis:

- NESDC (macroeconomic framing and loss-and-damage translation)
- NXPO (research funding and accountability perspectives on baselines)
- DGA (data exchange governance and open/non-open/GDX rails)
- NSO (official statistics and registry linkages)
- DDPM (disaster risk and emergency operations alignment)
- DLA and at least one non-Bangkok municipality (to complement BMA city operations)
- MSDHS (vulnerability indicators and sensitive data governance)
- FTI (anchor for private sector needs and co-investment logic)
- UDDC and DPT (anchors for planning-grade, engineering-ready use cases)

### 4.2 Additional categories to invite explicitly

To complete the NFCS-consistent chain the following categories should be added where feasible:

- **NFCS steering and oversight layer**: representatives from the NFCS inter-ministerial committee or equivalent high-level policy body so that the steering decision package has an in-room owner.
- **NMHS leadership**: TMD as climate service lead to align NFCS guidance that NFCS is initiated and led by NMHS while respecting DCCE-sponsored NCAIF delivery.
- **Financial governance and risk entities**: such as TBA and, where appropriate, budget bureau or fiscal policy representatives to ground economic loss and insurance-related decisions.
- **Sector regulators** linked to Human Settlement (energy, housing, transport, utilities) to ensure the service bundles map onto real regulatory and investment levers.

These recommendations remain consistent with TOR constraints and with WMO NFCS governance patterns while extending the invitee list beyond the immediate CRDB working circle.

## 5. Devil s-advocate stress test

### 5.1 Workshop logic and deliverables

- Risk: the workshop tries to do too many decision types at once (service bundles, baseline rules, governance roles, MEL, website blueprint), leading to shallow outputs for each.
  - Mitigation: narrow to 1–2 journeys but pre-decide which baseline and governance questions are in scope and which are explicitly deferred.
- Risk: steering bodies perceive CRDB as overstepping if the decision package reads like a line agency owning NFCS.
  - Mitigation: lean heavily on WMO wording about oversight and coordinating institutions and include explicit language that recommendations are for steering endorsement, not unilateral adoption.
- Risk: the website blueprint is treated as the real outcome and the governance and baseline logic is sidelined.
  - Mitigation: frame the annex as a UIP element derived from the service and governance design and make sure synthesis explicitly links pages to service bundles, governance roles, and MEL entries.

### 5.2 Stakeholder mix and incentives

- Risk: invited organizations cluster around data providers and existing CRDB partners while NFCS steering, economic, and disaster actors are missing, making outputs less adoptable by NFCS.
  - Mitigation: enforce a minimum representation rule across functions and treat absence of any function as a blocker to calling the workshop NFCS-ready.
- Risk: private sector and local governments feel tokenised if they are heavily cited in evidence but lightly represented in the room.
  - Mitigation: provide at least one seat per key evidence-holding category (private sector chambers, local design labs, municipalities) and give them speaking roles in journey walk-throughs.
- Risk: sensitive data holders (MSDHS, NSO, DDPM) perceive the baseline and access discussions as threatening or premature.
  - Mitigation: use WMO MEL and governance language to emphasise accountability safeguards and allow opt-in pilots rather than immediate national mandates.

### 5.3 Deliverability of post-workshop outputs

- Risk: the project team cannot realistically transform raw workshop artifacts into a 2-page NFCS decision package and website blueprint annex on the expected timeline.
  - Mitigation: pre-build skeletons for the decision memo and annex and structure session outputs so they drop into those templates with minimal rework.
- Risk: baseline and governance decisions reached in the workshop cannot be maintained because there is no named owner with resources for the UIP and data system.
  - Mitigation: use the quote bank emphasis on resourced ownership and explicit UIP operation arrangements to ensure every endorsed element has a named institutional owner and agreed cadence.

These stress test points should be used as a checklist during final agenda and invite confirmation rather than as a separate discussion in the workshop itself.
