# The Asphalt Commons: Traffic Demand Management and the Politics of Pain

**Date:** February 19, 2026 
**Topic:** Urban Development and Mobility – Traffic Demand Management (TDM) & Congestion Pricing 
**Lecturer:** Apiwat

The air in the lecture hall hummed with the specific, low-grade anxiety that accompanies the dissection of a wicked problem. Today’s subject was Traffic Demand Management (TDM), a bureaucratic acronym that attempts to sanitize a visceral urban reality: the traffic jam. The session began not with the lecturer, but with the students—a litany of case studies delivered with the earnestness of the unjaded. They spoke of Singapore’s certificate of entitlement, London’s congestion charge, and the chaotic tapestry of Jakarta. They presented the "ideal subjects" for study, glossing over the messy political combat that birthed them.

Then, the pivot happened. Apiwat, the lecturer, took the floor. He didn't start with a commendation. He started with a critique of the atmosphere itself.

"My voice is a little off today," he admitted, a rare moment of vulnerability that quickly dissolved into Socratic interrogation. "But let’s start from the first question I asked you. It seems a little unbalanced."

He zeroed in on the terminology. The slides had flickered between "Traffic," "Travel," and "Transportation." To the layperson, they are synonyms. To the urban strategist, they are distinct battlegrounds. "Make up your mind," Apiwat challenged. "What should the 'T' be? Nobody says 'Traffic' anymore... but when you say 'Traffic,' what comes to mind? Just the cars, right? You don't think about walking."

It was the first trap of the afternoon. By forcing the class to define the boundaries of the system, he was preparing them for the central thesis of the day: we are not managing metal boxes; we are managing human desire.

### [Expanded Analysis: The Semiotics of Mobility]

> _This section expands on the lecturer’s distinction between Traffic, Travel, and Transportation._
> 
> The lecturer’s insistence on distinguishing "Traffic" from "Travel" is not merely semantic pedantry; it is a fundamental shift in the ontology of urban planning. "Traffic" implies a hydraulic view of the city—cars are fluid in a pipe, and the engineer’s job is to increase the diameter of the pipe (supply-side intervention) to prevent bursts. This mindset dominated the mid-20th century, leading to the Robert Moses era of highway expansion which, invariably, induced more demand.
> 
> By shifting to "Travel" or "Mobility," we center the human agent. We acknowledge that the movement is a choice, not just a physical phenomenon. This aligns with the "Paradox of Intensification" in urban theory: you cannot build your way out of congestion. As Lewis Mumford famously critiqued in the 1950s, "Building more roads to prevent congestion is like a fat man loosening his belt to prevent obesity." The "T" in TDM represents the shift from the belt-loosening approach (Traffic) to the diet and lifestyle approach (Travel/Demand). If we treat the problem as "Traffic," the solution is concrete. If we treat it as "Demand," the solution is psychology and economics.

## The Theology of Derived Demand

"Do we need to reduce the demand? Yes or no?" Apiwat asked, scanning the room. The instinctual answer for an urbanist is 'yes'—less traffic is better. But Apiwat played the devil’s advocate, the voice of the greedy capitalist.

"I mean, the more people travel, isn't that better for the economy? Why do you want to reduce demand? Are you saying people should just stay at home?"

He steered the class toward the economic concept of **Derived Demand**. This is the foundational dogma of transport economics: travel is almost never the end product. It is a cost we pay to access what we actually want.

"I demand this water now because my throat is a little dry," Apiwat said, holding up a bottle. "This is direct. But usually, you don't travel because you love to travel. Unless you drive for the sake of fun."

He offered a personal confession to illustrate the point. "Like tomorrow. Originally, I was supposed to go to Geneva for two days. There was a meeting at the UN for a day and a half." He paused, the glamour of international diplomacy hanging in the air, before puncturing it. "I’m not going. I’m not telling you I don't enjoy travel anymore. Maybe 25 years ago, I said, 'Oh wow, free ticket, you better enjoy it.' That’s different. But now? I don't enjoy being on the plane."

The lesson was clear: If travel is a derived demand—a friction to be overcome—then the goal of TDM isn't to stop the activity at the destination (the meeting, the date, the job), but to manipulate the friction itself.

### [Expanded Analysis: The Friction of Distance and Agglomeration]

> _This section expands on the economic implications of reducing travel._
> 
> The lecturer touches on a critical tension in urban economics: the trade-off between **friction of distance** and **agglomeration economies**. Cities exist to minimize the friction of distance—to put labor, capital, and goods in close proximity. However, the success of a city increases that friction through congestion.
> 
> When the lecturer asks, "Isn't that better for the economy?", he is referencing the correlation between GDP and Vehicle Miles Traveled (VMT). Historically, these two lines moved in lockstep. Economic growth meant more movement. TDM attempts to "decouple" these lines—to sustain economic exchange without the physical movement of heavy assets (cars).
> 
> However, from a sustainability perspective, we must play the devil's advocate against the "derived demand" simplification. While commuting is derived, "cruising" or recreational travel is not. Furthermore, the modern "experience economy" turns travel into the product itself. If we treat all travel as a "cost" to be minimized, we risk designing sterile cities where movement is efficient but joyless. A purely utilitarian view of TDM risks creating a "teleportation city" model, ignoring the social value of the journey itself—the chance encounters on the sidewalk or the subway that constitute the social fabric of urban life.

## The Time-Space Shuffle

If we accept that we cannot simply crush demand (because that crushes the economy), we must rearrange it. Apiwat framed TDM as a game of Tetris played with time and space.

"You shift the demand... from where to where? And from when to when?" he asked. "Supply is generally very rigid. Therefore, the 'bottleneck' is pretty much a space and time limitation."

The class offered solutions: Multiple routes. Google Maps. But the lecturer was skeptical of the algorithmic savior. "My friend Joey, a grad student, is researching how Google tells people to go certain ways, but people choose different ways. I rarely drive, but when I drive, I’d rather go slow but continuously. Google tends to minimize travel time, even if you get stuck on the highway."

Then came the discussion of **Flexible Hours**. It sounds like a panacea—let people work when they want, and the peak hour curve flattens. But Apiwat, ever the pragmatist, demanded the "how."

"You are an urban strategist now for Bangkok," he announced. "Hypothetically, if I run for Bangkok Governor in the next few months—you never know—and you need to recommend flexible hours. How would you implement it?"

The students suggested laws. Apiwat scoffed. "The law usually takes about five years. I’m not King. Even the King couldn't... maybe." He stopped himself, grinning. "Governor cannot do anything. Honestly, Governor cannot do anything. That’s why I don’t want to become one."

The class eventually landed on a strategy of leverage: Start with the people you own. "I can tell the BMA (Bangkok Metropolitan Administration) officers when to come. I have the authority," Apiwat noted. From there, you use subsidies, corporate tax incentives, and dynamic pricing on the BTS (Skytrain) to nudge the private sector.

### [Expanded Analysis: The Downs-Thomson Paradox]

> _This section expands on the limitations of shifting demand._
> 
> The class discussion on shifting routes and times invariably runs into the **Downs-Thomson Paradox** (also known as the Pigou-Knight-Downs paradox). This theory posits that the equilibrium speed of car traffic on a road network is determined by the average door-to-door speed of equivalent journeys by public transport.
> 
> If the lecturer successfully implements flexible hours or widens a road (shifting space), the "Triple Convergence" occurs:
> 
> 1. **Spatial Convergence:** Drivers who used alternative routes switch to the improved one.
>     
> 2. **Temporal Convergence:** Drivers who traveled off-peak switch to the peak hour because it’s "better" now.
>     
> 3. **Modal Convergence:** People who took the bus switch to driving.
>     
> 
> This creates a zero-sum game. The lecturer’s skepticism about "Google Maps" efficiency hints at this—individual optimization often leads to collective inefficiency (Braess's Paradox), where adding a new route actually slows down the overall network. The only true way to escape this loop is not just rearranging the furniture (time/space shifting) but changing the cost of entry (pricing).

## The Work-From-Home Trap and the "Starbucks Run"

The conversation drifted to the modern holy grail of TDM: Remote Work. Surely, if we work from home, the traffic evaporates?

Apiwat leaned back, ready to dismantle this assumption with the "Rebound Effect."

"Do we consume less energy in total? Surprisingly, no," he said. "Offices are more efficient. At home, you run the AC. We aren't saving energy cost; we are just shifting it."

But the behavioral rebound is even more fascinating. He recalled a survey from the United States, conducted a decade ago—long before Zoom fatigue was a global condition. "Twenty million people already worked from home at least once a week. It turns out that the long trips reduced... but they do short trips. They go to Starbucks. They do a little bit of shopping during lunch. They go pick up their kids."

He painted a picture of the human inability to sit still. "It’s really interesting. People feel like they have time now. They don’t say, 'Oh, I have time, I’ll sit at home and read a book.' No, they add more activities."

And in the modern Bangkok context, this derived demand has mutated. "You guys order delivery," Apiwat accused playfully. "So you end up having derived demand, but not yours. Other people are traveling for you."

### [Expanded Analysis: Jevons Paradox in the Digital Age]

> _This section expands on the rebound effect of remote work._
> 
> The phenomenon Apiwat describes is a classic manifestation of the **Jevons Paradox**. William Stanley Jevons observed in the 19th century that as steam engines became more efficient with coal, coal consumption didn't drop—it skyrocketed because coal became a more viable power source for everything.
> 
> In mobility terms, "time" is the coal. Work-from-home policies make the "expenditure" of time more efficient (zero commute). Instead of "saving" that time, we spend it on other forms of mobility—the "Starbucks run" or the school pickup. Furthermore, the rise of the "Instant Delivery Economy" (Grab, Foodpanda, Uber Eats) has fragmented what used to be consolidated trips. Instead of one person driving to the supermarket to buy a week’s worth of food, we now have five separate motorcycle trips delivering five separate meals. The _Vehicle Kilometers Traveled_ (VKT) might actually increase per capita, even if the primary commuter stays in their pajamas. The congestion profile changes from a tidal wave (morning/evening commute) to a constant, chaotic hum of logistics.

## The Entrepreneurial Kids of Jakarta

When discussing Shared Mobility, the class touched on High-Occupancy Vehicle (HOV) lanes. It’s a standard Western solution: force people to carpool. But in Southeast Asia, every regulation is merely a market opportunity waiting to be exploited.

Apiwat’s eyes lit up as he recounted a trip to Indonesia. "Have you seen the 'Jockey'?"

He wasn't talking about horse racing. "In Jakarta, or maybe Bandung... basically, there was that kid. It’s not just one; they’re lining out just before the HOV lane."

The scheme is simple and devastatingly human. Drivers, alone in their cars, need passengers to legally enter the fast lane. Local children, spotting the gap between policy and reality, offer themselves as instant passengers for a fee.

"A driver would pick up this kid so they were together in the HOV lane," Apiwat explained, still sounding impressed by the audacity of it. "My question was, how did they get back? Because there’s HOV on both sides!"

The answer? "They go there, they cut off... well, the dangerous part is that they have to cross the highway. It was always a safety issue. It’s a risky business. But I was like, wow, entrepreneurial."

It was a stark reminder: you can design the perfect policy in a simulation, but on the streets of a developing megacity, poverty and ingenuity will always hack the system.

## The Moral Economy of the Road

The lecture moved to the most contentious tool in the TDM arsenal: **Congestion Pricing**. Economists love it. Politicians fear it. The public hates it.

"Why are people generally opposed to it?" Apiwat asked.

"It’s their right to be on the road," a student ventured.

"Almost a human right," Apiwat nodded. "I’m born with the free access to the road. Roads are supposed to be free."

He then launched into a crash course on public economics to dismantle this "right." He drew imaginary quadrants in the air: **Exclusivity** and **Rivalry**.

"If I drink this water, nobody else can drink it. That is a private good. The air in this room? I inhale, you inhale, there is still air. That is a public good."

The trap is that we think roads are air, but they are actually water. "Roads are not public goods at all," he declared. "They are **rival**. One car going onto the road means less space for the next car. But we treat them as public goods."

Congestion pricing, he argued, is simply correcting a categorization error. It turns the road into a "Club Good" or a "Toll Good." You pay the entry fee to the club.

"Theoretically speaking, no debate," Apiwat said. "Neoclassical economics? Anybody would agree congestion pricing is good. Politically? Super difficult."

### [Expanded Analysis: The Tragedy of the Asphalt Commons]

> _This section expands on the economic classification of roads._
> 
> The lecturer is applying Garrett Hardin’s "Tragedy of the Commons" to urban space. When a resource is rivalrous but non-excludable (like a pasture, or a road), individual rationality leads to collective ruin. Every driver gains +1 utility from entering the road, while the cost (delay) is distributed among all drivers (-0.0001 utility per person). Since the individual gain outweighs the individual share of the cost, everyone drives, and the road collapses into gridlock.
> 
> However, playing the devil's advocate against the "Club Good" transformation: turning public space into a commodity raises profound equity issues. If the road becomes a club, who gets blackballed? The "neoclassical" view assumes money is a neutral way to ration scarcity. But in a city with vast inequality, rationing by price is effectively rationing by class. This leads to the concept of **"Transport Justice."** Is the freedom of movement a civil right or a market commodity? If we price the road, we must simultaneously provide a "public option" (high-quality transit) that is non-rivalrous and non-excludable, otherwise, we are simply constructing an urban apartheid.

## The Equity Trap and the Tesla Truck

"Unfair for poor people," a student argued, explaining the resistance.

Apiwat accepted this but pushed deeper. It’s not just about income levels; it’s about the visible symbols of inequality. He brought up the UK PACT program currently trying to implement congestion pricing in Bangkok. The challenge isn't the technology—"We can monitor your cell phone for God's sake"—it’s the optical politics of who gets charged.

"Look at the roads. What do you see?"

"Tuk-tuks. Motorcycles. Pickup trucks."

"Technically, not difficult at all to charge them," Apiwat said. "But _would_ you charge them? Oh my God."

He presented a conundrum: How do you calculate the toll? By size? By pollution? By value?

"You could drive that hideous Tesla Truck," Apiwat said, shuddering slightly. "It’s so ugly, but it’s so interesting. I saw it so many times in Boston. You’re so ugly that you’re interesting."

The Tesla Truck is massive, takes up huge space, but emits zero local pollution. A beat-up motorcycle takes up zero space but spews fumes.

"Do you charge the Tesla more because of the space? Or the motorcycle because of the pollution? Or because of the energy?" he asked. "If you are charging according to pollution, the motorcycle pays more than the BYD car. Is that acceptable to you?"

The class fell into a debate about safety vs. pollution vs. annoyance. One student suggested charging motorcycles _less_ because they are lighter. Another suggested charging them _more_ because they are "annoying."

Apiwat laughed. "We want to charge them for being so annoying. I’m not saying you’re wrong." But he warned against "coupling" too many objectives. "Usually, safety is not part of congestion pricing rationale. You cannot use safety... that’s a different story."

If we start charging based on annoyance or safety, we risk perverse incentives. "Would that incentivize people to ride more motorcycles and get crashed more?" he mused. "It’s a fascinating brain exercise."

## The "Pancake" of Sukhumvit 39

So, where do you start? If you are the Governor, where do you draw the line for the congestion zone?

"Go for the biggest fish," a student suggested.

Apiwat countered with a story of failure—a cautionary tale involving a former student of his working for the Governor's team. They tried a modest experiment on **Sukhumvit 39**, a notorious shortcut alley.

"There’s no place for a pedestrian. So the student decided to do this experiment... widening the space for pedestrians." It was a noble, textbook urbanist move. "Most useful place for the pedestrians, because the Japanese expats and people there, they walk a lot."

They turned the two-way flow into a one-way system to reclaim space.

"The Governor had to go and remove the experiment overnight," Apiwat said, his voice dropping. "It created a super crazy uproar. People were complaining on all social media platforms just for that day. It was like... like the earthquake. That’s a pancake."

He recalled a dinner with a diplomat that same week. "I went to have dinner with somebody from the U.S. Embassy. He was complaining from the beginning until the very end of the dinner. And he was not even driving! I was like, okay, interesting."

The lesson? The rich and the middle class have voices that carry. "Middle-class politics," Apiwat noted. "Middle class tend to have stronger and bigger voices. Even if you assume people have alternatives—'Take the BTS!'—they say, 'No, my home is too far. It’s hot.' You charge me using the main road? I complain. Your experiment fails."

### [Expanded Analysis: The Political Economy of "The Shortcut"]

> _This section expands on the Sukhumvit 39 case study._
> 
> The failure of the Sukhumvit 39 experiment illustrates the fragility of **Tactical Urbanism** in a high-inequality context. Tactical urbanism advocates for "lighter, quicker, cheaper" interventions—using paint and bollards to test changes before making them permanent.
> 
> However, in Bangkok, the "Soi" (alley) system functions as a secondary arterial network for private vehicles to bypass the gridlocked main roads. By pedestrianizing a Soi, the planners were not just beautifying a street; they were severing a critical valve in the city’s hydraulic pressure system.
> 
> The "Uproar" represents the clash between the **Exchange Value** of the street (a place to be, to walk, to shop) and the **Use Value** of the street for the elite (a conduit for movement). When the lecturer mentions the "US Embassy guest" complaining, it highlights that the resistance to TDM is often strongest not among the poor (who already take transit), but among the globalized elite who view seamless private mobility as a baseline expectation of their status.

## The Parking Litmus Test

As the lecture wound down, Apiwat offered a final, cynical litmus test for any city claiming to be "good."

"My criteria of a good city? They charge for parking," he stated flatly. "Think of any good city that doesn't charge parking. Find one for me."

He waited. No one could.

"My argument—and this is my personal argument, I cannot say this publicly—is that we are trying to run before we even walk. We are not angry enough."

He pointed out the hypocrisy even within their own institution. "We are trying to do a low-carbon campus here at Chula. Unsuccessfully." Why? Because staff get free parking. "There are a few staff people who own cars, bring them here, and while they are here, they use motorcycles to go everywhere. It’s interesting logic."

He predicted that true congestion pricing in Bangkok is 20 years away. The resistance is too deep, the "right to the road" too entrenched.

"We always need other solutions. Parking. Subsidies. You cannot just internalize the costs and expect people to be rational. People are smart. They find a second license plate. They hire a kid to be on the car."

## The Future is a Historic Town

He ended with a glimmer of hope, not in the metropolis, but in the old towns. **Chiang Mai** is bidding for UNESCO World Heritage status, and part of that requires managing the vibration of cars shaking the ancient brick pagodas.

"The debate is whether to ban cars completely... or restriction. It’s Road Pricing. The rationale is not congestion per se, but the negative effects on historic areas."

And closer to home, he pointed to **Siam Square**. "You know, the pedestrianized areas that you guys see now? That used to be the road people would drive from Phayathai Road to Henri Dunant. All through traffic."

It changed. It became nicer. It became a place.

"So at least now, it's more difficult to drive there, right?" Apiwat smiled, packing up his notes. "So it's much nicer."

The lecture ended not with a solution, but with a philosophy: The city improves only when we make the private car uncomfortable. It is a war of attrition, fought one parking spot, one toll gate, and one "hideous" Tesla Truck at a time.
