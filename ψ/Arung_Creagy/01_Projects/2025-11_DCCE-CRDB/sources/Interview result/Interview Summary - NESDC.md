### 1. Role and Responsibility of the Agency

- **Key duty and roles:** NESDC is a primary data _user_ and policy planner, not a field implementer. Their main roles include drafting National Economic and Social Development Plans, evaluating national progress, conducting macroeconomic forecasting (GDP impacts), and approving large-scale national infrastructure projects and government loans.
    
- **Associated legal frameworks:** They operate under the National Strategy (Master Plan 18 on sustainable growth) and are currently tracking the 13th National Plan (specifically Milestone 11 regarding disaster resilience) while actively drafting the 14th National Plan. They are also the national host for tracking SDG goals.
    

### 2. Highlighted Key Projects

- **Loss & Damage (L&D) Assessment Project:**
    
    - _Status & Scope:_ Active (Ending June 2026). NESDC hired Chulalongkorn University (Ajarn Pongsak) to create a true economic Loss and Damage framework mapped to the Sendai Framework. The first phase focuses heavily on direct losses in the agricultural sector.
        
    - _Exclusions/Gaps:_ They are currently excluding indirect losses (e.g., opportunity costs, supply chain disruptions) because the data is too difficult to find. They plan to host a public seminar on their findings in May 2026.
        
- **SDG Voluntary National Review (VNR) 2025:**
    
    - _Status & Scope:_ Active. NESDC's International Cooperation Division is compiling the 10-year progress report on all SDGs to meet the 2030 agenda, cleaning data from various ministries to establish a single baseline (e.g., standardizing road fatality metrics between the Ministry of Interior and Ministry of Public Health).
        
- **Infrastructure Climate Risk Evaluation:**
    
    - _Status & Scope:_ Active routine. When evaluating massive infrastructure loans (e.g., the recent Chao Phraya water project), NESDC applies a standard 7% discount rate to account for climate change risks, disease outbreaks, and future uncertainties to ensure the investment is economically viable.
        

### 3. Current Workflow & Data Usage

- **Context, Hazard Scope, and Data Sources:** They look at climate impacts from a macroeconomic lens. To forecast the economic impact of a drought, they pull normal baseline data from the Ministry of Agriculture (crop yields, water usage) and the Ministry of Natural Resources (reservoir levels).
    
- **Modeling Techniques and Granularity:** The Macroeconomic Division uses historical data provided by the Accounting Division to forecast quarterly and annual GDP. Currently, they prefer data at the district (อำเภอ) level but acknowledge that having data down to the sub-district or community level for key economic hubs (like major tourist zones) would be ideal.
    
- **Inter-agency Collaborations & Cascading Impacts:** They coordinate across all ministries but note a major bottleneck in disaster data. They currently rely on the Department of Disaster Prevention and Mitigation (DDPM) for disaster statistics.
    

### 4. Climate Data Needs & Gaps

- **The "True Loss" vs. "Relief Fund" Gap:** This is their biggest pain point. NESDC stressed that DDPM data only shows _government compensation payouts_ (e.g., 9,000 THB per household). It completely fails to capture the true economic damage, lost business opportunity days, or logistics breakdowns (like the recent Hat Yai floods). They desperately need a standardized system to calculate real economic Loss and Damage.
    
- **Projections and Baselines:** The macroeconomic team lacks predictive climate risk data. When forecasting economic growth, they often have to blindly "assume" the climate impact because they do not have reliable forward-looking risk models to plug into their economic variables.
    
- **Budgeting Roadblocks (Or Lack Thereof):** Interestingly, NESDC noted that _there are no central budgeting roadblocks_ for climate adaptation. The National Strategy (Master Plan 18) explicitly mandates climate-resilient growth, and the Bureau of the Budget is already aligned with this. If an agency struggles to get budget, it is likely because they cannot properly quantify the risk or ROI, not because the central policy blocks it.
    
- **Specific Requests for DCCE:** DCCE needs to provide standardized indicators, baselines, and target metrics so NESDC can actually track and evaluate the success of Milestone 11 (societal immunity to climate change) under the 13th National Plan.
    

### 5. Next Steps & CRDB Integration

- **Map to Implementation Plan:** NESDC's urgent need to differentiate between "relief funds" and "true economic loss" perfectly validates WP4's "Minimum Viable Dataset for Loss & Damage Reporting." Their ongoing study with Chula should be directly integrated into CRDB's Logical Data Model for Loss & Damage.
    
- **Seed Use Cases for WP3 Conceptual Data Model (CDM):** * _Scenario:_ "Calculating the true macroeconomic loss (direct asset damage + business interruption/opportunity cost) of a major flood in a key economic zone to adjust quarterly national GDP forecasts and justify future infrastructure loan approvals."
    
- **Planned Participation:** NESDC confirmed they want to participate in the April 2026 Public Hearing/Workshop, though they noted they are currently very busy with field visits for the 14th National Plan. They will also invite the CRDB team to their Loss & Damage seminar in May 2026.
