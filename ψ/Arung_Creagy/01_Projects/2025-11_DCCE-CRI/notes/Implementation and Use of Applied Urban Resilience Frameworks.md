### **(a) Extraction: Implementation and Use of Applied Urban Resilience Frameworks**

**1. Urban Climate Resilience Framework (ACCCRN)**

- **Chronological Context:** 2012. Developed to operationalize resilience for local planning practitioners, moving away from standard "predict and prevent" climate impact approaches toward dynamic socio-ecological systems.
- **Data collection methods:** Participatory processes utilizing "Shared Learning Dialogues (SLDs)", which are "structured interaction processes designed to bring together divergent communities, sources of knowledge and perspectives" alongside external technical or scientific experts (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Data analysis methods:** Qualitative mapping and deliberation to identify intersections of "fragile systems, low-capacity agents, constraining institutions and their exposure to climate hazards" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Main outputs:** City-specific "Vulnerability assessments" and "Resilience strategies that defined tangible priority interventions" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Documented use of results:** City governments used the results to prioritize interventions tied to existing planning processes, funding specific actions such as "Flood monitoring and early warning systems" in infrastructure, "Mangrove restoration" in ecosystems, and "Disease surveillance" for health institutions (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** This framework demonstrates that engaging vulnerable populations directly through workshops generates highly localized, actionable investment priorities that purely quantitative models might miss.

**2. Integrated Methodological Framework (IDIS)**

- **Chronological Context:** 2022. Reacts against standard, reductionist Decision Support Systems (DSS) that combine metrics into mixed indexes, opting instead for a "post-normal" science approach using thresholds.
- **Data collection methods:** Combines "Qualitative Information" and "Data series" with the explicit "participation of involved actors, particularly in the first stage" to define objectives (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Data analysis methods:** Dynamic simulation models tracking interacting factors without combining them. Applies the strict rule "Threshold out, Measure out," where "any policy exceeding a threshold should be rejected or assigned the lowest priority" (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Main outputs:** Dynamic model outputs, vulnerability assessments, and scenario/policy analysis mapping "positive and negative effects of each policy or scenario on the different environmental, economic and social factors" (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Documented use of results:** In Fuerteventura, the framework was used to evaluate the water-energy nexus, allowing policymakers to discover that "planned policy measures... are far from achieving the proposed objectives," leading to a reorientation of actions to improve resilience (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Identifying explicit resilience thresholds (points of failure) provides a rigid quantitative mechanism for discarding unsustainable municipal policies during the curation of intervention strategies.

**3. European Resilience Management Guidelines (RESOLUTE CRAMSS)**

- **Chronological Context:** 2022. Shifts disaster management away from centralized, static procedural approaches toward real-time "resilience engineering" for Urban Transport Systems (UTS).
- **Data collection methods:** Continuous streaming of "Big Data from heterogeneous data sources/streams and sensors as the GPS positions of people... User Generated Data... Open Data, data from environmental and other kind of sensors" and social networks (Bellini et al., 2022).
- **Data analysis methods:** Real-time data mining, "semantic reasoning", and predictive simulation via the Collaborative Resilience Assessment and Management Support System (CRAMSS), operating as a "Multiple Input – Multiple Output (MIMO) system" (Bellini et al., 2022).
- **Main outputs:** Early warning tools, decision support dashboards, mobile emergency apps, game-based training apps, and a "4R communication strategy (right person, at the right time, in the right place with the right message)" (Bellini et al., 2022).
- **Documented use of results:** Operationalized in Florence and the Athens Metro to "support decision makers in taking better decisions" by generating real-time alarms, adjusting citizen evacuation flows, and optimizing the scarcity of first-responder resources (Bellini et al., 2022).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Incorporating big data and real-time simulation requires curating data infrastructure requirements (IoT, data privacy compliance) before a city can adopt advanced technical resilience frameworks.

**4. Climate Resilience Index (ESI)**

- **Chronological Context:** 2024. Builds on previous tools like the Notre Dame Urban Resilience Index to assess preparedness of US cities against environmental, economic, infrastructural, and social challenges.
- **Data collection methods:** Aggregation of "primary and secondary data from various sources, including federal, county, and city-level agencies, bureaus, institutions, and financial reports" (ESI, 2024).
- **Data analysis methods:** Variables are standardized (e.g., "standardized using city population to reduce bias"), scored individually against a "national average/average of major MSAs as a reference point," and assigned weights (e.g., Environment 40%, Economic 20%) to create a standardized uniform scale (ESI, 2024).
- **Main outputs:** Standardized scores, an overall ranking (1-9), and scorecards identifying specific weaknesses per city.
- **Documented use of results:** Used as a "diagnostic framework" and launchpad for benchmarking. Related case studies show governments utilizing equivalent data models to inform decisions: Baltimore used a "cost-benefit tool to aid decision-makers in choosing smart surfaces," while Memphis created a Regional Resilience Master Plan to tailor "suitable strategies for development, restoration, and conservation" based on risk mapping (ESI, 2024).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Standardization methods (like per capita normalization) are essential steps to extract valid comparative insights from municipal administrative data.

**5. Global Cities Resilience Index (GCRI)**

- **Chronological Context:** 2025. Created by Kearney and FII to react against indices that measure "past achievements" or generic ESG, focusing instead on "forward-looking readiness" and systemic capability.
- **Data collection methods:** Focuses entirely on "quantifiable, verifiable data from established sources rather than survey responses, expert opinions... or subjective evaluations" (Kearney, 2025).
- **Data analysis methods:** Indicator scores are normalized, then multiplied by a weighting of "Foundational importance" plus "Data richness" (to account for data quality), and finally aggregated by dimension weights (Kearney, 2025).
- **Main outputs:** A composite index score (0-100), overall ranking of 31 global cities, and visual "resilience profiles" (e.g., radar charts mapping performance across 5 dimensions) (Kearney, 2025).
- **Documented use of results:** Designed as an actionable tool "for identifying systemic gaps and prioritizing investments in adaptive capacity" by revealing areas for targeted interventions. For instance, revealing that London excels globally but has a vulnerability in economic collaboration, or guiding investors toward "urban ecosystems positioned for sustainable growth" (Kearney, 2025). _(Note: As an inaugural 2025 index, historical documented use by city governments is prospective)._
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Weighting indicators by "data richness" is a critical methodological technique for evaluating cities in the Global South where administrative records may be incomplete or unreliable.

**6. ICARIA Holistic Resilience Assessment Framework (RAF)**

- **Chronological Context:** 2026. Upgrades the RESCCUE framework by expanding beyond city boundaries into regional areas and incorporating nature-based solutions and compound climate events.
- **Data collection methods:** Combines administrative "models, plans, monitoring activities, risk assessment, or city incident records" gathered through collaborative "working sessions" with regional stakeholders (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Data analysis methods:** Objective-driven analysis where metrics are evaluated against reference thresholds to assign a development level of "incipient (graded 0 to 1), progressing (1 to 2), or advanced (2 to 3)" (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Main outputs:** An open-access web platform providing dynamic visualizations (donut and bar charts), summary reports, and data feeds that export directly into a Decision Support System (DSS) (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Documented use of results:** The Barcelona Metropolitan Area (AMB) used the outputs to transition from an essential to a comprehensive assessment, identifying "notable differences across AMB’s municipalities, indicating where targeted measures could address ecosystem service gaps" and informing a briefing to prioritize critical infrastructure actions (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Translating complex metrics into simple categorical development levels (incipient/progressing/advanced) via web dashboards is a proven method for facilitating cross-departmental communication and stakeholder engagement.


### **(b) Synthesis: Implementation and Use of Urban Resilience Frameworks**

**1. How Frameworks Are Implemented (Data Collection & Analysis)** The implementation of urban resilience frameworks operates on a spectrum ranging from deeply participatory, qualitative local processes to highly automated, quantitative big data models.

- **Data Collection Methods:** Earlier and community-focused frameworks (like ACCCRN and IDIS) rely heavily on participatory workshops, stakeholder dialogues, and expert knowledge-sharing to define localized vulnerabilities. Conversely, modern comparative indices (ESI, GCRI, Strategy&) explicitly exclude subjective surveys, relying instead on secondary administrative data, national statistics, and international databases (World Bank, UN). At the most technologically advanced end, operational frameworks like RESOLUTE bypass administrative data entirely, harvesting real-time remote sensing, IoT sensor streams, GPS data, and social media sentiment.
- **Data Analysis Methods:** The analytical methods reflect the data inputs. Qualitative frameworks synthesize narratives and map structural relationships. Quantitative indices use rigorous mathematical normalization (adjusting for population size), minimum/maximum scaling, and weighted aggregation (often incorporating weightings for "data richness" or "foundational importance" as seen in the GCRI). High-end operational tools (RESOLUTE) employ machine learning, semantic reasoning, and dynamic simulation. A notable hybrid approach is IDIS, which uses dynamic modelling but applies a strict "threshold" rule, rejecting any policy option that breaches a defined resilience limit rather than burying it inside a weighted average.

**2. How City Governments Actually Use the Results** The outputs of these frameworks—ranging from composite scores and web dashboards to real-time mobile alerts and localized vulnerability maps—are actively used by city governments to transition from reactive disaster management to proactive urban planning.

- **Informing Strategic Plans and Prioritizing Interventions:** Framework results are consistently used to allocate budgets and design infrastructure. For example, ACCCRN assessments led directly to investments in flood monitoring and mangrove restoration in Asian cities. The ICARIA tool allowed the Barcelona Metropolitan Area to pinpoint specific ecosystem service gaps across its various municipalities to target regional measures. Similarly, the ESI highlighted how Memphis used localized risk assessments to tailor development and conservation strategies across high-risk zones.
- **Monitoring Progress:** Frameworks are increasingly used to track performance over time. Tools like the GCRI and ICARIA are explicitly designed for dynamic, ongoing evaluation rather than static snapshots. ICARIA's web dashboards map "incipient, progressing, or advanced" states, allowing city managers to visually track whether targeted resilience policies are improving baseline vulnerabilities. IDIS simulation results in Fuerteventura actively revealed that current policies were failing to meet long-term water-energy goals, forcing a re-evaluation of the city's strategy.
- **Engaging Stakeholders:** Framework implementation itself is frequently used as a governance tool to break down municipal silos. The "Shared Learning Dialogues" in ACCCRN and the working sessions in ICARIA forced disconnected government departments, civil society, and scientists to align their data and priorities. Furthermore, platforms like RESOLUTE output direct communications to the public, generating real-time, context-aware early warnings ("4R communication") to actively engage citizens in disaster response.

###  (a) Extraction: Approaches to Analysis and Communication useful for local decision‑making

**1. Rejecting Mixed Composite Indices for Strict Thresholds (IDIS)**

- **What changed & Why:** In 2022, researchers reacted against conventional Decision Support Systems (DSS) that combine diverse criteria into weighted mixed indices, arguing these often mask fatal flaws in specific policy areas. The Integrated Methodological Framework (IDIS) shifted to dynamic simulation and strict limits (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Direct quote:** "The approach proposed avoids the use of indexes mixing non-reducible dimensions and, instead, keeps track of the positive and negative effects of each policy or scenario... The establishment of threshold values for each indicator provides a way to identify and assigned the lowest priority – even reject – those policies that would exceed the concerned resilience thresholds" (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Curators of future dashboards should embed strict pass/fail threshold alerts rather than relying solely on weighted averages that might obscure critical infrastructural or social vulnerabilities.

**2. Multi-Dimensional Visualizations over Single Global Indicators (ICARIA RAF)**

- **What changed & Why:** By 2026, frameworks like ICARIA explicitly moved away from reducing a city's resilience to a single numerical score, opting instead for categorical development levels to foster nuanced decision-making and cross-departmental dialogue (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Direct quote:** "The ICARIA resilience assessment method is not intended to produce a single global resilience indicator. Instead, it supports multidimensional decision-making... For each study, it reports the percentage of metrics achieved at each development level... incipient (graded 0 to 1), progressing (1 to 2), or advanced (2 to 3)" (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Designing communication tools (like dashboards) using categorical color-coding (incipient vs. advanced) across distinct dimensions facilitates clearer policy targeting than a single aggregated benchmark score.

**3. Shifting from Fixed Rankings to Dynamic Diagnostic Tracking (GCRI)**

- **What changed & Why:** In 2025, the Global Cities Resilience Index (GCRI) was developed to react against existing Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) indices that merely documented past achievements as static snapshots, prioritizing instead continuous capacity building for future uncertainties (Kearney, 2025).
- **Direct quote:** "Unlike traditional indices that are primarily used for benchmarking purposes, the GCRI is designed as an actionable tool... The framework explicitly avoids creating fixed rankings that will become outdated. Instead, indicators are designed to be regularly updated to track development over time" (Kearney, 2025).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** To support real capacity-building, any proposed resilience timeline or metric tracker must be engineered for continuous, iterative data inputs rather than one-off "league table" assessments.

**4. Shared Learning Dialogues (ACCCRN)**

- **What changed & Why:** Introduced around 2012, this approach moved away from top-down "predict and prevent" models towards iterative, participatory communication designed to build trust and shared language among disconnected local actors (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Direct quote:** Applied in 10 Asian cities, planners used "Shared Learning Dialogues (SLDs)... structured interaction processes designed to bring together divergent communities, sources of knowledge and perspectives in a manner that builds common understanding and trust while enabling responses to different interests" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Framework implementation protocols must include qualitative, facilitated workshops (like SLDs) to translate abstract resilience data into locally owned, tangible intervention strategies.

**5. Collaborative Storytelling and Local Narratives**

- **What changed & Why:** Around 2015, urban sociologists reacted against highly technical, agency-driven planning meta-narratives by emphasizing "co-construction" of local narratives. This allowed communities to articulate their own tipping points and systemic needs (Beilin & Wilkinson, 2015).
- **Direct quote:** "...resilience is about creating urban narratives that assist in changing structure and function at ecological and built design levels... Collaborative storytelling establishes what to make resilient, identifies desired outcomes and obstacles to achieving these preferences" (Beilin & Wilkinson, 2015).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Qualitative narrative extraction must be formally integrated alongside quantitative data regimes to ensure that the curated frameworks capture the lived realities and social justice priorities of the community.

**6. The "Powerful Gradient" Approach for Gap Analysis**
- **What changed & Why:** In 2022, planners applied the Plus Planning Cycle in Mexico City, utilizing narrative "descriptors" to clearly communicate the gap between the current reality and an idealized future, thereby avoiding confusing technical jargon (Mendieta-Márquez et al., 2022).
- **Direct quote:** "From a methodological point of view, this study shows the relevance that for any intervention project has the use of a ‘powerful gradient’ between the description of the current problematic situation (diagnosis) and the objective image or idealized (prospective) situation that is designed in order to build an action guide" (Mendieta-Márquez et al., 2022).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Structuring assessment outcomes as a comparative gradient (current state vs. idealized goal) provides a highly practical communication tool for local politicians to justify specific urban investments.

### (b) Synthesis

**1. Which approaches to analysis and communication seem to be most useful for local decision‑making?** The most useful approaches for local decision-making prioritize _diagnostic gap analysis_, _scenario visualization_, and _participatory knowledge integration_ over simple mathematical aggregation.

1. **Categorical Dashboards:** Tools like the ICARIA web platform visually communicate the _development level_ of various urban services (e.g., "incipient" vs. "advanced") rather than providing a single, opaque score (Brito et al., 2026). This allows specific municipal departments to see exactly where they stand and what immediate levers they need to pull.
2. **Threshold Policies:** Analytical approaches like the IDIS framework strictly enforce the rule of "Threshold out, Measure out" (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022). By communicating hard limits (e.g., maximum acceptable water depletion) instead of using weighted averages that hide fatal flaws, decision-makers are provided with clear, non-negotiable boundaries for urban planning.
3. **Participatory Narratives and Dialogues:** Communication methods such as "Shared Learning Dialogues" (Tyler & Moench, 2012) and "Collaborative Storytelling" (Beilin & Wilkinson, 2015) are proven to be highly effective. They translate scientific climate data into local, culturally relevant narratives, breaking down institutional silos and generating the political buy-in necessary to actually fund interventions.

**2. What design choices help avoid the “ranking trap” and instead support capacity‑building and learning?** The "ranking trap" occurs when frameworks reduce cities to competitive "league tables" (static snapshots of past achievements) which can penalize vulnerable cities and fail to provide actionable guidance for improvement. To avoid this, modern frameworks employ several intentional design choices:

- **Refusing Single Global Indicators:** Frameworks explicitly avoid rolling diverse data (e.g., social equity and physical infrastructure) into one composite number, as this mixes "non-reducible dimensions" and obscures localized vulnerabilities (Brito et al., 2026; Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
- **Focusing on Dynamic Readiness over Static Outcomes:** The GCRI explicitly designs its metrics to measure "systemic readiness" for _future_ disruptions rather than past ESG performance. It is built to be "regularly updated to track development over time," transforming the assessment from a one-off ranking into a continuous capacity-building monitor (Kearney, 2025).
- **Establishing "Powerful Gradients":** By defining an "idealized prospective situation" and evaluating the city's current distance from that specific local goal, planners focus on actionable, tailored improvement pathways (the gradient) rather than how the city compares to external competitors (Mendieta-Márquez et al., 2022).
- **Co-Creation of Indicators:** Engaging local stakeholders to define the resilience objectives and select the indicators ensures the assessment measures what actually matters to the community, fostering local ownership and "co-responsibility" in the learning process (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
