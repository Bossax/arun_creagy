- **Classification:** Applied to real city/cities
- **City/Case Study:** 10 Asian cities (Surat, Indore, Gorakhpur, Semarang, Bandar Lampung, Hat Yai, Chiang Rai, Can Tho, Da Nang, Quy Nhon).

**2. Good Urban Governance for Climate Change Adaptation**

- **Classification:** Applied to real city/cities
- **City/Case Study:** 10 Asian cities (Bangkok, Chennai, Surat, Cochin, Chittagong, Da Nang, Ho Chi Minh City, Dalian, Hangzhou, Ningbo).
- **Exact Supporting Quote:** “The case studies were undertaken by research partners in the ten cities selected for investigation. Given time pressures, these rapid assessments balanced primary and secondary data with cross-referencing and review”.
- **Chronological Context:** 2009. Builds on Mehta’s 1998 governance framework to react against early adaptation programs that disproportionately focused on rural livelihoods rather than rapidly urbanizing areas.
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Establishes specific governance metrics (decentralization, transparency, responsiveness, inclusion, experience) needed to interpret the institutional dimensions of resilience frameworks.

**3. Strategy& Urban Resilience Assessment Framework**
- **Classification:** Applied to real city/cities
- **City/Case Study:** 9 MENA cities (Abu Dhabi, Amman, Cairo, Casablanca, Dubai, Jeddah, Kuwait City, Muscat, Riyadh) and 11 global comparators (Cape Town, Houston, London, Nairobi, São Paulo, Seoul, Singapore, Sydney, Tokyo, Toronto, Zurich).

- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Offers a comprehensive 131-KPI quantitative scorecard structure crucial for understanding modern data requirements for benchmarking city performance.

**4. Climate Resilience Index (ESI)**
- **Classification:** Applied to real city/cities
- **City/Case Study:** 9 US cities (Baltimore, Berkeley, Charleston, Flint, Memphis, Pittsburgh, St. Louis, Seattle, Tampa).
- **Exact Supporting Quote:** “A pilot study of nine U.S. cities put the index into effect with an aim for evaluation of additional cities in the future.”.
- **Chronological Context:** 2024. Incorporates lessons from earlier indices (like Arup and Notre Dame) to standardize dimensions across environmental, economic, infrastructural, and social categories.
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Highlights the shift towards multi-dimensional benchmarking within localized US contexts, informing how comparative indices are constructed.

**5. Global Cities Resilience Index (GCRI)**

- **Classification:** Applied to real city/cities
- **City/Case Study:** 31 global cities (including London, Amsterdam, New York, Dubai, Toronto).
- **Exact Supporting Quote:** “Our analysis of 31 cities for the first edition of the GCRI—across Global North and Global South economies—provides insights into the foundations that enable urban resilience”.
- **Chronological Context:** 2025. Reacts against existing ESG measurements that merely assess past/current performance rather than measuring "systemic readiness" for future uncertainties.
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Demonstrates the latest evolution in resilience measurement towards predictive institutional and technological readiness.

**6. RESCCUE RAF & ICARIA Holistic Resilience Assessment Framework**

### (b) Synthesis

The analyzed sources reveal a mature, highly active field where the majority of urban resilience frameworks are **actively applied to real cities** (12 out of 17 identified frameworks have concrete evidence of application).

The literature shows a distinct chronological evolution in _how_ these frameworks are applied:

1. **Early Applications (2009–2014):** Driven heavily by international NGOs and philanthropic networks (e.g., Rockefeller Foundation’s 100 Resilient Cities, ACCCRN). Frameworks were applied through participatory workshops targeting basic governance, vulnerability mapping, and the shift away from purely physical disaster response towards social-ecological awareness (e.g., Tanner et al., 2009; Tyler & Moench, 2012).
2. **Transitional Applications (2015–2021):** Frameworks expanded to include sophisticated quantitative modeling of complex relationships, such as how municipal organization structures moderate financial investments in adaptation (Valdivieso et al., 2021) or addressing specific community vs. systemic needs in post-conflict/high-risk zones (Diner, 2022).
3. **Modern Applications (2022–2026):** Present-day framework applications are characterized by heavy integration of Big Data, Internet of Things (IoT), and composite indices. They test regional interdependencies (ICARIA), real-time urban transport variability (RESOLUTE ERMG), and multi-dimensional systemic readiness across dozens of global cities concurrently (Kearney GCRI; ESI Climate Resilience Index).

The presence of purely **conceptual frameworks** (e.g., Antifragility, Smart vs. Resilient City joints, Systemic vs. Community frames) serves to critique and refine these applications. They actively warn against "technocentrism" and hidden biases, ensuring that the applied frameworks do not regress into rigid, localized structural engineering tools, but rather maintain holistic, community-integrated paradigms.
