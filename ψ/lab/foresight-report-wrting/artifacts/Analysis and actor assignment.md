
### Leverage Point 1: **Zakat‑State Blended Finance Mechanism**

- **Gap addressed:** Vulnerable youth lack startup capital and flexible funding for alternative education; state budgets are rigid. Poverty drives early marriage, so youth need access to financial resources.
    
- **H2 transitional action:** Over the next 3‑10 years, establish a Public‑Private‑Religious Partnership (PPRP) that digitises and formalises zakat and waqf funds for youth upskilling. Digital payments can reduce leakage and ensure funds reach female beneficiaries.
    
- **Assigned actors:**
    
    - **Lead:** Local government organisations and provincial Islamic committees, because zakat and waqf distribution is a religious function and local bodies are trusted more than central authorities.
        
    - **Broker/Operator:** Civil‑society organisations such as the Luuk Rieng group (กลุ่มลูกเหรียง), which already connect orphans to education and economic opportunities. CSOs can transparently manage micro‑grants and coordinate with local leaders.
        
- **Rationale:** The child marriage report recommends using zakat and waqf to fund girls’ education and micro‑finance, while the Duly interview emphasises community trust and CSO leadership in economic programmes.
    

### Leverage Point 2: **Upgrading Pondoks to Community Resilience Hubs**

- **Gap addressed:** Climate change threatens livelihoods; centralised infrastructure may fail. Vulnerable youth often rely on pondok schools but lack safe and resilient learning environments.
    
- **H2 transitional action:** Legally empower mosques and pondoks as juristic entities; retrofit them with solar micro‑grids, offline AI learning hubs and emergency psychosocial support. These hubs can serve as safe spaces year‑round and during disasters.
    
- **Assigned actors:**
    
    - **Lead:** Religious leaders and civil‑society organisations. The Duly interview notes that community trust lies with religious leaders and CSOs; they should drive the design and management of resilience hubs.
        
    - **Support:** Local universities provide technology and data architecture; shifting the Internal Security Operations Command (กอ.รมน.) toward human‑security roles can re‑purpose military logistics for infrastructure support.
        
- **Rationale:** The importance of community‑designed education and safe spaces is emphasised. Additionally, climate disasters can push families back into early marriage; resilient hubs can help maintain educational continuity and provide psychosocial care.
    

### Leverage Point 3: **Skill‑Based Policy Sandbox**

- **Gap addressed:** Centralised education systems marginalise youth without formal certificates, keeping them out of the growing Halal‑tech and creative economy. Many pondok graduates lack recognised credentials.
    
- **H2 transitional action:** Create a legal sandbox allowing local schools and pondoks to issue “skill passports” (micro‑credentials) in languages, digital skills, halal entrepreneurship and cultural tourism. Link these credentials to hiring quotas in local enterprises to ensure demand.
    
- **Assigned actors:**
    
    - **Lead:** Private sector and local enterprises. Businesses will benefit directly from a skilled local workforce and can help define curriculum needs.
        
    - **Regulator/Enabler:** Equitable Education Fund (กสศ.) and academic institutions. They can set standards for micro‑credentials and ensure that programmes are inclusive and recognised nationwide.
        
- **Rationale:** The Duly interview highlights the need to decentralise education and recognise non‑formal skills, and emphasises investment in Halal and creative economies. By embedding micro‑credentials in law and linking them to private‑sector hiring, youth can access jobs without migrating or marrying early.

### Summary

These leverage points focus on the critical transition period of 5‑15 years. Each combines systemic reforms (finance, infrastructure, education) with named actors: local governments, Islamic committees, CSOs, religious leaders, universities, security agencies, private sector and educational regulators. Grounding these strategies in evidence from the provided research ensures that interventions align with local needs—reducing poverty-driven early marriage, building resilience to climate shocks and unlocking opportunities in the Halal‑tech and creative economy.
