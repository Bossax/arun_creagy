# PM - Continuity Spine (rewrite)

Purpose
- This is the shared continuity contract across Chapter 1–5 rewrite subtasks.
- It prevents drift in: scope, key definitions, narrative through-line, and cross-chapter promises.

Hard constraints
- The rewrite must follow the exact heading structure in [`ψ/lab/foresight-report-wrting/artifacts/base_report.md`](ψ/lab/foresight-report-wrting/artifacts/base_report.md).
- Style/depth anchor: [`ψ/inbox/Foresight Report - writing style reference.md`](ψ/inbox/Foresight%20Report%20-%20writing%20style%20reference.md).
- Final report prose is paragraph-based and final-facing. Avoid process language. Avoid quotation marks for emphasis.

## 1) Scope lock (what this report is, and is not)

### 1.1 Geography and population focus
- Geography: 3 จังหวัดชายแดนภาคใต้.
- Reader-facing naming: ใช้คำว่า 3 จังหวัดชายแดนภาคใต้ (ย่อ: 3 จชต.) อย่างสม่ำเสมอ.
- Population focus: เด็กและเยาวชน โดยเน้นกลุ่มเปราะบาง (โดยเฉพาะเด็กกำพร้าและเส้นทางการเรียนรู้/การศึกษา).

### 1.2 Time horizon
- Primary horizon: พ.ศ. 2590.
- When using intermediate horizons (backcasting/Three Horizons): keep the same step size across chapters (default: 5-year milestones unless evidence dictates otherwise).

### 1.3 What to keep as background (not the protagonist)
- Violence/security/unrest: treat as enabling context that shapes constraints, trust, and feasibility.
- The protagonist is children and youth lived reality, especially learning pathways, household economy, welfare access, and trust.

## 2) Narrative through-line (cross-chapter storyline)

This report builds one coherent line:

1) Chapter 1 establishes why looking to 2590 matters for child-centred decision-making in 3 จชต., and clarifies scope and foresight vocabulary.
2) Chapter 2 translates lived reality into an evidence-informed map of signals, drivers, system dynamics, and critical uncertainties.
3) Chapter 3 turns uncertainties into baseline and alternative futures, then articulates a desirable future and minimum conditions.
4) Chapter 4 converts the desirable future into strategy, pathways, leverage points, and a portfolio of actions that remain robust across scenarios.
5) Chapter 5 synthesizes the whole report into policy-relevant implications and strategic proposals, and names cross-cutting insights and next evidence-building tasks.

Continuity promise to the reader
- Each later chapter must explicitly build on terms introduced earlier, not redefine them.
- Any unsourced quantitative or legally specific claim must be held and tracked via the HOLD/GAP register.

## 4) Cross-chapter consistency rules

### 4.1 Definitions appear once, then referenced
- Chapter 1: introduces reader primer-level definitions (short and usable).
- Chapter 2: deepens definitions only where needed to interpret evidence.
- Chapters 3–4: use definitions as operational tools, not re-explanations.

### 4.2 Evidence hygiene
- If a claim is:
  - numeric,
  - legal,
  - historical specificity,
  - named programme scope/results,
  then it must have a traceable source or be marked HOLD and registered.
- Perplexity or secondary discovery notes are not citations.

### 4.3 Scenario naming and comparison
- Use one set of scenario names and descriptors consistently across Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 stress-testing.
- Ensure scenario comparison uses a fixed descriptor grid (do not change columns mid-report).

### 4.4 Recurrent system nodes
- When referencing the system map, keep the same central outcomes and node labels.
- Central outcome: คุณภาพชีวิตและโอกาสชีวิตของเด็กและเยาวชน (treat as the system outcome node).

## 6) Known continuity risks to watch

1) Violence/security becomes the main storyline instead of child-centred pathways.
2) Terms drift: signals vs drivers vs uncertainties used interchangeably.
3) Scenario logic not traceable to impact × uncertainty reasoning.
4) Action proposals imply legal feasibility without grounding.
5) Chapter 5 introduces new concepts not prepared earlier.
