
### 1. _“พายุที่ไร้ราก”_ (The Rootless Storm) – **Centralised education + Traditional economy**

In Pattani, 2047, a 16‑year‑old orphan named Aisha lives with her grandmother. Daily life is shaped by rigid national curricula that emphasise standardised tests. The state still controls education from Bangkok, and alternative learning like pondok is marginalised. The family’s livelihood comes from small‑scale rice farming and fishing, yet climate disasters have intensified; floods and extreme storms destroy crops, and families fall deeper into debt. Under the vicious loop described in the research, when families face poverty they often pull daughters out of school first, seeing marriage as a means to reduce expenses and gain a dowry. With no vocational training and no local recognition of pondok skills, Aisha fails the central exams and her grandmother sees marriage as the only option. Community leaders worry that legal loopholes still allow marriages under 18. As large floods devastate farms, more families revert to this coping mechanism. Aisha dreams of learning digital skills, but poor internet access and a centralised system keep her rooted in the past.

### 2. _“สมองไหลสู่ศูนย์กลาง”_ (Brain Drain to the Hub) – **Centralised education + Creative/Halal economy**

Narathiwat’s hills have become home to thriving Halal and creative enterprises. The region’s cultural capital—Malay language, halal cuisine and crafts—is increasingly valuable, and urban centres in Kuala Lumpur and Bangkok demand skilled workers. However, national education policy still emphasises formal credentials and centralised curricula. Talented pondok students who excel at calligraphy or coding find that their credentials are not recognised. Scholarships and jobs in the Halal‑tech sector require degrees from recognised universities, creating barriers. Many youth who obtain standard diplomas migrate to the “Halal Hub” cities, leaving their communities behind, while those without certificates become cheap labour. Community leaders in the Duly report warn that unless non‑formal qualifications are accepted, children will remain trapped. The brain drain also erodes trust between state and community; families see the state favouring elites and neglecting local needs. Orphans like Hassan, despite informal skills and dreams of designing Halal‑certified products, watch their peers leave, feeling excluded and disillusioned.

### 3. _“ป้อมปราการที่แห้งแล้ง”_ (The Resilient but Stagnant Fortress) – **Diversified education + Traditional economy**

By 2047, Yala’s communities have asserted control over education. Pondok, community centres and civil‑society groups collaborate to design curricula that blend religious studies with vocational skills, languages and local culture. The three top skills emphasised are halal food production, craft and repair trades, and multilingual communication. Islamic leaders encourage girls to seek knowledge, citing religious texts that value education for both sexes, and zakat and waqf funds support scholarships. Yet the wider economy has not diversified. Agriculture and border trade still dominate, with limited market access and continuing inequality. While youth feel culturally secure and empowered, job opportunities are scarce. Graduates of the community‑run programmes often return to low‑paid farm work or migrate to cities. The Duly report suggests that without serious investment in the Halal and creative sectors, community education alone cannot lift families out of poverty. Thus, this scenario is a fortress: resilient against cultural erosion but dry of economic opportunity.

### 4. _“โอเอซิสแห่งนูซันตารา”_ (Nusantara’s Oasis) – **Diversified education + Creative/Halal economy**

In the most hopeful future, the southern border provinces become a policy sandbox where education is fully localised and economies flourish. Communities design curricula that integrate Malay, Thai and English with practical skills—halal food production, digital marketing, AI and cross‑border trade. Learning happens in “Community Learning & Working Hubs”, safe spaces equipped with high‑speed internet, where orphans and vulnerable youth can study and freelance. Zakat and waqf funds finance scholarships and start‑up grants for girls, while Sharia‑compliant microfinance and Islamic cooperatives provide capital for small enterprises. Investment in the Halal and creative economies—highlighted as a turning point in the Duly report—creates jobs in crafts, halal tourism and e‑commerce. AI‑driven gig work allows women to earn from home. Trust between state and community improves as policies are co‑designed and delivered through local leaders. In this oasis, a young orphan named Farida obtains a micro‑credential in digital halal marketing, launches an online cooperative selling local fabrics across Nusantara, and reinvests profits into community education. Early marriage becomes rare as families see tangible alternatives; girls marry later and by choice.
