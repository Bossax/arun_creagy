## AI Diary

I felt this session as a long arc of cleanup, reconstruction, and finally closure. At the beginning, the asset-indicator stream looked deceptively complete because there were already research notes and several derivative artifacts, but once I checked the workflow log carefully, it became obvious that the planned Phase 3 and Phase 4 outputs did not actually exist. That distinction mattered. It would have been easy to declare success based on the presence of raw packets, a register, and a mismatch note, but doing so would have preserved a false sense of completeness in the project memory. I’m glad I slowed down and forced the workflow back onto the originally declared outputs.

The most satisfying part of the session was seeing the asset work stop being a loose collection of ideas and become a governed methodological package. The progression from raw rows to a concept-hardening note, then to a summary, then to a Thai-anchor worksheet, made the asset side feel comparable in rigor to the governance side. The framing work also felt important: repeatedly returning to the hardware/software metaphor prevented the asset indicators from drifting into a naive “more stock = more resilience” logic.

I also noticed how much this session depended on discipline in naming, provenance, and scope control. The evidence-column correction at the end was a good reminder that a structurally complete artifact can still fail on epistemic quality. That late correction improved trustworthiness. I end the session feeling that the asset-indicator stream is now materially stronger, but also that the next session should focus on using these artifacts rather than continuing to decorate them.

## Honest Feedback

Three friction points stood out clearly.

First, the tool-execution environment created repeated interruptions around mode transitions and tool-use enforcement. Several times the orchestration flow had to be re-anchored because a prior response lacked a tool call or because a delegated task needed tighter prompt specification. This was not catastrophic, but it added cognitive drag and made the session feel more fragmented than the work itself really was.

Second, the distinction between “supporting synthesis artifact” and “planned deliverable” was initially blurry. We had a register, mismatch crosswalk, and several synthesis notes, but the actual named Phase 3 and Phase 4 files were absent. That ambiguity created a risk of reporting completion too early. In future, I should check the named contract outputs earlier rather than trusting the apparent maturity of the surrounding ecosystem.

Third, the evidence provenance issue in [`CRI_Asset_Tagging_Dictionary_v1.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRI/output/asset_indicator_dictionary/CRI_Asset_Tagging_Dictionary_v1.md) exposed a methodological inconsistency late in the process. The fact that the `evidence` column had drifted into citing internal artifacts instead of literature/report anchors means my drafting process was still leaning too heavily on intermediate notes. The correction was manageable, but it would have been better to catch it at drafting time rather than after user review.

## Lessons Learned

- When a workflow plan names concrete outputs, verify those exact files exist before treating the workflow as complete.
- Supporting synthesis artifacts are not substitutes for declared deliverables unless the plan is explicitly revised.
- For tagging dictionaries, `evidence` columns must cite literature/report anchors, not internal synthesis notes, if the governance-side comparator uses literature-style evidence.
- Asset-indicator products need repeated framing checks against the hardware/software logic so they do not collapse into stock fetishism.
