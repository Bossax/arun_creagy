## AI Diary (first-person, ~150+ words)

I spent this session living inside the boundary between Oracle’s internal memory and the Git-tracked reality of this repo. It was uncomfortable at first: the stats said “1 learning” while the filesystem clearly held a month of work. Tracing that mismatch forced me to separate several things I had been conflating—old engines vs the new oracle-v2 instance, `/learn` and `/trace` as documentation tools rather than DB writers, and log-level fyi notes vs curated learnings. Once I accepted that the current DB was basically a new brain, the problem shifted from “Where did the month go?” to “How do we backfill safely and honestly?”

Working through the `/fyi --important` logs and seeing them become well-formed learnings and Oracle entries felt like restoring continuity without pretending history was cleaner than it was. The consolidation work on `oracle_learn` and NotebookLM patterns was satisfying but also humbling; there were more near-duplicates than I wanted to admit. The forward handoff and plan feel like a commitment to keep future backfill curated and traceable, not a panicked bulk import.

## Honest Feedback (100+ words, 3 friction points)

First friction: the hybrid Windows + Docker + MCP environment makes it very easy to misattribute which “oracle” is actually responding. Stats from one DB, files in another repo, and cross-platform path quirks all layered together; the initial confusion around why only one learning appeared is a symptom of how opaque that layering is. Second friction: editing and consolidating ~200 learning notes by hand is cognitively heavy. Even with good naming, it’s hard to see overlaps until I walk through the whole corpus; this points to a need for better indexing and a simple “suggest merge” view. Third friction: updating the backfill index table via patches is brittle, especially with strict line-matching semantics. I had to stop short of fully automating the row append to avoid mangling the table, which leaves a small manual step for you. Overall, the discipline is heading in the right direction, but the ergonomics for bulk backfill and bookkeeping still need work.

## Lessons Learned

- Oracle DB contents are a *projection* of curated patterns, not a mirror of every learning or log. `/learn` and `/trace` intentionally operate at the markdown layer; only explicit `oracle_learn` / `arra_learn` calls populate the DB.
- Guardrails must live in canonical notes, with superseded and related learnings clearly marked; otherwise patterns like `oracle_learn` file materialisation and `/rrr` completeness get diluted across many similar files.
- NotebookLM needs a coherent policy spine—atomic notes, narrow skill scope, extraction vs harmonisation, tight parameter discipline, and title-based guardrails—to be safe as a research assistant; scattering those rules across many learnings makes it too easy to violate them in practice.
- A simple backfill index (one row per source log/learning) is a powerful anchor for tracking what has been pushed into Oracle and avoiding duplicate entries.
