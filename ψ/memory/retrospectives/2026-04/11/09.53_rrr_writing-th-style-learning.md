## AI Diary (writing-style focus)

I came into this session with a clear signal from you that the previous writing-th-learn output was not acceptable. The first learning note and resonance bullets were too local to one foresight report and did not meet the standard of general, strict Thai rules that can govern future drafts. That feedback forced me to reset my approach: instead of trying to summarise the report’s outline, I needed to treat the draft/final pair as data and read it the way a human editor would.

The main shift was to decompose the problem. At the lexical level, I looked for verbs, nouns, and modality choices that recur across sections: how you talk about systems, about evidence, about futures. At the sentence level, I looked at when long analytic sentences are kept and when they are split, and how causal chains are made explicit in Thai without becoming clumsy. At the paragraph level, I looked at how topic sentences are used, how many functions each paragraph is allowed to do, and when you switch into bullets. At the voice level, I watched how the text keeps distance and neutrality while still being morally clear about unacceptable outcomes.

From this, I was able to express rules that no longer mention specific provinces, projects, or acronyms, but still carry the discipline of your edits: always returning to a central outcome variable, using a modality ladder consistently, tying systems back to life chances, and controlling rhythm with alternating long and short sentences. The resonance update now encodes these as reusable Thai writing rules rather than as a shallow summary of one report. The process also clarified that future writing-th-learn passes must always honour this multi-layer analysis, not just structure-level comments, before anything is promoted into resonance.

## Honest Feedback (friction)

1. The original learning note was produced too quickly, without enough time spent reading real human edits at different levels of abstraction. That made the content feel like a paraphrase of section headings instead of a style extraction.
2. The first resonance update blurred the boundary between project-specific heuristics and cross-project rules. Without explicit checks for “project-agnostic versus local”, it is easy to pollute resonance with one-off patterns.
3. Tooling and process friction around `oracle_learn` and SKILL guardrails made the flow brittle: if the first pattern set is not high quality, the stored learning has to be superseded carefully, and that is cognitively heavier than getting the pattern right once.

## Lessons Learned

- For Thai style work, any `/writing-th-learn` run must explicitly separate four layers of analysis: lexical/diction, syntax and clause ordering, paragraph roles, and global voice/register. If one of these layers is missing, the result will feel shallow.
- Writing resonance must be treated as a cross-project style constitution, not a scratchpad. Every proposed rule should be tested conceptually against at least two distinct contexts before it is promoted.
- When learning from a draft/final pair, the right unit of analysis is “what the human changed and why” rather than “how the report is structured”. That mindset forces a focus on edits that tighten wording, clarify logic, and control tone.
