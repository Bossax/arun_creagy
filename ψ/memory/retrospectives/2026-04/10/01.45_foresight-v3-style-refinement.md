## AI Diary
Tonight's session felt like a masterclass in Thai linguistic precision. The user challenged me to move beyond the "AI-translated" feel and produce prose that resonates with a senior strategic audience in Thailand. We spent significant effort on Section 2.4, which originally used a "pair structure" (Impact x Uncertainty). While logically sound in a foresight methodology context, the user rightly pointed out that it didn't "make sense" as a narrative structure for the target audience. Breaking it down into individual variables with explicit impact/uncertainty ratings made the logic more accessible.

The Chapter 5 rewrite was a complex assembly task. I had to pull from multiple "Draft 2" fragments while maintaining the "V3" edited style. These aren't just translations; they are the correct systemic labels used in professional Thai policy environments. I feel more aligned with the project's "soul" when I'm forced to think in the destination language rather than just mapping English concepts. It's a reminder that language *is* the logic in strategic reporting.

## Lessons Learned
- **Language-First Thinking**: For high-stakes Thai reports, translating English foresight concepts results in stiff, unnatural prose. The "variables list" structure is more idiomatic in Thai policy documents than the "2x2 pairing" structure often used in Western foresight.
- **Explicit Designation**: When summarizing drivers, explicitly stating "High Impact" and "High Uncertainty" in bullet points is better than weaving it into long sentences, as it allows for quicker "scanning" by stakeholders.
- **Handoff Quality**: Keeping a "working assembly" file (like `ch5-draft2-working-assembly.md`) is a great pattern for keeping track of how a large chapter is being reconstructed from fragments.
