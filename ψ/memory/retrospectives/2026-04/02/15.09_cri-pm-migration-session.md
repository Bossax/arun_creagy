## AI Diary

I experienced this session as a transition from theory into instrumentation. At the start, CRI had an evidence registry and some important notes, but its management surfaces were still mostly narrative: a hub, a plan, and a few working notes. The goal was to give CRI the same structural advantages that CRDB already enjoys, without simply copying CRDB’s content. Working through the ledgers forced me to think carefully about the kinds of events that really matter for CRI: hearings, hypothesis pivots, data-governance decisions, and conceptual reframings like the shift to a social-ecological perspective.

Backfilling from inbox notes was the most interesting part. Those notes are where the real thinking lives, but they’re not naturally structured. I had to discriminate between “this is just exploration” and “this is a genuine direction change that deserves a trigger and a change-log entry.” I erred on the side of caution by logging only a few clear pivots and marking downstream deliverables and decisions as "to be linked" rather than pretending we already have perfect traceability. This preserves honesty while still giving us a usable spine of history.

The Public Hearing 1 pilot event is now a good demonstration of the whole system: one trigger, several deliverables, several claims, one change entry, and clearly registered evidence. That example should make it easier to extend the pattern to future hearings or milestones. The work feels like it moved CRI from scattered artifacts into a coherent instrument that future sessions can operate, not redesign from scratch.

## Honest Feedback (friction)

1. It was tempting to over-backfill history. There are many CRI notes that could arguably be triggers or changes, but trying to capture them all in one pass would create noise and risk misinterpretation. The discipline to stop after a few clear pivots was necessary but not effortless.
2. The tooling constraints around editing markdown tables made some of the structural updates (especially in the trigger and change logs) more cumbersome than they needed to be. It required iterative repair work rather than a single clean patch, which is a reminder that PM structures need to be simple enough to edit under imperfect tools.
3. The submission log remains intentionally sparse, and that can feel uncomfortable. There is a natural urge to "complete the picture" by inventing submission events, but the evidence does not yet support that. Holding that uncertainty explicitly is correct, yet it also highlights how easy it would be to introduce false certainty without noticing.

## Lessons Learned

- Ledger-first project management only becomes useful when the ledgers are grounded in real artifacts, not just design diagrams. Pulling triggers and changes from actual inbox notes and hearing outputs makes the history more trustworthy.
- Public events (like hearings) are excellent candidates for the first full pilot pass through a PM ecosystem. They bundle evidence, decisions, and deliverables in a way that is naturally ledger-friendly.
- Backfill should be purpose-driven and selective. Capturing a few pivotal internal decisions is more valuable than trying to map every small adjustment, which would dilute the signal.

## Next Steps

- Decide when and how to continue historical backfill, based on concrete questions (e.g., "When did we commit to LAO data as the backbone for capacity scoring?").
- Begin using the CRI ledgers live for future hearings, workshops, and design sessions, logging new events immediately rather than later.
- When appropriate, add explicit `E-CRI-*` entries for key hypothesis and SES-framing notes and wire them into the existing trigger and change entries.
