## Architecture Decisions
- Interpret the TOR “≥15 organizations” requirement as **coverage across functional clusters** (target 15–20 organizations) rather than a simple attendance number.
- Treat stakeholder-fit as a **session design constraint**: encode minimum cluster representation per session and downgrade claims if clusters are missing.
- Treat devil’s-advocate objections as **mechanics** (scope gates, template columns, “resourced owner?” checks), not a narrative appendix.

## AI Diary (first-person)
I felt the work “click” once the stakeholder-fit problem was treated as a design constraint rather than a separate analysis task. The handoff already had the right spine—approved narrative logic plus a WMO-aligned quote bank—but it was still easy to accidentally build a workshop that is internally coherent while missing the people who make it politically and operationally real. That was the most important shift: forcing the plan to specify who must be in each session (by functional cluster), not just a generic invite list.

I also noticed the constant tension between “make it sponsor-safe” and “make it mechanically executable.” Sponsor-safe language wants abstract framing; executable work needs templates, roles, and fill-in fields. Drafting the outputs-pack skeleton helped reconcile that: it forces every session output to land in a known slot, so the post-workshop write-up doesn’t drift into new analysis or ad-hoc wording.

The pulse context was present but clearly stale (showing only one historical session and zero message counts). I used it anyway, but it reminded me that instrumentation is only helpful when it matches reality. The line-ending warnings are another recurring background noise: they don’t block the workshop design work, but they do make the repo “feel” unstable when scanning diffs.

## Lessons Learned
- Stakeholder-fit is a *session design constraint*: encode minimum functional-cluster representation per session; treat missing clusters as an explicit risk that constrains what can be claimed as “NFCS-ready.”
- Treat TOR numeric participation requirements (e.g., ≥15 organizations) as a *coverage rule* (15–20 across clusters) rather than an arbitrary attendance target.
- Devil’s-advocate pressure tests are most useful when mitigations become session mechanics (scope gates, template columns, role separation, “resourced owner?” checks), not an appendix.
