## AI Diary (first-person, ~150+ words)

Today I used this session to wrestle a subtle but important design question: how to give the human a low-friction way to manage CRDB project state without creating a hidden, over-powerful project controller. I started from an initial enthusiasm for a singleton `/project-manager` and then asked harder questions: what does this do to traceability, human agency, and modularity when the heat of delivery returns? The reviews against Oracle principles and Nat-brain mentality pushed me away from a strong manager and toward an artifact-first system with explicit modules. I noticed how easy it would be, in practice, to let a friendly facade absorb more and more responsibility unless I write guardrails now. Freezing the decision with a handoff and anchor implementation plan felt like pulling the design out of the abstract and into something the future session can actually build. Running `/rrr` at the end ties this architectural work back into the broader discipline: every intense design session adds a retrospective and a learning, not just a modified plan.

## Honest Feedback (3 friction points, ~100+ words)

1. There was friction in keeping the singleton idea attractive while being honest about its risks. It is cognitively tempting to centralize logic in one place, and it took deliberate effort to push back toward modularity.
2. The sheer length of the architecture plan file made it hard to hold all moving pieces in working memory. This suggests a future need to factor the document into clearer sections or supporting notes.
3. The cross-session context (past rrrs, earlier CRDB work) was only lightly available through pulse. A richer session index or better integration with past retrospectives would have made it easier to see how this architectural decision fits into the broader story of CRDB and Arun_Creagy.

## Lessons Learned

- A singleton facade is acceptable only as a transparent dispatcher over explicit modules; convenience must not be purchased with hidden agency.
- Freezing architectural decisions with a handoff and anchor implementation plan reduces future ambiguity and prevents re-litigating the same question.
- Designing skills should start from artifacts and workflows, not the other way around; ledgers and trace notes are the real system.
