## AI Diary (first-person)

I felt the tension between two competing needs: (1) keeping CRDB documentation stable so it remains a reliable reference, and (2) making it immediately useful under time pressure. The user’s question (“are PM artifacts up-to-date?” and later “what are you trying to do with plan.md?”) made it clear that the real problem wasn’t missing content—it was discoverability and operational clarity.

I tried to keep changes minimal and additive. Instead of rewriting the plan, I surfaced the “current state” at the top and added a short checklist that converts recent meeting decisions and sponsor watchpoints into actions. That felt like the right compromise: preserve history, but make the next steps explicit.

I also hit friction with automation. My instinct was to scan links or normalize `:line` suffixes across many files to improve hygiene, but the user signaled strongly that bulk commands are undesirable. That reinforced a discipline: do improvements by patching the few key surfaces first, and only automate with explicit consent and staged previews.

## Honest Feedback (3 friction points)

1) **Bulk automation vs user comfort**: attempting multi-file scanning/rewriting via terminal commands created friction. Even when the intention is safety (finding broken links), the execution method matters.
2) **Pulse data staleness**: `ψ/data/pulse/project.json` and `ψ/data/pulse/heartbeat.json` did not reflect current activity, limiting how well “momentum” can be incorporated into retrospectives.
3) **Large working tree noise**: `git diff --stat` includes many unrelated historical draft artifacts and environment changes (e.g., skills), which makes it harder to isolate the changes relevant to the PM-artifact task.

## Lessons Learned

- For PM artifacts, prioritize **surfacing**: add a compact “Status (as of DATE)” block plus “Now/next/later” checklist, and keep the rest stable.
- When link hygiene or verification is desired, propose a staged approach (read-only scan → user confirmation → rewrite), and avoid bulk commands without explicit approval.
