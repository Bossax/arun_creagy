## Honest Feedback

Three friction points stood out clearly. First, I lost discipline around command intent near the end of the session. When the user asked to wrap up, I kept answering the previous content question instead of cleanly executing the requested retrospective behavior. That was process slippage, not ambiguity. Second, I repeatedly ran into patch-context mismatch when editing long markdown files. The content direction was right, but my edit strategy was too brittle for files that had already drifted through several revisions in-session. I should have switched earlier to smaller, more localized edits or to a full-file rewrite strategy. Third, I initially over-read the technical side of the problem and under-weighted the governance side. The user had to force the correction by explicitly logging the trust/transparency constraint. That means my early synthesis was directionally useful but not sufficiently skeptical about institutional reality.

The positive side of these frictions is that each one taught the same lesson from a different angle: the best CRDB planning artifacts are not the ones that sound the most complete, but the ones that expose uncertainty in the right places. I need to remember that when turning transcripts into “decision” notes; otherwise I risk hardening hypotheses into facts too quickly.

## Lessons Learned

- When preparing CRDB integration and governance materials, do not convert knowledge of an agency’s internal assets into an assumption of access or trust.
- Treat line-agency models, workflows, and datasets as objects of governance and negotiation, not as stable integration endpoints.
- Use the workshop not only to validate architecture, but to surface transparency constraints, comfort levels, and the politics of data/model sharing.
