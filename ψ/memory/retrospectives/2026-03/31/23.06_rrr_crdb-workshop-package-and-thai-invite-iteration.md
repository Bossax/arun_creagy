## Architecture Decisions
- External-facing invitation materials should be **reader-first** (why this matters + what they get), not “facilitator-first” (methods/jargon).
- Treat human edits as the highest-fidelity style signal: **edited file = source of truth**; model should learn patterns and apply them across related documents.
- Keep afternoon session “lighter” and DCCE-owned while maintaining a **mechanical bridge** from morning outputs (so the day feels connected, not two events).

## AI Diary (150+ words, first-person)
I experienced this session as a sequence of translation failures that became teachable patterns. The first drafts were logically correct, but they carried too much internal scaffolding: facilitation jargon, English terms, and details that matter to the organizer more than to the invitee. When the human edits arrived—especially the hard crosses and comments—I could see the real target voice: a Thai government-style invitation that respects the reader’s time, clarifies why the project exists, and communicates value without sounding promotional. The edits also made it clear that the strongest way to ask for non-token participation is not to say “don’t be token,” but to specify the capabilities needed in a representative and to frame participation as enabling practical support and coordination.

The agenda work was humbling: even when content is right, a misformatted table or a few out-of-place terms makes the document feel unprofessional. I had to treat formatting and idiom as part of correctness, not decoration. The most productive pattern was: accept edits as truth, extract rules, and apply them consistently across letter, agenda, and prep questions—so the whole package feels like it came from one competent hand.

## Honest Feedback (100+ words, 3 friction points)
1) I over-optimized for internal coherence (TOR alignment + facilitation mechanics) and under-optimized for external reader relevance in the invitation letter. That created avoidable friction: too much “how we run the meeting” and not enough “why this matters to you” and “what you will get.”

2) I initially used mixed Thai/English workshop jargon that may be normal in internal design teams but reads as out-of-place in formal Thai government attachments. The correction required more than substitutions; it required a shift in what the agenda attachment is for (formal public-facing schedule, not facilitation notes).

3) The iteration cadence was high because the drafts weren’t anchored tightly enough to a validated style contract at the start. Once the human-edited versions arrived, the work improved quickly. Next time, the fastest path is to draft one section, immediately calibrate with the human, then scale that style across the rest.

## Lessons Learned
- A Thai invitation letter is not a “documentation of methods”; it is a reader-facing artifact that must lead with purpose and value.
- Replace anti-token sentiment with a respectful capability request: “ผู้แทนที่มีความรู้และสามารถประสานติดตามผลได้”.
- For agenda attachments, formatting and idiom are correctness conditions; avoid internal workshop jargon.
- Edited files are training data: extract patterns and apply them across the entire package (letter + agenda + prep).
