## Architecture Decisions

## AI Diary (150+ words, first-person)
I felt the moment the mismatch became explicit: the output was not merely imperfect, it was structurally different from what was expected. The fix was not to patch sentences but to change the workflow. I switched from a single-document write to a disciplined pipeline: lock structure to the base report, define continuity rules, and then draft chapter by chapter. That let me treat each chapter as a unit with its own obligations to the reader, while protecting the overall narrative arc.

The hardest part was holding two truths at once. The prose must read as final and confident, yet the evidence base is uneven in places, especially when numbers or legally operational claims would require stronger traceability. I handled that by separating concerns: keep internal evidence risk in a register and keep the report prose grounded, careful, and reader-first. I also watched for the user’s stylistic constraints: paragraph-driven writing, no bullet-led text, no process language, no emphasis via quotation marks. The result is not a short memo. It is a structured report that can be grown and verified further without losing its spine.

## Honest Feedback (100+ words, 3 friction points)
1) The initial expectation mismatch should have been detected earlier by strictly binding structure to the base report before drafting any audience-facing prose. I allowed the outline workflow to drift into a different document genre, which created avoidable rework.

2) The earlier draft exposed internal process language (for example referencing draft stages). Even if technically accurate, it violates audience-facing conventions and reduces perceived credibility. The correction required not just deletion of those words but a broader mindset shift to final document norms.

3) Depth and length were underestimated. A 40+ page report behaves differently from a short brief: it needs more context building, smoother transitions, richer conceptual scaffolding, and more explicit reader education. The chapter pipeline is the right remedy, but it should have been the default given the style reference.

## Lessons Learned
- When a base report exists, treat it as a schema and lock headings early. Style and depth are downstream of structure.
- Separate internal evidence QA from reader-facing prose; final-facing documents should not leak scaffolding.
- For long reports, use chapter artifacts plus continuity spines to prevent drift and enable incremental improvement.
