# Lessons Learned — Thai invite iteration patterns (CRDB workshop package)

## What changed in my writing after human edits

1) **Reader-first invitations**
- Invitation letters must lead with **why this matters** and **what the organization will get**, not meeting mechanics.
- Mechanics (methods/stations/boards) belong in internal facilitation notes, not attachments.

2) **TOR vocabulary as the safe backbone**
- Use TOR-aligned wording consistently (ชุดข้อมูล / ผลิตภัณฑ์สารสนเทศ / ความต้องการใช้งาน) and avoid invented consultant-y terms.

3) **Avoid “robotic emphasis”**
- Avoid quotation marks for emphasis, informal catchphrases, and unnecessary English in Thai government attachments.
- Agenda correctness includes formatting (tables) and idiom, not only content.

4) **Ask for non-token participation without saying it**
- Don’t mention tokenism. Specify the capabilities needed in the representative:
  - understands the unit’s datasets/products and constraints
  - can reflect real needs
  - can coordinate follow-up

5) **Make questions answerable**
- Prefer prompts about current practice, bottlenecks, and 1–2 actionable suggestions.
- Scope dataset requests so they are not interpreted as exhaustive inventories.

## Implications for future drafts
- Draft one section, calibrate with human edits early, then propagate style rules across letter + agenda + prep.
- Maintain a session writing plan and append learn-back links (Nothing is Deleted).
