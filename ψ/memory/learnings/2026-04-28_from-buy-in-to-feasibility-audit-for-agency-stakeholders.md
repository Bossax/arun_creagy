# Learning: From Buy-In to Feasibility Audit

## Pattern
When designing national coordination workshops (like the CRDB/NCAIF Public Hearing for TOR 5.2.6), there is a recurring temptation to ask participants for **Institutional Buy-In** or **Governance Ratification**. 

However, technical staff and line agency representatives usually lack the legal authority to bind their organizations to new standards or MOUs on the spot. Asking for "commitment" in this context leads to:
1.  **Bureaucratic Silence**: Participants stop contributing to avoid making a "mistake" for their agency.
2.  **Expert Drift**: Consultants design a "perfect" system that is never implemented because the real hurdles weren't surfaced.

## Correction: The Feasibility Audit
Instead of "Ratification," the session must be framed as a **Technical Feasibility Audit** or **Implementation Stress-Test**.

*   **Change the Persona**: Participants are invited as **Subject Matter Experts** (Diagnostic Experts) whose job is to "find the holes" in the consultant's draft.
*   **Change the Question**: Instead of *"Will you agree to this?"*, ask *"What specific technical, legal, or procedural barriers will stop this from working in your agency?"*
*   **The Result**: You extract a **Gap Analysis for Implementation** (Red Hurdles) and a **Utility-Based Priority List** (Green Benefits), which provides the technical team with a realistic development roadmap.

## Implementation Notes
- Use **Neutral Terminology**: Avoid consultant jargon (e.g., "HMW", "Stress-Test", "Handshake"). Use professional equivalents (e.g., "Implementation Challenges", "Inter-agency Alignment").
- **Energy Management**: The "Audit" mode is high-interaction and low-risk, making it ideal for the afternoon session when energy levels typically drop.
