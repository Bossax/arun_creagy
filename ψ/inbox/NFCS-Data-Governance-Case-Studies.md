National Frameworks for Climate Services and the Optimization of Data Governance for Climate Risk Management: A Comparative Global Analysis

The institutionalization of climate services through the National Framework for Climate Services (NFCS) represents a critical evolution in the global strategy to manage climate-related risks. As the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events increase, the shift from providing generalized meteorological data to delivering tailored, actionable climate information has become a prerequisite for sustainable development. The Global Framework for Climate Services (GFCS), established by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO), provides the overarching architecture for this transition, yet its success is fundamentally dependent on how individual nations apply its principles to their unique socio-economic and environmental contexts. Central to this application is the enhancement of data governance—the structured management of data availability, usability, integrity, and security—to ensure that climate information facilitates informed decision-making across all levels of society.   

The Strategic Architecture of National Frameworks for Climate Services

An NFCS is not merely a technical system but a coordination mechanism designed to bridge the historical divide between climate information providers, primarily National Meteorological and Hydrological Services (NMHSs), and the diverse set of users who require this information for risk management. This mechanism is built upon five foundational pillars that mirror the GFCS structure: observations and monitoring; climate services information systems; research, modeling, and prediction; user interface platforms; and capacity building. The application of these pillars at the national level requires a rigorous five-step process: identifying stakeholders, conducting national consultations, developing strategic and costed action plans, securing high-level political endorsement, and implementing the framework with robust monitoring and evaluation.   

The governance of data within this framework is guided by the principle that climate information is an international public good, necessitating free and open exchange while respecting national security and citizen safety. This duality creates a complex regulatory environment where nations must harmonize international standards, such as the WMO Information System (WIS) and the Integrated Global Observing System (WIGOS), with domestic data protection laws.

The underlying trend suggested by these principles is a move toward "democratized data," where the value of information is measured not by its volume but by its ability to reduce vulnerability. The causal relationship between improved data governance and risk reduction is evident: when mandates are clear and institutional overlaps are minimized, the reliability of science-based predictions increases, leading to more effective early warnings and long-term adaptation planning.   

Kenya: Institutionalizing Co-Production and County-Level Integration

Kenya’s application of NFCS principles is driven by the recurring devastating episodes of floods, droughts, and pests that have historically hampered its socio-economic development. The Kenyan NFCS aims to coordinate actors along the entire climate services value chain, focusing on the co-design and co-production of information. This approach recognizes that top-down data delivery often fails to meet the specific needs of local farmers or pastoralists who require hyper-local, sector-specific advisories.   

The governance model in Kenya emphasizes the enhancement of weather observation networks and monitoring systems, which are essential for generating high-resolution data. By reviewing the capacities of existing climate services and identifying the gaps in user sectors such as agriculture and disaster risk reduction, Kenya is positioning its meteorological services as a "decision-support system" rather than a mere data repository.   

A critical component of the Kenyan framework is the "User Interface Platform," which serves as a structured means for researchers and providers to interact with end-users. In Kenya, this platform is decentralized, aiming to build capacity at both national and county levels. This decentralization is a significant governance innovation; it acknowledges that risk management is inherently local and that data governance must therefore support the flow of information to the most granular administrative units.   

Malawi: The National Framework for Water and Climate Services (NFWCS)

Malawi has taken a specialized approach by establishing the National Framework for Water and Climate Services (NFWCS), reflecting the country's high dependency on water-sensitive sectors and its vulnerability to hazards like Cyclone Freddy. The NFWCS is a multi-sectoral initiative that aligns with the Malawi Vision 2063, prioritizing the integration of disaster risk reduction (DRR) into all national development activities.   

Financial and Strategic Allocation for Climate Resilience

To realize the goals of the NFWCS, Malawi has developed a comprehensive costed action plan. The total estimated budget of approximately USD 63.4 million for the 2024–2028 period reflects a significant national and international commitment to climate data infrastructure. This budget is strategically allocated across the five GFCS pillars to ensure a balanced development of the climate services value chain.

The governance of data in Malawi is characterized by the establishment of "Stakeholder Coordination Committees," which facilitate ongoing feedback and ensure that the Department of Climate Change and Meteorological Services (DCCMS) remains responsive to user requirements. A notable second-order insight from Malawi’s framework is the emphasis on "citizen science" through the participation of youth and local communities. By utilizing mobile applications like "Waterproofing Data," young learners can record local rainfall and flood events, thereby filling data gaps in areas where formal meteorological stations are absent. This bottom-up data generation not only enhances the national database but also fosters local ownership of climate resilience efforts, transforming children and youth into active agents of change.   

Tanzania: Legal Mandates and Digital Transformation in Climate Services

Tanzania launched its NFCS in August 2018, with the Tanzania Meteorological Authority (TMA) serving as the lead institution. The Tanzanian framework is unique in its strong linkage between climate services and the nation's broader digital transformation agenda. The framework recognizes that for climate services to be effective, they must be supported by a robust legal foundation and a modern, integrated data infrastructure.   

The implementation of the NFCS in Tanzania is governed by several key pieces of legislation that clarify mandates and responsibilities. The Meteorological Act No. 6 of 1978 and its subsequent updates, including the TMA Act No. 2 of 2019, establish the TMA as the authoritative provider of weather and climate services. This legislative clarity is essential for avoiding the institutional overlaps that frequently plague climate service delivery in other nations.

Tanzania’s focus on data governance also extends to the quality management of its services. The TMA is ISO 9001:2015 certified for its provision of meteorological services to the aviation sector, a standard it aims to expand across other service areas. This commitment to international standards ensures that the data provided is credible and usable for complex decision-making in sectors like transport and infrastructure.   

Furthermore, Tanzania has integrated climate services into its "Open Contracting" reforms. By building locally owned, transactional e-procurement systems like the National e-Procurement System of Tanzania (NeST), the government is creating a more transparent environment for the procurement of climate-resilient infrastructure. This illustrates a sophisticated third-order insight: robust data governance in general public financial management is a critical enabler for the effective implementation of specific climate change risk management strategies.   

Burkina Faso: Navigating Infrastructure Gaps and Market Demand

Burkina Faso, as one of the initial pilot countries for the NFCS in Africa, faces significant challenges related to its landlocked geography and highly variable Sahelian climate. The impacts of climate change in Burkina Faso are projected to lead to a 1.7°C temperature rise and increased variations in rainfall by 2050, threatening the livelihoods of its 70% rural population.   

The National Framework for Climate Services in Burkina Faso, endorsed in April 2016, identifies baseline needs and gaps in the national chain linking climate knowledge to action. A primary gap is the inadequacy of the ground-based observation network; many existing stations lack the digital infrastructure for real-time data transmission, resulting in data being "siloed" at the local level. To address this, projects supported by the World Bank and the Climate Risk and Early Warning Systems (CREWS) initiative are modernizing 40+ meteorological and 10+ hydrological stations with telemetry and satellite monitoring equipment.   

Socio-Economic Valuation and Willingness to Pay

A pioneering aspect of the climate services landscape in Burkina Faso is the research into the market value of climate information. Studies have shown that 63% of small-scale farmers are willing to pay for climate information services (CIS), such as seasonal forecasts and decadal weather outlooks. This data point is transformative; it suggests that climate services can transition from a purely donor-funded public good to a sustainable service delivery model.   

The willingness to pay is directly linked to the perceived utility of the information in reducing crop failure and optimizing the use of inputs. For instance, cowpea and sesame farmers who received tailored agro-meteorological advisories were better able to manage the risks of dry spells and erratic rains. This finding underscores the broader implication that data governance must focus on "tailoring" and "packaging"—ensuring that the raw data is translated into formats that are immediately actionable for end-users.

Bhutan: Technological Leadership and High-Altitude Resilience

Bhutan, a small mountainous nation, faces unique climate risks, including glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs) and extreme temperature variability. Despite being the world’s first carbon-negative country, Bhutan is highly vulnerable due to its reliance on climate-sensitive sectors like hydropower and agriculture. The nation’s approach to NFCS principles is characterized by a high degree of technological integration and a focus on professionalizing data management.   

Bhutan was the first country to operationalize a customized Climate Services Toolkit (CST) web platform with the support of the WMO. This platform enables the National Centre for Hydrology and Meteorology (NCHM) to provide tailored climate products for the agriculture, water, and health sectors. The CST provides sub-national data that can be linked directly to sector-specific decision support systems, a critical capability for managing risks in terrain as diverse as the Himalayas.   

The GovTech Model and Data Governance Baseline

The establishment of the Government Technology (GovTech) Agency in Bhutan marks a shift toward a holistic national data governance policy. A 2024 baseline study conducted by the GovTech Agency and UN DESA identified that while data is increasingly central to government operations, the lack of a unified management strategy often limits its value. The governance of climate data in Bhutan is now being integrated into a "Four Pillars" framework consisting of policy, institutions, processes, and people.

A significant governance challenge in Bhutan is the transition from "informal community-based management" to professionalized entities. For example, rural water supplies managed by untrained volunteers often become dysfunctional; the government is now promoting professional management models that utilize localized water data and performance agreements. This transition reflects the broader insight that technical infrastructure must be matched by institutional maturity to ensure the long-term sustainability of climate services.   

Vietnam: Transboundary Governance and Climate-Smart Agriculture (CSA)

Vietnam’s climate risk management is defined by its massive river systems, specifically the Mekong and Red River deltas, and its vulnerability to rising sea levels. With over 70% of its surface water originating from upstream countries, Vietnam’s data governance must operate at a transboundary level. The Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development (MARD) and the Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment (MONRE) have led efforts to integrate climate services into the National Adaptation Plan (NAP) and the country’s Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs).   

The governance of climate data in Vietnam is increasingly focused on "Climate-Smart Agriculture" (CSA). CSA initiatives in the Mekong Delta use agro-climate information services to help farmers adjust to saltwater intrusion and varying flow regimes. These services are supported by automated observatory systems; by 2020, Vietnam aimed to have a density of hydrometeorological observatories similar to developed countries, with 90% automation.

Vietnam's experience highlights the "inter-ministry collaboration" bottleneck. Insufficient information sharing between the lead climate ministry (MONRE) and other sector ministries often restricts the overall success of adaptation policies. Furthermore, the "growth-first" economic model sometimes prioritizes urban infrastructure in ways that undermine long-term climate resilience, such as developing wetlands that serve as natural flood buffers. These tensions illustrate that data governance must not only be technical but also strategically aligned with national economic planning to avoid "maladaptation".   

Cross-Cutting Themes in Global NFCS Application

The case studies of Kenya, Malawi, Tanzania, Burkina Faso, Bhutan, and Vietnam reveal several overarching trends and insights that are relevant to the global community of climate risk practitioners.

The Intersectional and Gendered Impacts of Weather and Climate

Data governance is frequently criticized for being "gender-neutral," yet empirical evidence shows that climate impacts are experienced differently by different groups of women and men. For example, in many rural contexts in Africa and Asia, women have limited access to early warnings due to lower literacy rates or restricted access to mobile technology. Effective NFCS governance must therefore prioritize the collection of gender-disaggregated data and the implementation of gender-responsive policies.   

The research into gendered impacts reveals that rural women, the elderly, and those with disabilities are often the most affected by climate-related disasters. In Vietnam, programs have specifically targeted women for training in Integrated Pest Management and sanitation campaigns, recognizing their central role in household-level climate resilience. This highlights a critical governance principle: inclusivity in the "User Interface Platform" is not just a matter of social equity but a prerequisite for the effectiveness of the entire climate services system.   

The Paradox of Data Availability versus Usability

A recurring insight across all regions is that while climate data is often available, it is underutilized due to a "usability gap". Only 23% of Ministries of Health globally utilize meteorological information in their health surveillance systems, despite the known links between climate and vector-borne diseases. This indicates that the bottleneck is not the generation of data, but the lack of "translation" services that can turn raw climate model outputs into sector-specific advisories.   

The transition to the WMO Information System 2.0 (WIS 2.0) and the WMO Unified Data Policy are intended to address these technical barriers by promoting more fluid data exchange. However, the institutional barriers—such as the lack of trained data analysts in sector ministries—remain significant.   

The Role of Public-Private Partnerships (PPP) in Data Dissemination

As climate risks grow, governments are increasingly looking to the private sector to assist in the dissemination of climate services. In Tanzania and Malawi, partnerships with mobile network operators have enabled the broadcast of climate and agriculture information via SMS and radio to millions of households. In Burkina Faso, the digitalization of agricultural services aims to improve access to information and inclusive financial services like index-based insurance.   

These partnerships raise new questions about data governance, specifically around data privacy and the ethical use of information. The Malabo Convention and regional frameworks like the ECOWAS Data Protection Act provide essential guidelines, but the practical enforcement of these laws at the country level often lacks the necessary independent oversight.   

Strategic Synthesis and Recommendations

The analysis of countries applying NFCS principles demonstrates that the enhancement of data governance is the lynchpin of effective climate change risk management. The journey from "observations" to "action" requires a sophisticated alignment of technology, policy, and human capacity.

Core Strategic Recommendations

The following recommendations emerge from the synthesis of the reviewed frameworks and case studies:

Strengthen Legal Mandates: Countries must ensure that their National Meteorological and Hydrological Services (NMHSs) have clear, legally enshrined mandates for data collection and service provision to avoid institutional fragmentation.   

Prioritize Co-Production: Climate services should move away from a "linear delivery" model to a "collaborative co-production" model, ensuring that end-users are involved in the design of data products from the outset.   

Invest in Localized Governance: Governance structures must support the flow of information to the sub-national and community levels, empowering local leaders and youth to generate and act upon climate data.   

Integrate Gender Perspectives: Data collection and service design must be intersectional, addressing the specific vulnerabilities and needs of women, the elderly, and marginalized groups.   

Professionalize Data Management: Transition from informal, volunteer-led systems to professional entities supported by standardized quality management and sustainable financing models.   

Enhance Transboundary Data Exchange: For nations sharing critical water or air resources, transboundary data governance through regional organizations is essential for managing systemic climate risks.   

The evolution of National Frameworks for Climate Services is a testament to the global recognition that managing climate risk is no longer an optional component of development but a core strategic requirement. By applying NFCS principles to enhance data governance, countries can transform climate information from a technical output into a powerful tool for building a more resilient and sustainable future. The success of these frameworks will ultimately be measured not by the complexity of their models, but by the tangible reduction in vulnerability and the preservation of lives and livelihoods in the face of a changing climate.

Synthesizing Regional Frameworks
I have identified the core operational structures of the National Framework for Climate Services (NFCS) in Kenya and Tanzania, specifically focusing on how they transition from raw meteorological observations to 'co-produced' climate services. My analysis reveals that these frameworks prioritize five key pillars—agriculture, disaster risk reduction, energy, health, and water—to ensure that climate data is not just collected but actively tailored for decision-makers. I am now synthesizing how these countries define clear institutional mandates to prevent the overlapping responsibilities that often hinder effective climate governance.
Resolving Data Exchange Contradictions
I am currently resolving a tension between the global principle of 'free and open' climate data exchange and the practical limitations of national security and institutional capacity. While international guidelines advocate for transparency, my research into West African and East African case studies shows that data minimization and localized sovereignty are becoming central to national legislation. I have found evidence in Tanzania of a shift toward 'locally-owned' digital systems that integrate over 20 government services, effectively replacing fragmented, manual processes with automated data-sharing protocols.
Mapping Infrastructure Integration
New insights from Bhutan and Vietnam highlight a sophisticated link between climate services and infrastructure resilience. In Bhutan, I am examining how 'water flagship' programs use specialized climate toolkits to professionalize rural water management, moving away from informal volunteer models toward data-driven oversight. In Vietnam, I have observed a strong focus on transboundary data governance, where hydrological information sharing in the Mekong and Red River basins is utilized to mitigate saltwater intrusion and protect millions of hectares of farmland. These examples demonstrate the practical application of climate data in high-stakes resource management.
Deepening Case Study Analysis
My next actions involve a deep dive into the specific legislative frameworks and standardization protocols used in Malawi and Burkina Faso. I will be looking for documented instances of public-private partnerships that facilitate climate data flow, as well as searching for independent evaluations from global institutions to verify the long-term impact of these frameworks on national resilience. This will allow me to finalize a selection of high-quality sources that illustrate the full cycle of climate data governance.
