# TLDR Answer

Local-government governance capacity for climate adaptation and disaster risk management (DRM) is best measured using **process-oriented indicators**—such as frequency, timeliness, coordination, and learning loops—anchored to administrative events and supported by clear tagging logic; frameworks that distinguish process from stock indicators and use management-science proxies (e.g., service delivery timeliness) enhance comparability, decision-use, and reduce subjectivity.

## 1. Introduction

Local governments play a pivotal role in climate adaptation and disaster risk management (DRM), yet measuring their governance or implementation capacity remains challenging due to the complexity of urban systems and the diversity of adaptation actions. Recent literature emphasizes the need for robust indicator frameworks that capture not only static resources ("stock" indicators) but also dynamic processes—such as the frequency of plan revisions, timeliness of procurement cycles, interdepartmental coordination, and organizational learning loops—to assess adaptive capacity effectively (Hossain & Samiha, 2024; Rogers et al., 2023; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Chapagain et al., 2025; Fila et al., 2024; Siders, 2019). Process-oriented metrics are increasingly recognized for their ability to reflect real-time administrative performance and facilitate cross-municipality comparisons (Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Kenney & Gerst, 2021). Taxonomies and tagging logics grounded in municipal mandates or legal documents help standardize indicator selection, reducing inter-rater variance compared to frameworks relying solely on expert judgment (Rogers et al., 2023; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). Management-science literature further contributes proxy indicators from public administration—like service delivery timeliness or case throughput—that can be repurposed for governance-capacity assessment without requiring new data collection (Serdar et al., 2021). The distinction between process and stock indicators is critical: frameworks that explicitly separate these types tend to support better decision-making, such as budget reallocation or coordination reforms (Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Kenney & Gerst, 2021). However, gaps remain in operationalizing these concepts across diverse local contexts. This review synthesizes recent advances in indicator development, classification logic, and practical measurement strategies for local government climate adaptation capacity.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Process-Oriented Indicators: Definitions & Data Sources

Process-oriented indicators focus on dynamic aspects of governance such as:

- **Frequency**: Number of plan revisions or coordination meetings per year.
- **Timeliness**: Speed of procurement cycles or response times.
- **Coordination**: Number of interdepartmental meetings; existence of formalized coordination protocols.
- **Learning Loops**: Evidence of after-action reviews or policy updates based on feedback.

These are typically derived from administrative records (meeting minutes, procurement logs), official reports (annual performance reviews), or stakeholder surveys (Hossain & Samiha, 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Fila et al., 2024; Kenney & Gerst, 2021).

### 3.2 Indicator Frameworks: Taxonomy & Tagging Logic

Recent frameworks employ structured taxonomies that classify indicators by type (input/process/output/outcome/impact) and domain (governance, infrastructure, social systems). Tagging logic often relies on:

- Alignment with municipal mandate documents (legal duties/KPIs)
- Decision rules specifying when an indicator is classified as "process" vs "stock"
- Use of long-lists refined via Delphi surveys or participatory workshops (Hossain & Samiha, 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Feldmeyer et al., 2019)Frameworks like MONARES (Feldmeyer et al., 2019)or those reviewed in global city samples (Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022)provide detailed action fields (e.g., participation, budget allocation) with explicit classification rules.

### 3.3 Management-Science Proxies for Governance Capacity

Public administration literature offers measurable proxies such as:

- Service delivery timeliness
- Case throughput rates
- Coordination costs
- Bureaucratic friction indices

These are often available from routine administrative datasets at the municipal level and can be adapted as governance-capacity metrics without new data collection (Serdar et al., 2021).

### 3.4 Process vs Stock Indicators: Distinctions & Justifications

Process indicators measure ongoing activities/events; stock indicators measure static resources/assets (e.g., number of trained staff). Frameworks that clearly separate these types justify the distinction by:

- Emphasizing comparability across municipalities
- Supporting targeted decision-making (e.g., identifying bottlenecks)
- Reducing ambiguity in evaluation (Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Kenney & Gerst, 2021)Some studies argue that process metrics anchored to administrative events improve comparability more than asset-based measures (Kenney & Gerst, 2021), while others highlight the need for both types to capture holistic capacity (Siders, 2019).

## 4. Discussion

The literature demonstrates growing consensus that process-oriented indicators—especially those tied to concrete administrative events—offer superior comparability across municipalities compared to traditional asset-based ("stock") measures (Hossain & Samiha, 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022; Kenney & Gerst, 2021). These metrics enable real-time tracking of adaptive actions such as plan updates or interdepartmental coordination meetings—a critical advantage given the dynamic nature of climate risks (Fila et al., 2024). Tagging rules grounded in legal mandates or official KPIs further enhance reliability by reducing subjective interpretation during indicator classification (Rogers et al., 2023; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). Management-science proxies like service delivery timeliness are both feasible and valuable for assessing governance capacity without imposing additional data collection burdens on municipalities (Serdar et al., 2021).

However, challenges persist: many frameworks lack standardized definitions or fail to specify data sources/timelines for monitoring (Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). There is also a tendency to focus on outputs rather than outcomes/impacts due to data limitations. While explicit separation between process and stock indicators supports better decision-use (e.g., resource allocation), some argue that both types are needed for a holistic view—processes drive change but stocks represent underlying potential (Siders, 2019).

## 5. Conclusion

Process-oriented indicators—especially those linked to administrative events—are increasingly recognized as essential tools for measuring local-government governance capacity in climate adaptation/DRM. Frameworks employing clear taxonomies/tagging logic grounded in mandate documents enhance reliability; management-science proxies offer practical measurement options; explicit separation between process/stock improves decision-usefulness but should be balanced with holistic assessment needs.

### Research Gaps

Despite progress, significant gaps remain regarding standardized definitions/taxonomies across contexts; operationalization in small/medium municipalities; integration with outcome/impact measures; and empirical validation linking indicator use to improved adaptation outcomes.
