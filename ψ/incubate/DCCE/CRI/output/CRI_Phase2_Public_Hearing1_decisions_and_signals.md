## 2. Validated design stances

### 2.1 Profiling over ranking

- Stakeholders strongly endorsed the idea that **CRI should not be a single 1–76 ranking**.
- There was clear appetite for **multi-dimensional profiles** that reflect:
  - Impact (Fiscal Relief / Human Impact).
  - Coping, Adaptive and Transformative capacity.
  - Data-richness / confidence.
- Ranking is still politically useful, but the index should emphasise **profiles for diagnosis and design**, not competition.

### 2.2 Capacity profiles as multi-scores

- The separation into **Coping / Adaptive / Transformative** capacity tiers was positively received and understood when linked to concrete examples (emergency response, planning and budgeting, structural reform).
- Stakeholders asked for **visualisations** that clearly show where a province is strong/weak along these axes, aligning with the **radar/profile** approach described in Phase 2 methodology.
- There was acceptance that capacity scores will initially reflect **structural readiness** (existence of mechanisms) rather than full process quality, provided this is clearly communicated.

### 2.3 Data-richness overlay and two-speed measurement

- Concern about indicator validity (especially LPA-style checklist data) reinforced the need for a **data-richness / confidence overlay (0–3)**.
- Participants appreciated the distinction between:
  - **Baseline proxies** (what can be measured now, often binary), and
  - **Target process-quality metrics** (what should be measured later for evaluation and accountability).
- Hearing 1 therefore **validates the two-speed measurement stance** already encoded in Phase 2 methodology.

### 2.4 Impact vs capacity separation

- The distinction between **Impact Index (backward-looking, fiscal relief and human impact)** and **Capacity Profiles (forward-looking structural readiness)** was well received.
- Stakeholders recognised that high impact in the past does not automatically imply low capacity, and vice versa; combining impact and capacity in a single score would obscure important stories.

## 3. Stakeholder requirements

### 3.1 Output formats

Stakeholders requested that CRI outputs be made available in several forms:

- **Interactive maps and dashboards**
  - Provincial and district maps for impact and capacity scores.
  - Ability to toggle between capacity tiers and data-richness overlays.
- **Tabular / Excel exports**
  - Downloadable tables for use in planning, budgeting and reporting systems.
- **GIS-ready layers**
  - Shapefiles or equivalent for integration into existing GIS workflows in ministries, municipalities and research institutions.
- **Narrative briefs**
  - Short provincial profile narratives that can be inserted into plans and reports.

### 3.2 Spatial resolution

- **Minimum:** provincial-level outputs for national and inter-provincial comparison.
- **Preferred:** district-level capacity and impact outputs, with the ability to link back to **tambon/village-level data** where forms and registries support it.
- Stakeholders emphasised that data must be **trustworthy and auditable at the chosen scale**, even if that means not publishing some very granular outputs in the first release.

### 3.3 Use-case alignment

Hearing 1 confirmed that CRI will be used for:

- **Planning and budgeting**
  - Prioritising provinces/districts for adaptation investment.
  - Designing programmes that target weak capacity tiers (e.g. strengthening adaptive capacity where coping is strong but planning is weak).
- **Reporting and accountability**
  - Feeding into national adaptation reports and city network reporting (e.g. C40, ICLEI).
  - Supporting ministries and municipalities to demonstrate progress.
- **Finance and risk management**
  - Providing a public, transparent reference for banks and investors on physical climate risk in different provinces.
  - Helping listed companies and financial institutions respond to IFRS/ISSB-type disclosure expectations for physical risk.

### 3.4 Capacity-building and guidance

- Stakeholders requested **training and guidance materials** that explain:
  - How to interpret CRI maps and capacity profiles.
  - How to connect CRI with existing planning/budgeting cycles.
  - How to read and use the **data-richness/confidence** cues.
- Quick-win suggestions included:
  - Short online tutorials and example use-cases.
  - A one-page "How to read your CRI capacity profile" guide.

## 4. Tensions and trade-offs

### 4.1 Granularity vs data quality

- Desire for fine spatial granularity (tambon/village) conflicts with current **data quality and system design**:
  - Data is often captured granularly but stored and processed at higher levels.
  - Many registers and forms are not structured for analytics.
- Design signal: **prioritise robust district-level outputs with transparent data lineage**, while planning for future upgrades that enable safer use of sub-district data.

### 4.2 Capacity measurement vs audit risk

- Local authorities expressed concern that capacity indicators might be used to **audit or penalise** them.
- To mitigate this, CRI must:
  - Frame capacity scores as **opportunities for support and investment**, not as compliance scores.
  - Clearly separate **structural readiness** (what exists) from **performance** (how well it is used), and avoid over-claiming on the latter.

### 4.3 Standardisation vs local flexibility

- A single national indicator set risks missing local particularities, but fully bespoke indicators by province would undermine comparability and feasibility.
- The hearing supports a **standardised core indicator set**, with room for **supplementary local indicators** (especially in pilot provinces and city networks).

### 4.4 Fiscal vs physical loss narratives

- Some participants still equate **Fiscal Relief** with total damage, or expect CRI to capture full economic loss.
- CRI design must therefore maintain the **Fiscal Relief Index framing**, while clearly explaining:
  - What is included/excluded.
  - How exposure proxies and capacity scores complete the picture.

## 5. Open questions for follow-up / Hearing 2

The following questions remain open and should be addressed in subsequent design work and consultations:

1. **Transformative capacity operationalisation**
   - Which indicators are realistically observable at sub-national level, and which should remain at national/strategic level?
   - How to avoid overburdening local authorities while still capturing structural reform signals?

2. **Formal spatial resolution contract**
   - What is the formal minimum resolution (province vs district) that DCCE expects for Phase 2 outputs?
   - How will CRI handle areas where only partial or low-quality sub-district data is available?

3. **Data-governance roadmap**
   - Which **forms, registers and digital systems** should be upgraded first to support CRI (e.g. damage/relief forms, LPA modules, budget systems)?
   - How can these upgrades also serve broader adaptation M&E and disaster risk management needs?

4. **Finance and private-sector integration**
   - How should CRI outputs be packaged for banks and investors (e.g. API, regular bulletins, scenarios)?
   - What collaboration is possible between DCCE, regulators and industry associations on physical-risk metrics?

5. **Training and institutionalisation**
   - Which agencies will be responsible for training and ongoing support on CRI interpretation?
   - How should CRI be embedded in existing national planning/budget frameworks and city-network commitments?

These open questions should be revisited in **Hearing 2** and documented in subsequent methodology revisions.
