# CRI Capacity Tagging Dictionary v3: Institutional Readiness Framework

## 1. Methodological Conclusion: The "Institutional Readiness" Choice

This version of the dictionary freezes the structural options for **Governance** based on a synthesis of the **CRI Phase 2 Methodology** and the **Urban Resilience Framework Curation** (specifically the *Strategy&* and *Valdivieso et al. 2021* studies).

### Why the "Institutional Readiness" Lens?
We have moved away from value-based "Good Governance" (e.g., Tanner et al. 2009) toward a **Readiness-based functional framework** because:
1.  **Actionability:** It identifies specific "Functional Pillars" (Departments) that Mayors and Governors actually manage.
2.  **Data Alignment:** It aligns with the **LPA (Local Performance Assessment)** and other Thai administrative audits, which are structured as "readiness" checks (Do you have the plan? Is the budget active? Are the staff trained?).
3.  **The Governance Multiplier:** It treats Governance as the **Institutional Domain** of the SETS framework—the primary lever that determines if Social, Ecological, and Technological assets can actually be activated.

---

## 2. Frozen Structure: The 6 Pillars of Institutional Readiness

For the `governance_pillar` column, we adopt these 6 canonical functions:

1.  **Planning & Strategy (The Blueprint):** Integration of resilience into mandates and long-term visions.
2.  **Finance & Procurement (The Fuel):** The flow and velocity of budget for resilience and emergency response.
3.  **Coordination & Partnerships (The Connective Tissue):** Multi-stakeholder collaboration and transboundary (cross-scale) management.
4.  **Service Delivery & Ops (The Interface):** The front-line performance and responsiveness of climate-relevant services.
5.  **Data & Digital Governance (The Intelligence):** The systems that enable risk-informed decisions and community agency.
6.  **HR & Institutional Capacity (The Muscle):** The technical skills, training, and headcount required to execute.

## 4. Concept definitions (v3)

This section summarises, for each v3 indicator concept, how the literature frames the underlying capacity. It is a synthesis over the detailed extractions in [`ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRI/inbox_source/Urban_Resilience_Concept_Check.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRI/inbox_source/Urban_Resilience_Concept_Check.md) (Q1–Q6) and is intended to guide indicator vetting and interpretation.

### 4.1 Planning & Strategy

- **Plan revision cycle** — The frequency and quality of updating core plans (climate/DRM, land use, development) in light of new risk information, demographic change, and learning from past events. A high-capacity system institutionalises iterative review, monitoring, and post‑event learning rather than treating plans as static documents.
- **Risk‑informed planning** — The degree to which multi‑hazard risk assessments and vulnerability diagnostics are embedded in land‑use, infrastructure, and investment planning. This includes using hazard maps and multi‑risk diagnostics to steer siting, zoning, and capital budgeting.
- **Policy Integration** — The strength of cross‑sectoral and multi‑level coordination mechanisms that align climate resilience objectives across departments and tiers of government. Integration is reflected in joint strategies, shared data platforms, and participatory processes that break sectoral silos.

### 4.2 Finance & Procurement

- **Emergency budget flow** — The speed, flexibility, and reliability with which funds can be mobilised and disbursed during and after shocks to protect basic services and support recovery. This includes contingency reserves, disaster funds, flexible budget lines, and risk‑transfer instruments.
- **Climate budget tagging** — The existence and use of budget classifications or tagging systems that identify climate‑relevant spending (adaptation and mitigation). This enables tracking, reporting, and reallocating public finance toward climate‑resilient investments over time.
- **Asset Mobilization Velocity** *(design placeholder)* — Conceptually, the speed at which public, private, and community assets (equipment, facilities, supplies) can be mobilised and deployed to front‑line response. Formal definitions and measurement patterns are **pending additional evidence** (for example Consensus.ai or sector‑specific studies).
- **Resource allocation scale** — The magnitude and coverage of financial resources dedicated to resilience and emergency functions relative to the overall budget and risk profile. It captures both the existence of dedicated reserves and the share of spending plausibly related to resilience and disaster management.

### 4.3 Coordination & Partnerships

- **Multi‑stakeholder collab.** — The density and quality of structured interactions between government, private sector, academia, civil society, and vulnerable communities (for example Shared Learning Dialogues). High capacity is reflected in institutionalised mechanisms that surface diverse knowledge and jointly steer resilience priorities.
- **Distributed Command Agility** *(design placeholder)* — Conceptually, the ability of leadership and command functions to decentralise, delegate, and re‑route decision‑making authority during crises (for example SOPs for local delegation). Specific empirical measures are **pending** until supporting literature is added.
- **Transboundary Feedbacks** *(design placeholder)* — Conceptually, the ability of governance arrangements to recognise and manage cross‑jurisdictional and cross‑scale feedbacks (for example upstream–downstream watershed effects). Concrete definitions and metrics are **pending** additional sources.
- **Formal coordination bodies** — The existence and mandate of formal cross‑departmental working groups, taskforces, or coordination bodies (for example inter‑office climate risk groups, resilience committees) that steer resilience strategy and link fragmented departments.

### 4.4 Service Delivery & Ops

- **Service delivery timeliness** — The rapidity with which essential climate‑relevant services (emergency response, utilities, social protection) can respond to and recover from disruptions. It covers both response time (time to first action) and recovery time (time to restore acceptable service levels).
- **Case throughput rate** *(partially evidenced)* — The rate at which complaints, incidents, or service requests (for example Traffy Fondue cases) are processed and resolved. While throughput is discussed in transport and service literature, the exact label and operationalisation in this corpus are limited; this concept should be used cautiously and refined as new sources are added.
- **Digital Social Mediation** *(design placeholder)* — Conceptually, the use of digital platforms, social media, and peer‑to‑peer tools to match needs and resources (for example crowdsourced aid‑matching). Formal definitions and metrics (reach, latency, inclusiveness) are **pending** explicit literature.
- **Infrastructure robustness** — The capacity of physical assets and networks (transport, energy, water, social infrastructure) to maintain function or fail safely under shocks. It is typically assessed through structural design standards, performance under stress, and the presence of protective and redundancy measures.

### 4.5 Data & Digital Governance

- **Data interoperability** — The degree to which data can be shared, integrated, and used across departments and sectors through interoperable systems, shared standards, and formal data‑sharing agreements. High capacity implies whole‑of‑government platforms and cross‑sector digital infrastructures that support shared situational awareness.
- **Digital climate services** — The deployment of digital tools (early warning systems, digital twins, real‑time dashboards, geo‑tagged data platforms) to monitor, predict, and communicate climate‑related risks. It reflects the maturity of data pipelines, analytical capabilities, and citizen‑facing channels.
- **Risk Assessment depth** — The breadth and sophistication of risk diagnostics, moving from single‑hazard, technical studies toward multi‑risk, participatory assessments that incorporate cascading impacts, systemic fragilities, and institutional constraints.

### 4.6 HR & Institutional Capacity

- **Emergency staff capacity** — The availability, distribution, and competencies of personnel required to manage crises (emergency response teams, technical staff, specialised health personnel). It captures both headcount and institutional arrangements (dedicated teams, cross‑functional groups).
- **Staff Training frequency** *(partially evidenced)* — The regularity and coverage of training programmes related to resilience, risk management, and emergency operations. Current evidence is indirect (for example after‑action learning and capability indices); explicit training‑frequency metrics should be locked in only when clear source support is available.
