# CRI Public Hearing 1 — Implementation and Governance Signals

## 1. Purpose

This note captures **how agencies expect to use CRI**, the **enabling conditions** they need, and the implications for **implementation, governance and capacity-building**. It complements the methodology-focused note [`CRI_Public_Hearing1_Methodology_Implications.md`](CRI_Public_Hearing1_Methodology_Implications.md).

## 2. Use-case inventory

### 2.1 Planning and budgeting

Agencies expect to use CRI to:

- Prioritise provinces and districts for **adaptation and disaster-risk-reduction investment**.
- Identify **which capacity tiers** (Coping / Adaptive / Transformative) require strengthening in each territory.
- Justify **budget proposals and project selection** using transparent, evidence-based criteria.

### 2.2 Programme design and monitoring

- Design targeted programmes (e.g. early warning upgrades, planning reforms, social protection) in areas with specific capacity weaknesses.
- Track whether investments lead to **improvements in capacity profiles** over time.
- Use CRI as part of **adaptation M&E** (e.g. for national adaptation plans or sectoral strategies).

### 2.3 Reporting and city-network commitments

- Support reporting under:
  - National climate-change and adaptation strategies.
  - City-network commitments (e.g. C40, ICLEI), which require climate risk/resilience metrics.
- Provide common language and numerics for **multi-level governance** discussions between local, provincial and national actors.

### 2.4 Private sector, finance and banks

- Listed companies and banks signalled interest in using CRI as a **public reference for physical climate risk**, aligned with IFRS/ISSB and TCFD-style disclosure.
- Potential uses include:
  - Screening locations for high-risk projects.
  - Portfolio-level physical-risk analysis by province/district.
  - Engaging with clients on resilience investments in high-risk/high-capacity-gap areas.

## 3. Data governance implications

### 3.1 Forms, provenance and update cycles

Hearing 1 surfaced several governance needs:

- **Form design:** Many current forms for disaster damage, relief claims and administrative assessments are not designed for downstream analytics.
  - Required action: gradually **revise forms and digital workflows** to capture key attributes (location, asset type, vulnerable groups, process performance) in structured form.
- **Provenance and traceability:** Agencies need clarity on **who collects what, at which level, and how it is aggregated**.
  - Required action: maintain a clear **data lineage register** for each CRI indicator and dataset, including known limitations.
- **Update cycles:** CRI will only be credible if **update cycles** are predictable.
  - Required action: align CRI refresh cycles with existing update rhythms (e.g. annual LPA, budget cycles, disaster reporting).

### 3.2 Access tiers and openness

- Stakeholders identified two broad user groups:
  - **Policy and implementation users** (maps, profiles, narrative briefs).
  - **Technical users** (researchers, data teams, banks) needing structured data (e.g. GIS, CSV, APIs).
- Governance signal:
  - Define clear **access tiers**, balancing openness with privacy and political sensitivities.
  - Ensure a **public baseline** (maps, high-level tables) while enabling deeper access for technical partners under appropriate agreements.

## 4. Capacity-building needs

Hearing 1 highlighted that CRI will only be usable if accompanied by **targeted capacity-building**:

- **Who needs training?**
  - Provincial and local planning/budget teams.
  - Disaster management units.
  - Data and GIS teams in key ministries and municipalities.
  - Selected private-sector and financial-sector users.

- **What needs to be covered?**
  - Interpretation of impact maps vs capacity profiles.
  - Understanding the **two-speed measurement** and **data-richness/confidence** concept.
  - Linking CRI outputs to planning/budget processes (e.g. how to translate capacity gaps into concrete project proposals).
  - Basics of GIS and data handling for those using shape files and APIs.

- **How should it be delivered?**
  - Combination of **workshops, online modules, short guides** and helpdesk-style support.
  - Integration into existing **DCCE and DLA training programmes**, rather than creating entirely new standalone initiatives.

## 5. Tooling expectations

### 5.1 Dashboards, GIS, Excel and APIs

Stakeholders expressed clear expectations that CRI should be available through:

- **Web-based dashboards** hosted by DCCE (or the relevant data centre), with map and chart views.
- **GIS-ready layers** (shapefiles / GeoJSON) for provinces, districts and (when safe) tambons.
- **Excel/CSV downloads** for integration into planning and budgeting workflows.
- **APIs** for technical users (e.g. banks, researchers, analytics units) to query CRI scores programmatically.

### 5.2 Integration with existing systems

- CRI should align with and, where possible, plug into:
  - DCCE data centre and dashboards.
  - DLA/LPA data systems.
  - National planning and budgeting platforms.
  - Sectoral information systems (water, agriculture, transport, etc.).

## 6. Quick wins and dependencies

### 6.1 Quick wins

From Hearing 1, the following are realistic quick wins:

- **Deliver an initial set of provincial/district capacity profiles and impact maps**, even if some components are marked as low-confidence or data gaps.
- **Provide a simple “reading guide”** that provincial teams can immediately use to discuss their profile internally.
- **Publish guidance on how CRI can support planning and budgeting**, with a small set of case examples.

### 6.2 Dependencies

- Progress on the above depends on:
  - Completing the **Phase 1 data-quality audit and gap flagging**.
  - Finalising the **capacity-tagging dictionary and scoring**.
  - Agreement on **spatial resolution and update cycles** with DCCE and key partners.
  - Resource allocation for **dashboard/GIS/API implementation** within DCCE’s data infrastructure.
