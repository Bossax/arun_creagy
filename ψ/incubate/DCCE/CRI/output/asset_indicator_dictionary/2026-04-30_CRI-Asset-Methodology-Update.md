# Methodology Update: Pivot to Service-Oriented Asset Indicators

evidence [[2026-04-30-restructuring-asset-indicators]]

## 1. The Fundamental Flaw of the Original Framing
The original framing of CRI Asset Indicators (Version 1) was anchored in the academic **SETS (Social-Ecological-Technological Systems)** framework. While theoretically robust, forensic auditing and stakeholder feedback (recorded in **E-CRI-040**) revealed a critical structural flaw: **Operational Invisible Silos.**

*   **Academic vs. Operational Mismatch**: SETS categorizes assets by their "nature" (e.g., a tree is Ecological, a pipe is Technological). However, urban resilience functions are inherently cross-domain. Forcing a local official to split "Stormwater Pipes" from "Green Retention Ponds" into different academic buckets creates an unnecessary cognitive and administrative burden.
*   **Linguistic Alienation**: Terms like "Ecological Stock" and "Form-Function Stock" do not exist in the vocabulary of Thai Local Administrative Organizations (อปท.). This creates an "adoption wall" where the people responsible for maintaining the assets cannot find them in the index.
*   **The Mismatch Problem**: By separating assets from the governance processes that activate them, v1 often missed the **SFW Nexus** (Solid Waste, Flood, and Wastewater), leading to a risk where high hardware scores (e.g., more pumps) masked operational vulnerabilities (e.g., pumps clogged by uncollected waste).

## 2. Focus on Local Interpretation and Action
The primary goal of this pivot is to ensure that the **Climate Resilience Index is an Actionable Diagnostic tool**, not just a research artifact.

*   **Departmental Mandates**: The new structure groups assets by **Public Service Mission (ภารกิจ)**. This aligns with Thai local government budget lines and mandates. An official (e.g., Director of Public Works) reading the index should see their own job description and departmental KPIs reflected in the indicators.
*   **Operational Thai Language**: All concepts are being renamed into "Official-Facing" Thai (e.g., renaming "Ecological cooling stock" to "พื้นที่สีเขียวและร่มไม้เพื่อลดความร้อน" or "Green areas and shade for heat reduction").
*   **The Hardware/Software Bridge**: We maintain the **M = A × G** (Resilience = Asset × Governance) formula. Local actors can now clearly see that their "Hardware" (e.g., a drainage network) is only as good as the "Software" (e.g., the maintenance budget and drain-clearing routines) that activates it across the **3 Resilience Capacities** (Absorptive, Adaptive, Transformative).

## 3. Summary of the Pivot: Service-First Framing
CRI is pivoting from an **Academic Taxonomy (SETS)** to a **Functional Service System** architecture.

*   **SETS as a "Lens," not a "Bucket"**: We no longer group indicators *by* SETS. Instead, we group them by *Service*, and use SETS as a completeness check within each service (e.g., "In the Water Defense service, do we have a mix of Technological and Ecological assets?").
*   **The 6 Proposed Systems**:
    1.  **Urban Cooling & Health**: Mitigating heat and ensuring thermal safety.
    2.  **Integrated Water & Disaster Defense**: Flood routing and coastal protection.
    3.  **Basic Utility & Mobility Continuity**: Maintaining power, water, and transport lifelines.
    4.  **Local Economy & Emergency Finance**: Fiscal buffers and supply chain security.
    5.  **Community Capability & Social Safety Nets**: Social trust, volunteers, and early warning.
    6.  **Waste & Environmental Hygiene**: Preventing systemic failure through maintenance and hygiene.

## 4. Ongoing Investigation: Establishing the New Grouping Structure
We are currently in a "Research Hardening" phase to ensure the new 6-system structure is canonical and evidence-based.

*   **International Benchmarking**: We are cross-checking the proposed systems against **ISO 37123 (Indicators for Resilient Cities)** and the **City Resilience Framework (CRF)** to ensure no critical resilience domains are missed.
*   **The SFW Nexus Evidence**: We are specifically documenting the physical link between **Solid Waste Management (SWM)** and flood risk. Research shows waste-clogging can reduce drainage capacity by up to 50%, justifying "Waste" as a standalone resilience system in urban climate adaptation.
*   **NotebookLM Deep Research**: We are utilizing the **"Urban Resilience - Infrastructure and Assets"** notebook to extract further options for grouping functional urban services, ensuring our structure remains faithful to the global SETS literature while being optimized for Thai local action.
