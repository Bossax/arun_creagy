## 1. Introduction

Urban climate resilience research has evolved to emphasize the importance of measuring and managing the physical, natural, and social 'stocks'—the capitals and assets—that underpin a city's ability to withstand, adapt to, and transform in response to climate shocks. The Social-Ecological-Technological Systems (SETS) framework has become central in distinguishing static assets (stocks) from dynamic processes (flows), highlighting the need for multidimensional indicators that capture not just aggregate volumes but also accessibility, spatial distribution, and system interdependencies (Sharifi, 2023; Grimm et al., 2018; Muñoz-Erickson et al., 2021; Markolf et al., 2018). Recent literature identifies a wide array of stock indicators across physical infrastructure (e.g., energy grids, water systems), natural assets (e.g., green spaces, biodiversity), and social capital (e.g., community networks, institutional capacity) (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2022; Sharifi & Yamagata, 2016; Zhang et al., 2020). These are mapped onto resilience capacity layers—coping, adaptive, and transformative—each requiring distinct metrics for effective governance (Mehryar et al., 2022; Lowe et al., 2024; Turchi et al., 2023). However, critiques persist regarding over-reliance on static infrastructure leading to 'resilience lock-in' or obsolescence thresholds that undermine long-term adaptability (Buzási & Csizovszky, 2022; Hung et al., 2024; Markolf et al., 2018). This review synthesizes global frameworks and empirical studies to curate a taxonomic inventory of urban resilience stock indicators, analyze their operationalization within SETS-informed approaches, and critically evaluate their application across the resilience capacity spectrum.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Taxonomic Inventory of Stock Indicators

Urban climate resilience literature consistently categorizes stock indicators into three main domains: physical/built capital (infrastructure robustness/redundancy), natural/ecological capital (green space area/biodiversity), and social/institutional capital (community networks/governance structures) (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2022; Sharifi & Yamagata, 2016; Zhang et al., 2020). Table-based inventories frequently include:

- **Physical:** Energy supply diversity/redundancy; water/wastewater infrastructure; emergency shelters; transportation network redundancy; building density; critical facility coverage (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Al-Humaiqani & Al‐Ghamdi, 2022; Zhang et al., 2020).
- **Natural:** Urban green space per capita; tree canopy cover; biodiversity indices; soil/water body presence; ecosystem connectivity (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2022).
- **Social:** Socio-demographic structure; civil society engagement; healthcare access; knowledge/risk competence; municipal budget reserves (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2022; Lowe et al., 2024).

### 3.2 SETS Framework Distinctions: Stocks vs. Flows

SETS-based approaches explicitly differentiate between static stocks (e.g., infrastructure assets or green spaces) and dynamic flows/processes (e.g., energy throughput or information exchange). Stocks represent the structural floor—the minimum baseline required for administrative action—while flows capture system adaptability under stress (Sharifi, 2023; Grimm et al., 2018; Muñoz-Erickson et al., 2021). For example:

- Access to hospitals = stock indicator;
- Surge capacity during disaster = flow/process indicator (Oza et al., 2025).

### 3.3 Mapping Indicators Across Resilience Capacity Layers

**Coping Capacity:** Metrics focus on immediate buffers—redundant energy/water systems, emergency financial reserves, survival infrastructure such as shelters or early warning systems (Mehryar et al., 2022; Al-Humaiqani & Al‐Ghamdi, 2022; Turchi et al., 2023). **Adaptive Capacity:** Emphasizes asset diversity (multiple water sources), economic redundancy (alternative livelihoods), latent social capital (community organizations/networks), flexible land use policies (Zeng et al., 2022; Turchi et al., 2023; Chapagain et al., 2025). **Transformative Capacity:** Indicators include institutional readiness for reorganization (multi-stakeholder governance platforms), land-use flexibility indices, transition plans for fixed assets/infrastructure repurposing (Lowe et al., 2024; Turchi et al., 2023; Sousa et al., 2023).

### 3.4 Asset Presence vs. Accessibility/Spatial Distribution

Recent studies critique aggregate volume measures alone as insufficient. Instead:

- Accessibility/spatial distribution of green spaces or healthcare facilities is prioritized as an equity/social justice metric (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Chang et al., 2021).
- Spatially explicit indices reveal clusters of vulnerability where stocks exist but are inaccessible due to socio-economic or geographic barriers (Chang et al., 2021).

### 3.5 Lock-In & Obsolescence Indicators

Indicators used to detect 'resilience lock-in' or obsolescence include:

- Proportion of budget allocated to maintenance vs. innovation;
- Degree of infrastructural modularity/flexibility;
- Evidence of path dependency in land use or technology adoption;
- Frequency of maladaptive outcomes following heavy investment in static infrastructure without adaptive pathways (Buzási & Csizovszky, 2022; Hung et al., 2024; Markolf et al., 2018).

## 4. Discussion

The reviewed literature demonstrates significant progress toward multidimensional inventories of urban climate resilience stocks but highlights persistent challenges in operationalizing these concepts within policy and practice. SETS frameworks provide a robust theoretical basis for distinguishing between static assets ('structural floor') and dynamic processes ('flows'), yet empirical applications often lag behind conceptual advances (Sharifi, 2023; Grimm et al., 2018). Many measurement tools remain focused on coping/adaptive capacities with limited attention to transformative potential—a gap that risks reinforcing status quo vulnerabilities rather than enabling systemic change (Mehryar et al., 2022; Lowe et al., 2024). The shift from aggregate volume metrics toward accessibility/spatial distribution is crucial for addressing equity concerns but requires improved data integration across sectors (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Chang et al., 2021). Finally, the identification of lock-in/obsolescence thresholds remains underdeveloped: while some studies propose modularity/flexibility indices or track maintenance-to-innovation ratios as proxies for system rigidity/maladaptation risk (Buzási & Csizovszky, 2022; Hung et al., 2024), standardized metrics are lacking.

## 5. Conclusion

Urban climate resilience scholarship has advanced toward comprehensive taxonomies of physical/natural/social stock indicators using SETS-informed approaches that distinguish static assets from dynamic processes. While inventories now span all three capacity layers—coping buffers through transformative readiness—the field continues to grapple with standardizing metrics for structural floors and detecting lock-in/obsolescence risks associated with inflexible infrastructures.

### Open Research Questions

A major future direction involves developing standardized metrics for structural floors across diverse urban contexts while integrating spatial equity considerations into all layers of capacity assessment.
