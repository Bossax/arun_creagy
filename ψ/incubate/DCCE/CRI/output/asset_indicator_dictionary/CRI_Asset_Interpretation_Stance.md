# CRI Asset Interpretation Stance

## 1. Executive Summary: The Strategic Pivot

In response to trigger **T-CRI-006**, the Climate Risk Index (CRI) adopts a **Hybrid Asset-Process Posture**. While the core of Phase 2 remains focused on **Institutional Readiness (Governance Processes)**, we re-introduce asset-based indicators as a "Conceptual Bridge." This stance ensures that stakeholders with a static/readiness mindset are provided with familiar physical and natural foundations, which are then explicitly linked to the dynamic governance processes required to activate them.

## 2. Theoretical Foundation: The Hardware-Software Metaphor

We define the relationship between assets and resilience through the **SETS-I (Social-Ecological-Technological-Institutional)** lens, utilizing the **Hardware vs. Software** metaphor:

- **Assets as Hardware (Structural Potential):** Physical infrastructure, natural buffers, and social stocks represent the "Hardware" or **Potential Energy ($P$)** of a system. They define the maximum action space available but do not constitute resilience in isolation.
- **Governance as Software (Activation Logic):** Institutional processes, planning, and coordination represent the "Software." Resilience is only realized when the software successfully activates the hardware to provide a **Service Flow ($S$)**.

## 3. Analytical Logic: From Stock to Service

To maintain analytic rigor, all asset indicators must be interpreted through the following three diagnostic ratios:

1.  **The Mismatch Index ($M = A - G$):**
    - Identifies jurisdictions where Asset growth ($A$) outpaces Governance capacity ($G$). 
    - **High $M$** signals a "Fragility Anchor" (Risk of Systemic Failure or White Elephants).
2.  **The Activation Ratio ($Q = S/P$):**
    - Measures how much of the Potential embedded in the stock is actually realized as a service.
    - **Interpretation:** Resilience is not the stock ($P$), but the efficiency of the activation ($Q$).
3.  **Non-Linear Thresholds:**
    - We acknowledge that the relationship between assets and resilience is **non-linear**. Assets are "enabling conditions" up to a threshold; beyond that point, without governance, they transition from benefits to **maladaptive liabilities (lock-in)**.

## 4. Safe Interpretation Guardrails (Mandatory)

To prevent the "Ranking Trap" and wealth bias, the following guardrails are mandatory for all curated asset indicators:

### Guardrail 1: Mitigating Wealth Bias (Hazard-Exposure Normalization)
Asset stocks often correlate with municipal wealth. To ensure equity, assets must be **normalized against hazard exposure**. 
- *Rule:* Instead of "Total Drainage Length," we use "Drainage Coverage relative to the 1-in-50-year Flood Footprint."

### Guardrail 2: Detecting Lock-in & Obsolescence
We must flag assets that create path dependencies.
- *Rule:* Assets with high CAPEX but low modularity (e.g., rigid sea walls) must be interpreted with a "Lock-in Warning" if they shrink the future solution space for adaptive migration.

### Guardrail 3: SETS Domain Mapping
Assets are categorized across the three non-institutional domains to complement the Institutional (I) framework:
- **Technological Stocks:** Built infrastructure, energy grids, digital hardware.
- **Ecological Stocks:** Natural capital, wetlands, green-blue connectivity.
- **Social Stocks:** Community facilities, human capital, demographic resilience.

## 5. Implementation Stance: Assets as Enabling Conditions

When communicating results, assets shall be presented as **"Resilience Foundations"**:

- **Low Asset / High Governance:** "High Efficiency, Low Ceiling." The system is well-managed but structurally capped. *Recommendation: Capital Investment.*
- **High Asset / Low Governance:** "High Potential, High Risk." The system has the hardware but lacks the operating software. *Recommendation: Institutional Strengthening.*
