
Here are three distinct frameworks for grouping urban infrastructure and assets into functional systems, exactly as presented in the sources:

**1) Perspective Name: Critical Urban Systems**

- **Candidate service-system categories:**
    - Water supply
    - Food supply
    - Supporting ecosystems (that manage runoff, flood control, or food production),
    - Energy
    - Transport
    - Shelter
    - Communications
- **Evidence:** "A framework for urban climate resilience.pdf"

**2) Perspective Name: Color-Coded Ecological & Engineered Infrastructure**

- **Candidate service-system categories:**
    - Gray infrastructure (centralized, built-engineered infrastructure like pipes, roads, and storage facilities),
    - Green infrastructure (terrestrial vegetation)
    - Blue infrastructure (aquatic systems)
    - Turquoise infrastructure (wetlands)
    - Brown infrastructure (vacant, unvegetated land)
- **Evidence:** "A social-ecological-technological systems framework for urban ecosystem services.pdf"

**3) Perspective Name: Comprehensive Wealth Asset Portfolio**

- **Candidate service-system categories:**
    - Produced capital (e.g., machinery, urban land, property, roads),
    - Nonrenewable natural capital (e.g., fossil fuels, minerals, metals),
    - Renewable natural capital (e.g., agricultural land, forests, marine fish stocks, mangroves, renewable energy),
    - Human capital
    - Net foreign assets
- **Evidence:** "world-bank-chaing-wealth-of-nation.pdf"


**4. The Built Environment Systems Classification**

- **Shelter systems:** Encompasses all building typologies used for housing and accommodation.
- **Life support systems:** Covers the critical utilities necessary for urban survival, such as energy and water supply networks.
- **Movements systems:** Includes all transportation infrastructure, such as roads, bridges, and highways.
- **Open space systems:** Comprises spaces utilized for parks and recreational purposes.

Evidence: Al-Humaiqani, M. M., & Al-Ghamdi, S. G. (2022). _The built environment resilience qualities to climate change impact - Concepts, frameworks, and directions for future research.pdf_.

**5. Strategic Urban Services Framework**

- **Water:** Covers water supply, wastewater, and stormwater services.
- **Waste:** Encompasses solid waste disposal and management.
- **Energy:** Manages power grids and energy infrastructure.
- **Mobility:** Manages transportation and the movement of people and goods across the city.
- **Natural areas:** Uniquely integrated as a formal "strategic service" due to their vital role in providing ecosystem services, supporting health, and mitigating climate impacts,.

Evidence: Russo, B., et al. (2023). _Climate Resilience Assessment in Regions, Cities, Strategic Services, and Critical Infrastructure_Implementation and Outcomes.pdf_.

** 6. Core Critical Urban Systems**

- **Water and food supply:** Essential provisioning networks required for basic urban survival.
- **Ecosystems:** Natural systems (both local and remote) that support provisioning and deliver key services, such as food production, runoff management, or flood control,.
- **Energy and Transport:** Critical infrastructure networks whose failure jeopardizes human well-being and precludes higher-order economic activity.
- **Shelter and Communications:** Essential housing and the communication networks required for coordination and societal connectivity.

Evidence: Tyler, S., & Moench, M. (2012). _A framework for urban climate resilience.pdf_.

**7. The Multi-Dimensional Action Fields Framework**

- **Environment:** Groups natural elements including soil and green spaces, water bodies, biodiversity, and air.
- **Infrastructure:** Encompasses settlement structures, energy, telecommunications, traffic, and drinking/wastewater systems.
- **Economy:** Focuses on functional systems of innovation, business stability, and overall economic structure.
- **Society:** Covers research, healthcare, civil protection, socio-demographic structures, and knowledge/risk competence.
- **Governance:** Groups administrative functions, municipal budgets, strategic planning, and citizen participation.

Evidence: Feldmeyer, D., et al. (2019). _Indicators for Monitoring Urban Climate Change Resilience and Adaptation.pdf_.
