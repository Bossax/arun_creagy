## 2. Impact Index (Phase 1) implications

### 2.1 Fiscal Relief Index framing

Hearing 1 reinforced that **Fiscal Relief** must be clearly differentiated from **total economic loss**:

- Stakeholders repeatedly asked how much of the "real damage" is captured if the numerator relies on **relief and emergency payments**.
- There is a risk that users interpret low relief spending as low risk, when in many cases it may signal **administrative gaps, coverage limits, or reliance on private insurance** (especially in urban/industrial contexts).

Implications for Phase 1 methodology:

- The document should:
  - Emphasise the **fiscal-vulnerability focus** of the economic pillar.
  - Explicitly note that **relief is a policy- and eligibility-limited proxy**, not a direct damage metric.
  - Treat Fiscal Relief consistently as an **index of budgetary strain**, not as a complete loss estimate.

### 2.2 Administrative gap and urban blind spots

Hearing participants confirmed concerns about:

- **Missing or under-reported relief data** in some provinces and years.
- **Urban and industrial areas** where households and firms rely on private insurance or self-finance, and thus rarely appear in state relief records.

Implications for Phase 1 methodology:

- Strengthen the rationale for the **Administrative Gap flag**, noting that hearing stakeholders explicitly endorsed:
  - Treating `hazard = true` + `relief = 0` as a **data gap**, not as evidence of low risk.
  - Avoiding ranking provinces as low risk where satellite hazard and exposure show clear threats but relief is absent.
- Reinforce the use of **exposure proxies for urban/industrial risk**, not just agricultural relief series.

## 3. Capacity Index (Phase 2) implications

### 3.1 Capacity tiers and SES framing

Hearing 1 validated the **three-tier capacity model (Coping / Adaptive / Transformative)** and its SES framing, with caveats:

- Participants appreciated the conceptual clarity but questioned:
  - Which **Transformative** indicators are realistically observable at local level.
  - Whether some Transformative signals (e.g. structural reforms, major zoning changes) should be treated as **national or provincial-level** rather than tambon-level indicators.

Methodology implications:

- Clarify in Phase 2 methodology that:
  - Transformative capacity in Phase 2 will be **lightweight and selective**, focusing on a few structural signals that can be derived from existing administrative or planning data.
  - Where Transformative capacity cannot be reliably quantified, it may be **presented qualitatively** or as a separate narrative layer, not forced into composite scores.

### 3.2 Baseline vs target metrics and data-richness

Stakeholders expressed concern that LPA-style indicators and checklists might be over-interpreted as performance evidence.

Methodology implications:

- Phase 2 methodology should:
  - Reiterate that Phase 2 capacity scores are **baseline proxies for structural readiness**, tagged with **data-richness/confidence (0–3)**.
  - Emphasise that **target process-quality metrics** (e.g. drill quality, enforcement of zoning) are intentionally documented but not yet available at scale.
  - Use Hearing 1 feedback as evidence that this **two-speed measurement stance** is not just a technical choice but a stakeholder requirement.

### 3.3 Spatial resolution and aggregation rules

Hearing participants discussed the trade-offs between Tambon, District and Provincial resolution.

Methodology implications:

- Document in Phase 2 methodology:
  - A default **district-level publication resolution**, with clear conditions under which tambon-level or aggregated provincial-level outputs will be produced.
  - A statement that **aggregation rules** (how tambon-level indicators roll up to district/province) will prioritise **interpretability and auditability** over maximal granularity.

## 4. Data and indicator system implications

### 4.1 Form and registry upgrades

Hearing 1 highlighted that current **damage/relief forms and administrative registers** are not always designed for analytical use.

Implications for both phases:

- CRI methodology documents should:
  - Note that some desired indicators (e.g. disaggregated loss by asset type, coverage of vulnerable groups) are **blocked by current form design**.
  - Position CRI as a driver for **data system upgrades** (forms, digital systems, registries) that would benefit both CRI and broader adaptation M&E.

### 4.2 Evidence governance

- The hearing emphasised the importance of transparency in **data lineage, limitations and uses**.

Methodology implications:

- Ensure that both Phase 1 and Phase 2 documents:
  - Reference the **CRI Evidence Registry** and **Evidence Coverage Map** as living instruments.
  - Use **E-CRI-###** ids (starting with E-CRI-010–E-CRI-015) in notes when citing hearing-derived evidence.

## 5. Recommended edits (by document)

### 5.1 CRI Phase 1 Methodology

Add a short subsection (recommended under **Section 6. Data Quality & Limitations**) that:

- Re-states the **Fiscal Relief Index** framing as a fiscal-vulnerability metric.
- Summarises hearing feedback on:
  - Limitations of compensation-based proxies and relief eligibility rules.
  - The need for Administrative Gap flags and urban exposure proxies.
- Cross-references hearing evidence via ids that will be registered as **E-CRI-013** and **E-CRI-014**.

### 5.2 CRI Phase 2 Methodology

Add a subsection (e.g. **"Validated by Public Hearing 1"**) that:

- Lists key stances validated by stakeholders:
  - Profile over ranking.
  - Multi-score capacity profiles.
  - Data-richness / two-speed measurement.
  - Separation of impact vs capacity.
- Clarifies how **Transformative capacity** will be treated cautiously in Phase 2 (lightweight, selective, and sometimes qualitative).
- References the same hearing evidence ids once registered.
