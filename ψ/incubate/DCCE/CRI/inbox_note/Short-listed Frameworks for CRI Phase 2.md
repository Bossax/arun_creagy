### **(a) Extraction: Literature Review Notes**

**Section 1: Capacity-Based, Governance-Oriented Frameworks**

- **Atomic Note 1: Agent Capacities within Social-Ecological Systems (SES)**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2012. Shifted focus away from rigid "predict and prevent" physical infrastructure models toward measuring the dynamic abilities of human systems.
    - **Extraction:** The Urban Climate Resilience Framework defines core dimensions as "infrastructure and ecosystems (both captured under the rubric of ‘systems’), social agents and institutions" and measures agent capacities through "Responsiveness... Resourcefulness... Capacity to learn" (Tyler & Moench, 2012). It was applied to "resilience planning activities undertaken in 10 cities across Asia" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** This establishes a foundational theoretical structure for assessing how local actors (agents) interact with physical infrastructure (systems) under specific legal/cultural rules (institutions).
- **Atomic Note 2: Good Urban Governance Dimensions**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2009. Reacted against the exclusion of poor urban populations in climate adaptation by centering political and institutional attributes as the primary drivers of resilience.
    - **Extraction:** Defines the capacity to deliver resilience through five governance dimensions: "(1) decentralisation and autonomy, (2) accountability and transparency, (3) responsiveness and flexibility, (4) participation and inclusion and (5) experience and support" (Tanner et al., 2009). The framework was utilized for "rapid climate resilience assessments conducted in ten Asian cities" (Tanner et al., 2009).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** These five parameters provide a direct, testable taxonomy for evaluating the institutional strength of provincial governments.
- **Atomic Note 3: Action-Oriented Institutional Capacities**
    - **Chronological Context:** Post-2020. Developed in response to the COVID-19 pandemic to measure cities' institutional agility rather than static vulnerability.
    - **Extraction:** The Strategy& Urban Resilience Assessment Framework evaluates the "institutional capacity to respond, recover, and transform based on their available strategies, governance, capabilities, data and systems, delivery of services, and financing" (Strategy&, n.d.). It assesses the ability to "Respond, Recover, and Transform" across "nine MENA cities and 11 comparators from around the world" (Strategy&, n.d.).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** Tagging administrative data into "Respond, Recover, Transform" capacity buckets offers a highly practical method for building an action-oriented provincial index.

**Section 2: Successful Use of Administrative Data**

- **Atomic Note 4: Empirical Moderation via Municipal Administrative Data**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2021. Advanced the field by shifting from qualitative case studies to large-N quantitative modeling of municipal administrative records.
    - **Extraction:** Evaluated local government adaptation outcomes "using data from 345 Chilean municipalities over a nine-year period" utilizing strictly administrative sources such as "Annual public accounts of the mayors... Municipal budgets... Staff (studies, functions, remuneration)... Municipal ordinances and by-laws... Transparency scores" (Valdivieso et al., 2021). Results proved that "municipal organizational robustness—operational rules, planning, managerial flexibility and integration, and accountability—is the most quantitatively outstanding moderating factor" in driving investment (Valdivieso et al., 2021).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** Validates that standard public administrative records (budgets, staff counts, meeting minutes) are robust, sufficient data sources for modeling actual resilience governance.

- **Atomic Note 5: Verifiable Secondary Data for Global Benchmarking**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2025. Reacted against perception-based or survey-heavy ESG rankings to create a standardized, objective baseline for comparing systemic readiness.
    - **Extraction:** The Global Cities Resilience Index (GCRI) relies entirely on "quantifiable, verifiable data from established sources rather than survey responses, expert opinions... or subjective evaluations" sourced from groups like the World Bank and UN, applying this to "31 cities" (Kearney, 2025).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** Demonstrates that a scalable, comparative index can and should be built entirely on existing secondary statistical and administrative databases without requiring new primary data collection.

**Section 3: Design Patterns for Provincial-Scale Indices**

- **Atomic Note 6: Regional Scale and Categorical Aggregation**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2026. Expanded the prior RESCCUE city-level framework to address regional ecosystems and cross-municipal interdependencies.
    - **Extraction:** The ICARIA Holistic Resilience Assessment Framework measures regional systems across "organizational, spatial, functional, and physical" dimensions, synthesizing administrative "models, plans, monitoring activities, risk assessment, or city incident records" (Brito et al., 2026). It avoids single scores, stating it "is not intended to produce a single global resilience indicator" but instead reports metrics on a categorical gradient of "incipient (graded 0 to 1), progressing (1 to 2), or advanced (2 to 3)" (Brito et al., 2026).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** Structuring a provincial index to output categorical development levels (incipient vs. advanced) rather than a single numerical rank prevents the "ranking trap" and provides clearer diagnostic guidance.

- **Atomic Note 7: Methodological Weighting for Data Quality and Scale**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2024–2025. Addressed inherent biases in secondary data collection across unequal geographies and administrative capacities.
    - **Extraction:** To normalize comparisons, the ESI Climate Resilience Index ensures "Metrics measuring cumulative values, such as total emissions for a city, were standardized using city population to reduce bias against cities with larger populations" (ESI, 2024). Furthermore, the GCRI mitigates missing variables by calculating indicator scores based not just on performance, but multiplied by "Foundational importance" plus "Data richness" (Kearney, 2025).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** Incorporating a mathematical weighting for "data richness" into the index design is essential for fairly evaluating provincial regions that suffer from structural data poverty.

**Section 4: Common Pitfalls and Criticisms**

- **Atomic Note 8: The Technocratic Trap and Mixed-Index Obfuscation**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2022. Reacted against "predict and prevent" engineering models that ignore social justice, and composite indices that bury fatal flaws in weighted averages.
    - **Extraction:** Wardekker (2022) notes that focusing solely on a "‘System Resilience’ frame" (engineering/infrastructure) at the expense of a "‘Community Resilience’ frame" leads to a "dialogue of the deaf" where marginalized vulnerabilities are ignored. Simultaneously, the IDIS framework warns against "the use of indexes mixing non-reducible dimensions," proposing instead strict thresholds where "any policy exceeding a threshold should be rejected or assigned the lowest priority" (Martínez-Fernández et al., 2022).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** A new index must include explicit community/social justice indicators and hard fail-state thresholds to prevent high economic scores from masking severe socio-ecological vulnerabilities.
- **Atomic Note 9: Data Fragmentation at Regional Scales**
    - **Chronological Context:** 2026. Identified operational barriers during the implementation of the ICARIA regional assessment in the Barcelona Metropolitan Area.
    - **Extraction:** The assessment revealed that at a regional level, "knowledge is highly dispersed, requiring the involvement of many stakeholders and good coordination at a regional level" resulting in situations where "many metrics remained unanswered" (Brito et al., 2026).
    - **Implication for downstream analysis:** Index design for provincial scales must assume incomplete data and explicitly design fallback proxy indicators for instances where cross-jurisdictional data sharing fails.

### **(b) Synthesis / Inference**

**1. Which frameworks are conceptually closest to a capacity-based, governance-oriented perspective?**
The **Strategy& Urban Resilience Assessment Framework** and the **Urban Climate Resilience Framework (Tyler & Moench, 2012)** are the closest conceptual matches. Strategy& explicitly organizes its entire 131-KPI index around the institutional _capacities_ to "Respond, Recover, and Transform" in the face of shocks. Tyler & Moench define urban resilience through the dynamic _capacities_ of social agents (responsiveness, resourcefulness, capacity to learn) interacting with institutions. Additionally, the parameters defined by **Tanner et al. (2009)** (decentralization, accountability, transparency, participation) provide the definitive governance-oriented lens for evaluating how political structures moderate these capacities.

**2. Which ones demonstrate successful use of administrative data for resilience assessment?** The study by **Valdivieso et al. (2021)** is the premier example of utilizing administrative data, successfully leveraging municipal budgets, staff records, public accounts, and transparency audits across 345 Chilean municipalities to empirically measure how organizational robustness dictates resilience investment. At the index level, the **Global Cities Resilience Index (GCRI)** (Kearney, 2025) and the **Climate Resilience Index** (ESI, 2024) successfully bypass subjective primary surveys by relying entirely on verifiable administrative data, national statistics, and established secondary databases (e.g., World Bank, CDP reporting) to assess urban readiness.

**3. What design patterns could be adapted into a provincial-scale index similar to CRI?**

- **Domains:** A four-to-five pillar structure is the universal standard. A provincial index should adapt dimensions like those found in ICARIA and GCRI: _Organizational/Governance, Economic/Financial, Social/Human Capital, and Physical/Environmental_.
- **Indicators:** Indicators must rely on verifiable secondary data (GCRI). For a capacity-based approach, these indicators should be mapped into operational phases: the ability to _anticipate/prepare, respond, recover, and transform_ (Strategy&; ICARIA).
- **Aggregation:** To avoid the "ranking trap", the index should adopt ICARIA's _categorical output design_, reporting the percentage of regional services operating at "incipient, progressing, or advanced" levels rather than a single arbitrary score. Additionally, it must utilize the ESI's _per-capita normalization_ to balance rural vs. dense provinces, and adopt the GCRI's _data richness weighting_ to prevent penalizing provinces simply because they have poorer administrative tracking.

**4. What common pitfalls or criticisms should we avoid?**

- **The Technocratic / Engineering Bias:** Avoid framing resilience purely as the physical robustness of infrastructure (System Resilience). As Wardekker (2022) warns, ignoring the "Community Resilience" frame leads to policies that neglect social cohesion and equity.
- **Mixed-Index Obfuscation:** Avoid standard Decision Support Systems that average out all variables into a single score. Martínez-Fernández et al. (2022) warn this mixes "non-reducible dimensions," where a high economic score might hide a fatal environmental vulnerability. Strict pass/fail thresholds should be embedded.
- **Unrealistic Data Expectations at Scale:** When scaling to a province/region, Brito et al. (2026) warn that knowledge becomes highly siloed across disparate municipal and regional stakeholders, leading to unanswered metrics. The index must be designed to tolerate data fragmentation by utilizing proxy indicators and explicitly acknowledging structural data poverty.
