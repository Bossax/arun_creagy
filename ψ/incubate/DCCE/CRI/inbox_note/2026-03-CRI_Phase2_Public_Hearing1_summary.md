# CRI Phase 2 Public Hearing 1 — Summary

## Context

- **Event:** Public hearing on the development framework for Thailand's Climate Risk Index (CRI) Phase 2.
- **Timing:** March 2026 (Hearing 1 / first consultation).
- **Format:** Hybrid (in-person + MS Teams online).
- **Role in project:** First formal methodology consultation for Phase 2, validating the **capacity profile** direction and clarifying expectations for how CRI will be used by agencies.
- **Relationship to phases:**
  - **Phase 1 (Impact / Fiscal Relief Index):** Hearing participants were introduced to the existing impact index and its constraints (historical, fiscal-response based, data gaps).
  - **Phase 2 (Capacity Profiles):** The hearing focused on how Phase 2 will shift emphasis from **risk ranking** toward **Resilience Capacity Profiles** (Coping / Adaptive / Transformative), and how administrative/process indicators can be used within this framing.

## Agenda (high-level)

- Welcome, objectives and overview of the CRI project.
- Introductory session on climate risk concepts (hazard, exposure, vulnerability, impact, risk).
- Recap of Phase 1 CRI and motivation for Phase 2.
- Presentation of Phase 2 design stance:
	- Profile over ranking.
	- Impact vs Capacity separation.
	- Capacity tiers (Coping / Adaptive / Transformative).
	- Two-speed measurement and data-richness overlay.
- Mentimeter interactive questions:
	- Participant background and stakeholder types.
	- Baseline understanding of climate risk.
	- Intended uses of CRI in agencies.
- Open discussion and Q&A on:
	- Spatial resolution (province / district / sub-district).
	- Indicator design and data constraints.
	- How agencies expect to use CRI (planning, budgeting, private sector, banks, city networks).
	- Tooling (maps, Excel, GIS, APIs).
	- Capacity-building and quick wins.

## Participants & stakeholder types (high-level)

- **Central government:** line ministries, DCCE, DLA, NESDC, disaster and planning agencies.
- **Sub-national government:** provincial and local administration (municipalities, LAOs).
- **Knowledge institutions:** universities, research institutes.
- **International partners and networks:** development agencies, city networks (e.g. C40 / ICLEI-type participants via DCCE).
- **Private sector and finance:** selected representatives from listed companies, banks / financial sector, and sectoral associations.
- **Civil society:** NGOs and community-oriented organisations with interest in climate resilience.

Stakeholders varied widely in prior familiarity with climate risk concepts. Early Mentimeter results showed average self-rated understanding around the **mid-range** (roughly "2.5 out of 5"), justifying the introductory teaching segments.

## Narrative summary — Part 1

Part 1 focused on **conceptual grounding** and **Phase 1/2 framing**:

- The facilitation team introduced the purpose of the hearing: to gather feedback and design signals to refine the CRI methodology, not to present a finished product.
- A teaching segment clarified key climate risk concepts:
  - **Risk vs impact:** risk as probability of impact given hazard, exposure and vulnerability; impact as realised damage.
  - **Risk components:** hazard, exposure, vulnerability (including sensitivity and adaptive capacity).
  - Examples were used to illustrate how these pieces combine in practice.
- Participants were asked, via Mentimeter, to rate their understanding of climate risk and to select which definition best matched **climate risk** and **impact**. The exercise was used to:
  - Check baseline understanding.
  - Reinforce formal definitions.
- The team then reintroduced **CRI Phase 1**:
  - Phase 1 used a **backward-looking impact index** (Germanwatch-style), aggregating historical records of deaths, affected people and fiscal relief payments.
  - Limitations of using only historical loss/relief were discussed (climate change non-stationarity, incomplete relief data, administrative gaps, urban blind spots).
  - The Fiscal Relief Index was framed as a **fiscal vulnerability / response intensity** metric, not full economic loss.
- The rationale for **Phase 2** was then presented:
  - Phase 2 will complement the Impact Index with **Resilience Capacity Profiles** grounded in SES and governance/process indicators.
  - Capacity is explicitly decomposed into **Coping, Adaptive, Transformative** tiers.
  - Existing administrative datasets (e.g. LPA, Sustainable City) will be tagged and re-used rather than collecting new nationwide primary data.

## Narrative summary — Part 2

Part 2 transitioned into **design choices, stakeholder requirements and practical constraints**:

- Spatial resolution:
  - Participants debated whether outputs should be produced at **province, district, or sub-district / tambon** level.
  - Many stakeholders expressed that data is often collected at **village / tambon level** but that actionable decision-making and budgeting happens at **district/province** level.
  - The hearing signalled that **district-level as primary unit with the ability to drill-down by tambon where data exists** is acceptable, as long as data quality and form design are aligned.
- Capacity tiers and feasibility:
  - Stakeholders generally supported the idea of reflecting **Coping, Adaptive and Transformative** capacities.
  - However, some raised concerns that **Transformative capacity** may be difficult to measure or act on at tambon level and may sit more naturally at **province or national policy** level.
  - There was a suggestion to prioritise **Coping + Adaptive** for local scoring, while keeping Transformative capacity as a qualitative / higher-level layer.
- Data and indicator design:
  - Participants highlighted that many current disaster-event forms and relief-claim forms are **not designed with analytics in mind**, leading to:
    - Loss of detail between the level where data is captured and the level where it is stored in digital systems.
    - Aggregation that obscures key attributes (e.g. vulnerable groups, asset types).
  - The hearing reinforced the need to **upgrade forms and registries** if CRI is to support forward-looking decision-making (damages, exposure, capacity).
  - Concerns were raised that **capacity indicators** could feel like an "audit" of local authorities; the team clarified that the intent is to **support capacity-building and funding access**, not to punish.
- Use cases and external alignment:
  - Bangkok and other cities flagged that they are already required to report to **international city networks (e.g. C40/Resilient Cities)** and global initiatives, where climate risk and resilience indicators are mandatory.
  - Financial-sector participants pointed to **IFRS/ISSB and TCFD**-style requirements for climate risk disclosure; they expressed interest in using CRI outputs to support **physical risk assessment for assets and portfolios**.
  - There was interest in using CRI as part of:
    - Provincial and municipal development planning.
    - Budget prioritization and project selection.
    - Reporting to networks and international commitments.
    - Bank and investor risk assessments for large projects or portfolios.

## Key points and questions (descriptive)

- **Clarification of CRI purpose**
  - CRI is not a predictive tool that says exactly when/where disasters will occur; it is a **decision-support index** combining impact history and structural capacity to guide targeting and prioritisation.
  - The audience was repeatedly reminded that "Fiscal Relief" is not equal to total loss.

- **Profile vs ranking**
  - There was strong interest in seeing **profiles (multiple scores)** rather than a single ranking:
    - Stakeholders asked for breakdowns by capacity type and possibly by thematic clusters (e.g. planning, finance, infrastructure, social protection).
    - Ranking is still seen as politically useful (e.g. to motivate high-level action), but the risk of misinterpretation was recognised.

- **Indicators and administrative burden**
  - Local authorities warned that some indicators could create **additional reporting burden** if they do not re-use existing data flows (LPA, budget reports, sectoral registries).
  - There was a request that CRI emphasise **re-using existing indicators** where possible and only propose minimal new data collection.

- **Spatial and sectoral blind spots**
  - Participants reiterated concerns about **urban and industrial risk being under-reflected** if the index relies heavily on agricultural relief payments.
  - There was support for using **exposure proxies** and other datasets to fill these blind spots.

## Next steps mentioned in the hearing

- Refine Phase 2 methodology and capacity-tagging rules using the feedback from Hearing 1.
- Select and confirm **pilot provinces / case studies** for deeper ground-truthing of the capacity profile approach.
- Prepare **prototype capacity profiles and Fiscal Relief maps** to present at Hearing 2 / second consultation.
- Develop a concrete proposal for **capacity-building and training** for local users (how to read CRI and how to integrate it into planning/budgeting cycles).
- Explore **data-governance upgrades** (forms, registries, digital systems) that would support both CRI and wider adaptation M&E needs.
