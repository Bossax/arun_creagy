### **(a) Extraction: Chronological Timeline of Urban Resilience

**Pre-2000s: Origins in Engineering and Ecology**

- **What changed:** The foundational concepts of resilience were established in disparate fields, contrasting physical resistance with ecological absorption.
- **Why it changed:** A need emerged to describe how different systems handle shocks, initially focusing on materials and later ecosystems. "Ecological resilience (introduced in a consistent manner in Holling, 1973) is also nowadays trending for climate change provisions" (Gkoumas et al., 2022).
- **Citations:**
    - _Urban Resilience: Methodologies, Tools and Evaluation_: "Ecological resilience (introduced in a consistent manner in Holling, 1973) is also nowadays trending for climate change provisions" (Gkoumas et al., 2022).
    - _A framework for urban climate resilience_: "The origins of the term ‘resilience’ imply strength and resistance, but in its more recent applications in ecology, socio-ecological systems... resilience is understood to require flexibility, learning and change" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Recognizing the roots in physical robustness versus ecological adaptability sets the baseline for distinguishing whether modern frameworks evaluate static structural strength or dynamic learning.

**Early 2000s to 2007: Emergence of Urban Resilience and Climate Adaptation**

- **What changed:** Resilience transitioned from pure ecology and engineering into urban planning and seismic risk, expanding to include social systems facing climate change. The IPCC formalized a definition bridging social and ecological elements.
- **Why it changed:** Increasing extreme weather events and natural disasters necessitated a paradigm that accommodated complex human environments rather than just structural ones. "Urban Resilience (including seismic), were introduced in Bruneau et al. (2003) and MCEER (2006)" (Gkoumas et al., 2022).
- **Citations:**
    - _Urban Resilience: Methodologies, Tools and Evaluation_: "Urban Resilience (including seismic), were introduced in Bruneau et al. (2003) and MCEER (2006)" (Gkoumas et al., 2022).
    - _A framework for urban climate resilience_: "The IPCC defines resilience as ‘the ability of a social or ecological system to absorb disturbances while retaining the same basic structure and ways of functioning, the capacity of self-organization, and the capacity to adapt to stress and change.’" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Integrating early definitions of urban adaptation establishes the required terminology parameters for curating contemporary resilience data.

**2009 - 2012: The Shift to Governance, Agents, and Social-Ecological Systems (SES)**
- **What changed:** The focus shifted sharply from physical infrastructure to the role of institutions, vulnerable populations, and local governance. Framework dimensions evolved to map social behaviors alongside physical systems.
- **Why it changed:** Planners realized that "engineering robust systems" was insufficient and that local governance and marginalized groups determined actual urban survival. Frameworks reacted against the traditional "predict and prevent" approach (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Citations:**
    - _Urban Governance for Adaptation: Assessing Climate Change Resilience in Ten Asian Cities_: Proposed dimensions include "(1) decentralisation and autonomy, (2) accountability and transparency, (3) responsiveness and flexibility, (4) participation and inclusion and (5) experience and support" (Tanner et al., 2009). Applied via "rapid climate resilience assessments conducted in ten Asian cities" (Tanner et al., 2009).
    - _A framework for urban climate resilience_: Proposed dimensions as "infrastructure and ecosystems (both captured under the rubric of ‘systems’), social agents and institutions" (Tyler & Moench, 2012). Evidence of real-world application: "resilience planning activities undertaken in 10 cities across Asia through the Asian Cities Climate Change Resilience Network" (Tyler & Moench, 2012).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Categorizing variables into systems, agents, and institutions provides a structural template for organizing the data requirements of subsequent governance and equity analyses.

**2015 - 2018: Framing Divisions, Antifragility, and Systematized Dimensions**

- **What changed:** Academic literature fractured into distinct interpretations (System vs. Community resilience) and introduced evolutionary concepts like "antifragility." International organizations like UN-Habitat systematized dimensions for broader applicability.
- **Why it changed:** There was recognition that generic "resilience" masked competing priorities (e.g., maintaining infrastructure vs. social cohesion). Traditional metrics were deemed insufficient, leading to "antifragility" (Taleb, 2012) where systems "benefit from the variability of the environment" (Gkoumas et al., 2022).
- **Citations:**
    - _Urban Resilience: Methodologies, Tools and Evaluation_: Contrasts "the ‘System Resilience’ frame, which focuses on maintaining functions and processes, and the ‘Community Resilience’ frame, which emphasises urban life and community capacity & self- sufficiency" (Wardekker, 2022).
    - _Climate Resilience Assessment in Regions, Cities..._: Notes the 2018 UN-Habitat dimensions: "organizational, spatial, functional, and physical", which were later adapted to assess the "Barcelona Metropolitan Area" (Brito et al., 2026).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** Identifying the divergence between system-focused and community-focused frameworks is critical to ensure that curated measurement tools do not inadvertently overlook social justice.

**2020 - 2026: Forward-Looking Readiness, Composite Indices, and High-Tech Data Fusion**

- **What changed:** Assessments moved from qualitative diagnostics and past performance metrics to predictive, composite indices utilizing digital twins, AI, and continuous tracking. Dimensions expanded to include global integration and technology.
- **Why it changed:** Unprecedented scale of climate threats and global interconnectedness meant past performance no longer guaranteed future survival. Assessments needed to measure "systemic readiness" to handle future uncertainties (Kearney, 2025).
- **Citations:**
    - _Global City Resilience Index_: Shifts focus to "systemic readiness" utilizing dimensions of "institutional governance; sustainable finance and business; technology and innovation; social and human capital; and global integration" assessed across "31 cities" (Kearney, 2025).
    - _Climate-Resilience-Index-2024_: Established "four major measurable cornerstone categories are: environmental, economic, infrastructural, and social", evaluated in "Baltimore, MD... Seattle, WA... Tampa, FL" (ESI, 2024).
    - _Bridging urban divides for inclusive and sustainable development_: Highlights reliance on "data-driven urban planning tools such as digital twins, remote sensing technologies, and geographic information systems (GIS)" like "Seoul’s S-Map, a 4D urban digital twin" (ESCAP, 2025).
- **Implication for downstream analysis:** The transition to remote sensing, digital twins, and big data mandates that modern resilience evaluations must assess the technical data-gathering capacity of the city in question.


### **(b) Synthesis**

** 1. What changed in what gets measured (dimensions) and how it gets measured (methods/data) over time?**

- **Evolution of What Gets Measured (Definitions & Dimensions):** Initially, the concept began with a strict _engineering_ definition focused on the physical robustness and ability of infrastructure to bounce back to a stable state. This transitioned to an _ecological_ definition emphasizing the ability to absorb disturbance and adapt to new states. By the late 2000s, it shifted into a _social-ecological_ systems perspective, measuring the capacities of social agents, governance structures, and institutions alongside infrastructure. In the 2020s, the dimensions expanded into a _forward-looking urban systems_ approach, measuring not just localized physical/social traits, but a city's systemic readiness, global integration, technological capacity, equity/justice, and financial structures to anticipate and transform from unknown future shocks.
- **Evolution of How It Gets Measured (Methods & Data Regimes):** Early methodologies relied heavily on _qualitative diagnostics_, primary surveys, and participatory local knowledge gathered post-disaster. As the field matured, methods shifted towards structured _scorecards_ and rigid hierarchical frameworks. By the 2020s, the dominant methodology became _hybrid mixed-method assessments_ and _composite indices_ (aggregating normalized quantitative and qualitative variables). Consequently, the data regimes evolved from isolated administrative and statistical inputs to _multi-source fusion_, integrating big data, IoT sensors, remote sensing, and 4D digital twins capable of predictive, real-time dynamic simulation.
