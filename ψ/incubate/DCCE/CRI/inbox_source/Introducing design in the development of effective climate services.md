>Christel, I., Hemment, D., Bojovic, D., Cucchietti, F., Calvo, L., Stefaner, M., & Buontempo, C. (2018). Introducing design in the development of effective climate services. _Climate Services_, _9_, 111–121. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cliser.2017.06.002.

The life cycle of the climate service development process can be structured using the **Design Study Methodology**, which outlines a sequential, yet iterative, process composed of nine stages grouped into three main phases: **Precondition, Core, and Analysis**.

**1. Precondition Phase (Problem Characterisation with Human-Centred Design)** This 'front end' phase focuses on building a cross-disciplinary understanding of the project, identifying promising collaborations, and defining team roles. It is heavily grounded in human-centred design to ensure the final service is useful, usable, and tailored to the end-users. The key steps in this phase include:

- **Knowledge exchange:** Fostering a shared understanding between climate scientists and designers regarding the primary purpose of the project and the main domain and informational challenges.
- **Understanding the problem space:** Engaging with industry stakeholders through workshops, surveys, and interviews to explore the industry landscape, user needs, and potential applications for climate predictions.
- **Defining the role of design:** Determining specific contexts of use, defining primary objectives, and developing a clear design brief for the climate service prototype.
- **Understanding data and user capacity:** Analyzing the complex, probabilistic climate prediction data alongside the specific questions users need to answer.
- **Characterizing user requirements:** Gathering user focus groups to precisely characterize the end-users and confirm how the information will actually be utilized in their day-to-day decision-making (e.g., for planning, maintenance, or investment decisions).

**2. Core Phase (Visualisation Design and Evaluation)** This phase moves into the active design, implementation, and deployment of the climate service system.

- **Design and Implementation:** An interdisciplinary team—often including design researchers, climate scientists, and data designers—collaborates to translate probabilistic data into an accessible user interface. This involves rapid experimentation with visual design vocabularies and interface prototypes to balance scientific comprehensiveness with clear, aesthetic communication.
- **Deployment and Evaluation:** Early versions of the prototype are formally evaluated with a subset of potential end-users. Developers observe how users freely explore the tool and ask them to perform realistic tasks to gauge the product's usefulness, user-friendliness, and ability to cover their daily information needs.

**3. Analysis Phase (User Engagement and Reflection)** The final phase focuses on reflection, writing, and outward-facing validation.

- **Multiple channels of communication:** Stakeholder engagement is treated as an integral component, utilizing diverse communication channels such as web applications, interactive exhibition modules, media coverage, and industry conferences to reach different target audiences.
- **Reflection:** The team reflects on the design process and user feedback to suggest future improvements, refine how probabilities are communicated, and identify new ways to increase the service's trustworthiness.

While these phases provide a structured framework, **the development process is not strictly linear and can loop back to any of the preceding stages** to refine the service based on new insights, user feedback, or data constraints.
