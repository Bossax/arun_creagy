## 1. Introduction

Urban resilience frameworks have evolved significantly since 2015, with a strong emphasis on climate adaptation and the integration of adaptive, absorptive, and transformative capacities. The indicator concepts listed in the provided table—such as plan revision cycles, stakeholder coordination, emergency budget disbursement, and critical infrastructure robustness—are widely validated and remain central to contemporary urban resilience assessment (Zeng et al., 2022; Ribeiro & Gonçalves, 2019; Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Mabrouk et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2022; Al-Humaiqani & Al‐Ghamdi, 2022; Giulia, 2023; Lv & Sarker, 2024; Lowe et al., 2024). Recent literature confirms their continued relevance but also highlights persistent challenges: many indices still over-rely on static or output-focused (lagging) indicators rather than dynamic, process-based (leading) ones (Shiozaki et al., 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). The Social-Ecological-Technological Systems (SETS) framework is increasingly recognized for its potential to address emerging gaps by incorporating technological agency (e.g., IoT-enabled early warning), cross-scale feedbacks between neighborhood assets and regional systems, and institutional plasticity for adaptive governance (Sharifi, 2023; Riaz et al., 2023). However, empirical integration of these SETS dimensions remains limited. Alternative index structures—such as flow-based models, temporal/spatial frameworks, and input-output-outcome taxonomies—are gaining traction as complements or critiques of traditional asset/process and coping/adaptive/transformative categorizations (Yang et al., 2023; Carvalhaes et al., 2021; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). Overall, while foundational pillars are robustly supported, advancing urban resilience measurement requires greater attention to technological mediation, multi-scalar interactions, and governance flexibility.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Empirical Support & Thematic Mapping of Indicator Concepts

Most indicator concepts from the table align closely with validated pillars in recent literature:

**Plan revision cycle**, **post-event review completion**, **coordination/multi-stakeholder collaboration**, **community engagement/social support**, **emergency budget/procurement timelines**, **climate budget tagging**, **financial mechanisms/resource allocation**, **service delivery timeliness**, **case throughput rate**, **data governance/interoperability**, **climate innovation/communication tech**, **emergency/human resource capacity**, **risk-informed planning**, **risk assessment/diagnostics**, **critical infrastructure robustness**, and **progress in implementation** are all widely cited as essential components (Zeng et al., 2022; Ribeiro & Gonçalves, 2019; Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Mabrouk et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2022; Al-Humaiqani & Al‐Ghamdi, 2022; Giulia, 2023; Lv & Sarker, 2024).

These indicators map onto three main capacities:

- Coping/absorptive: Immediate response functions (e.g., emergency funds disbursement)
- Adaptive: Ongoing learning/planning processes (e.g., plan revision cycles)
- Transformative: Systemic change/inclusion/innovation (e.g., digital platforms for engagement) (Zeng et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2022; Al-Humaiqani & Al‐Ghamdi, 2022).

### 3.2 Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

- **Leading indicators**: Process-oriented measures that signal future performance or readiness—plan revision cycle, stakeholder coordination mechanisms, community engagement breadth/depth, risk-informed planning processes (Feldmeyer et al., 2019; Mabrouk et al., 2024; Lowe et al., 2024).
- **Lagging indicators**: Outcome/output measures reflecting realized performance—service delivery timeliness after events, critical infrastructure robustness post-disaster (Shiozaki et al., 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). Recent reviews emphasize the need to balance both types but note an over-reliance on lagging indicators in practice (Shiozaki et al., 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022).

### 3.3 SETS Integration & Emerging Gaps

The SETS framework is increasingly referenced but not yet fully operationalized:

- **Technological Agency**: Digital infrastructure’s role (IoT sensors/smart grids) is recognized as crucial for real-time monitoring and mediating social behavior during shocks; however, few indices systematically capture this dimension (Sharifi, 2023; Riaz et al., 2023).
- **Cross-Scale Feedbacks**: Interactions between neighborhood engineering assets (e.g., green infrastructure) and regional ecological health are conceptually acknowledged but rarely measured empirically (Sharifi, 2023).
- **Institutional Plasticity**: Governance flexibility—the ability to shift from centralized to distributed management—is highlighted as vital but underrepresented among standard indicators (Sharifi, 2023).

### 3.4 Structural & Presentation Alternatives

Critiques of current index structures have led to several alternatives:

- Asset vs. Process & Coping/Adaptive/Transformative remain dominant but face criticism for being too static or siloed (Carvalhaes et al., 2021).
- Alternative/complementary taxonomies include:
    - **Flow-based vs. Stock-based models**: Emphasize resource movement versus static inventories (Yang et al., 2023).
    - **Temporal vs. Spatial structures**: Assess how resilience evolves over time or varies across locations/scales (Mabrouk et al., 2024).
    - **Input–Output–Outcome frameworks**: Distinguish between policy efforts (inputs), activities/results (outputs), and real-world impacts (outcomes); recent studies show most municipal adaptation metrics still focus on outputs rather than outcomes or impacts (Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022).

## 4. Discussion

The reviewed literature confirms that most core urban resilience indicator concepts remain empirically robust and relevant for municipal-level climate adaptation assessment (Zeng et al., 2022; Ribeiro & Gonçalves, 2019; Feldmeyer et al., 2019). However, there is a persistent gap between conceptual frameworks—which increasingly call for dynamic measurement—and actual practice that often defaults to static or output-focused metrics (Shiozaki et al., 2024; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022). Leading indicators such as plan revision cycles or stakeholder coordination are essential for proactive governance but are less frequently operationalized than lagging outcome measures like service delivery timeliness.

The SETS framework offers a promising path forward by emphasizing technological agency (digital infrastructure’s role), cross-scale feedbacks between local engineering assets/ecosystems/regional health outcomes, and institutional plasticity/flexibility in governance models (Sharifi, 2023). Yet empirical validation of these dimensions is still rare; most indices do not fully integrate technological mediation or multi-scalar interactions.

Alternative structural approaches—including flow-based models that track resource movement over time/space and input-output-outcome taxonomies that clarify causal chains from policy effort to social reality—are gaining traction but remain underutilized at the municipal level due to data limitations or institutional inertia (Yang et al., 2023; Carvalhaes et al., 2021; Goonesekera & Olazabal, 2022).

## 5. Conclusion

In summary, foundational urban resilience indicator concepts retain strong empirical support within post-2015 climate adaptation literature at the municipal scale. However, significant conceptual gaps persist around integrating technological agency (digital infrastructure), cross-scale feedbacks between local assets/ecosystems/regional outcomes, and institutional plasticity into practical measurement systems. While alternative structural approaches such as flow-based models or input-output-outcome frameworks are emerging as valuable complements/critiques to traditional asset/process/capacity categorizations, their adoption remains limited.
