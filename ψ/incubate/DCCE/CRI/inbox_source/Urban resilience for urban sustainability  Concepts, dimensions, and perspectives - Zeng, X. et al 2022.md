# Methodology

The methodology of this paper relies on a mixed-method approach consisting of a systematic literature review guided by the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) protocol, paired with a narrative review.

To derive the indicators and concepts, the researchers followed a structured process to analyze and synthesize the existing body of literature:

- **Database Search and Criteria:** The researchers conducted targeted searches in the Web of Science and Scopus databases for peer-reviewed journal articles published in English between January 1, 2001, and November 30, 2021. They used search strings containing keywords such as "urban resilience", "sustainability", and "urban management".
- **PRISMA Screening Process:** The initial identification stage yielded 1576 documents. Applying the PRISMA steps (identification, screening, eligibility, and inclusion), the researchers filtered out duplicates, off-topic papers, and articles lacking full text, ultimately narrowing the pool down to the 49 most relevant papers for in-depth qualitative analysis.
- **Interdisciplinary Data Extraction:** Acknowledging that urban resilience and sustainability are complex and multifaceted, the researchers extracted insights across several academic domains, including urban management, planning, geography, environmental science, and computer science.
- **Cluster Analysis:** The researchers utilized a software tool called VOS viewer to conduct bibliographic coupling and cluster analysis. By analyzing how frequently certain author keywords co-occurred across the papers, they mapped out logical groupings of topics and visualized the interconnections between different concepts.
- **Synthesizing and Categorizing the Indicators:** Using a narrative review approach, the authors comprehensively analyzed the findings within the final 49 selected papers. They identified the specific variables, resources, and metrics that previous scholars had researched to measure a city's ability to withstand shocks or maintain environmental health. They then synthesized and categorized these metrics into the three core capacities of urban resilience (adaptive, absorptive, transformative) and the three core dimensions of urban sustainability (environmental, economic, social).

# Interpretation of the indicators
The indicators for urban resilience and sustainability are designed to translate broad, complex concepts into measurable elements for **results-oriented assessment**. They should be interpreted as tools that capture specific, individual phenomena—such as the percentage of tree canopy cover or the number of impoverished individuals—which can then be aggregated into a composite index based on their relative weights. By breaking down complex systems, these indicators validate broader theoretical frameworks and offer tangible insights into the specific dynamics of a city.

For the reader—particularly city planners, policymakers, researchers, and citizens—the intended practical uses of these indicators include:

- **Establishing Baselines and Benchmarks:** They define the current status of an urban environment, providing a necessary foundation for measuring ongoing environmental, economic, and social changes. They act as benchmarks to compare current conditions against past baselines or future targets.
- **Informing Stakeholders:** They are meant to educate both the general public and policymakers about a city's specific strengths, weaknesses, and priority areas, thereby helping to align collective efforts toward achieving sustainability goals.
- **Driving Policy and Decision-Making:** Metrics provide the unambiguous data required for policymakers to make informed decisions and formulate effective public awareness policies. Furthermore, indicators reflect specific policy measures and highlight the populations they impact; the text notes that indicators which successfully garner political backing are more likely to be accepted and implemented.
- **Evaluating Sustainable Practices:** They serve as active, practical tools used to continuously evaluate, review, and implement sustainable urban practices.
