## Revised cross-cutting observations after adding the four interviews

1. **The user landscape is wider than the first matrix implied.** The ecosystem now clearly includes private-sector intermediaries ([FTI](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/Interview%20summary%20notes/Interview%20Summary%20-%20FTI.md)), city operators ([BMA](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/Interview%20summary%20notes/Interview%20Summary%20-%20BMA.md)), local implementation intermediaries ([UDDC](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/Interview%20summary%20notes/Interview%20Summary%20-%20UDDC.md)), and multi-scale planning regulators ([DPT](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/Interview%20summary%20notes/Interview%20Summary%20-%20DPT.md)), not only central agencies and analysts.
2. **“Single source of truth” is no longer just a policy-governance theme.** It is now also a business, city-operations, and local-budgeting requirement. FTI, UDDC, BMA, and DPT each describe costs created by fragmented hazard baselines and unclear owner authority.
3. **Spatial granularity splits into at least three distinct demand tiers.** Provincial comparison remains useful for high-level communication, but newer interviews add strong evidence for planning-grade city data (UDDC, DPT), operational urban data (BMA), and facility/industrial-estate or sector-location analysis (FTI).
4. **Interoperability is not only about cataloging.** BMA and DPT point to machine-readable exchange, GIS-ready formats, and reduction of manual website checking or PDF/paper handling as necessary design conditions for real use.
5. **Tiered service design is now strongly evidenced.** UDDC asks for raw data plus executive dashboards; BMA needs technical operator feeds plus simplified decision products; FTI asks for trusted, usable interfaces rather than broken websites; this strengthens the case for differentiated product layers in Chapter 2.
6. **Budget justification emerges as a recurring downstream use.** UDDC needs DCCE-authoritative evidence to justify proactive spending; FTI needs impact-to-cost translation; BMA and DPT imply the same logic for urban infrastructure and planning investment.
7. **Urban heat and water-management use cases are now materially stronger.** BMA adds operational heat-management and flood-operations evidence, while FTI and DPT add business and planning implications of heat and water scarcity beyond the earlier central-agency framing.
