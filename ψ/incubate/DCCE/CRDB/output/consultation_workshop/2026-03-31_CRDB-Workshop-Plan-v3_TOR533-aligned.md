# CRDB Workshop Plan v3 (TOR 5.3.3 Brainstorming Meeting — TOR533 aligned)

## TOR 5.3.3 compliance header (explicit)

**What this meeting is (TOR-literal)**

- This workshop is the **TOR 5.3.3 Brainstorming Meeting** under the CRDB/DCCE project scope.
- Purpose (TOR-literal): **exchange data, information products, and needs/demand** among relevant agencies.
- Format (TOR-literal): at least **1 time**, at a **hotel** or via **hybrid/live stream** (final modality confirmed by DCCE).

**Minimum participation requirement (TOR-literal)**

- The meeting must evidence **≥ 80 participants** (minimum).
- Evidence package (minimum): registration list + sign-in sheet, participant list by organization, group assignment list, and photos.

**How this plan supports TOR downstream deliverables**

- Meeting outputs are structured to feed:
  - **Information Product Inventory** (TOR 5.3.4)
  - **Baseline Data Inventory** (TOR 5.3.5)
  - **Gap Analysis (Supply vs Demand)** (TOR 5.3.8)
  - **Policy/technical recommendations** (TOR 5.3.9)

## 1) Purpose and audience (v3)

This plan designs a practical, evidence-ready **brainstorming meeting** that enables:

1) structured exchange of **datasets**, **information products**, and **participant needs/demand**; and
2) capture in a format that can be consolidated into CRDB TOR-facing inventories and gap-analysis work.

**Primary audience**

- DCCE/CRDB sponsor team and implementing team (workshop owner and output steward).

**Participant audience**

- Relevant agencies and stakeholders across the climate risk and adaptation data value chain (providers, integrators, operators, users, and governance/rails actors).

**NFCS note (scope guardrail)**

- NFCS can be used as **background ecosystem framing** (to help participants see the national landscape), but **NFCS is not the promised workshop output**.

- The agenda must include explicit moments where participants:
	- name and describe datasets they hold (or can steward),
	- name information products they produce (or rely on), and
	- state needs/demand in an actionable form.

### 2.2 Headcount: design for ≥80 total while keeping breakouts workable

- Plan for **≥80 participants** total.
- Use a breakout rule that protects discussion quality while scaling to 80+.

- Keep **1–2 hazard-focused decision journeys** as the “organizing narrative,” but selection must satisfy:
	- **Maturity**: enough known datasets/products exist for many orgs to contribute (not only 1–2 technical units).
	- **Broad contribution**: stakeholders beyond one niche community can add value.
	- **Invite-feasibility**: the target participant mix is realistically achievable for this workshop.

**Default journey choice (v3)**
- **Journey 1 (default): Flood risk** (highest maturity and broadest stakeholder coverage).
- **Journey 2 (conditional): Heat risk** only if participant mix includes sufficient public health / urban operations / local authority practitioners to contribute meaningfully.
	  - If not, Journey 2 becomes **TBD at Session 1** based on who is in the room (or is dropped to run one journey well).

## 4) Default breakout rule (must be stated and used)

**Rooms and size**

- Default: **max 6 breakout rooms**.
- Target: **≤10 participants per room**.

**Composition (mixed-hat)**

- Each room should mix:
	- dataset providers and stewards,
	- integrators/processors,
	- operators/implementers,
	- end users (planning / budgeting / response / adaptation).

**If 2 journeys are used**

- Allocate **3 rooms per journey** (3 + 3 = 6 rooms).

**If only 1 journey is used (fallback)**
All 6 rooms work on the same journey, but with different **lenses** to prevent redundancy:
	- Lens A: datasets + access/maturity constraints
	- Lens B: information products currently produced
	- Lens C: user needs/demand and “what good looks like”
	- Lens D: priority gaps to unlock immediate usability
	- Lens E: minimum operating touchpoints (who talks to whom, when)
	- Lens F: service bundle/blueprint consolidation

## 5) Workshop structure (five-session spine; baseline-first then blueprint)

This structure preserves the five-session spine, but explicitly aligns session ordering and outputs to TOR 5.3.3 exchange and evidence capture.

### Day framing (explicit): Morning = core workshop value; Afternoon = DCCE briefing + feedback (lighter)

This workshop is designed as a **two-part day** with a mechanical bridge between segments:

- **Morning (core structured work block)**: structured exchange + **service bundle/blueprint work**.
  - The morning is where the workshop creates its highest-value artifacts and most structured capture.
- **Afternoon (lighter segment; DCCE-owned)**: **DCCE briefing** on **NCAIF + data management** direction + **structured feedback capture**.
  - The afternoon is designed to be **low-demand**: validate direction, surface constraints, and collect actionable feedback (not another deep co-design/stress-test workshop).

The bridge between morning and afternoon is implemented via **bridge artifacts** (see “Morning-to-afternoon bridge” below), so the afternoon discussion is grounded in what the group produced in the morning.

### Session 1 — Frame, TOR533 confirmation, and Journey selection (Plenary)

**Purpose**

- Confirm this is the **TOR 5.3.3 brainstorming meeting** and what evidence will be produced.
- Confirm whether the workshop runs **1 journey or 2 journeys**, using the tightened criteria.
- Confirm default breakout rule (6 rooms max; ≤10 per room; mixed-hat).

**Journey selection criteria (operational)**

- Choose journeys only if:
  - ≥3 clusters can contribute (provider + integrator + user/implementer), and
  - the journey is not limited to one narrow ministry/community.

**Expected outputs**

- Confirmed Journey 1 (default flood) and Journey 2 (conditional heat or dropped).
- Final breakout room allocation (3+3 if 2 journeys; 6 lenses if 1 journey).

# Appendix - Q&A
## a) Journey vs Use case

A  *journey* is a structured narrative of “who does what, in what order, with what information,” while a *use case* or MVP slice is usually a narrower product/feature or scenario carved out of that journey. Using the **same journey** across breakouts keeps everyone anchored on the same storyline, so differences in input come from *roles and perspectives*, not from competing story frames.

More detail:

1. **Journey vs use case**
- **Journey**
  - End‑to‑end path: from trigger → decisions → actions → outcomes.
  - Includes multiple actors (eg national planner, DRR operator, LAO, data provider) and multiple information touchpoints.
  - Good for: aligning mental models, mapping data and product needs across the whole value chain, seeing hand‑offs.

- **Use case / MVP**
  - A narrower “slice” where a specific actor tries to do a specific task with data (eg “provincial planner prepares a flood risk budget proposal” or “operations center issues heatwave warning”).
  - Good for: scoping products and features, writing user stories, designing one concrete service.

In practice: 
- One journey may contain **several use cases** or MVPs. For example, a flood preparedness journey could include:
  - Use case A: data provider compiles baseline flood risk layers.
  - Use case B: planner uses those layers to prioritize investments.
  - Use case C: DRR operator uses them to update contingency plans.

2. Why identical journey across breakouts is often better

If you fix **one journey per round** *and* give the **same journey** to each breakout group:

- **Shared anchor, diverse perspectives**  
  Every table is “looking at the same movie,” but from different seats (data owner, integrator, planner, operator, etc.). That lets you compare:
  - where people disagree on what exists,
  - where responsibilities are unclear,
  - where data and product gaps show up in the same steps.

- **Synthesis is mechanically easier**  
  When all groups use the same steps, you can stack their outputs step‑by‑step:
  - Step 1 (hazard monitoring): all tables’ notes side by side.  
  - Step 2 (risk assessment): same.  
  - Step 3 (planning/budgeting): same.
  This makes it much easier to build the baseline inventory, product inventory, and needs/gaps views without re‑interpreting different stories.

- **Less cognitive load for participants**  
  You only explain the journey once. People don’t have to re‑contextualize when they move from one exercise to the next. That’s important when you have many actors, overlapping roles, and limited time.

- **Stronger evidence for TOR 5.3.3**  
  TOR wants a brainstorming meeting that **exchanges data, products, and needs**. If every breakout works on the same journey, you can show:
  - for each journey step, *which datasets and products* were surfaced by whom, and  
  - *which needs* and gaps were recorded.  
  That is cleaner evidence than disparate scenarios that are harder to line up.

3. When multiple journeys or per‑MVP breakouts make sense

There *are* cases for varying the frame:
- If you have **time for multiple rounds**, you can run:
  - Round 1: Journey A (eg flood 2025 event) — all groups same journey.  
  - Round 2: Journey B (eg heatwave) — again all groups same journey.  
- If the TOR or sponsor cares about **specific MVPs** (eg “heat‑health dashboard” vs “flood compensation eligibility tool”), you might:
  - keep one **macro journey** as the anchor (eg “from early warning to compensation”), and  
  - have sub‑groups zoom in on different MVPs **inside** that journey.

The risk with designing breakouts per MVP or per use case *without* a shared journey is:
- groups talk past each other in different contexts;
- synthesis turns into interpretive work (you have to align mismatched stories);
- you lose the ability to see where the same data/product problem appears across multiple actors along a single path.

4. Summary
- **Journey ≠ use case**: journey is the full storyline; use case is a specific slice inside it.
- Using the **same journey per breakout round** is better when your goals are: shared mental model, clean synthesis, and strong TOR‑type evidence.  
- Per‑MVP or per‑use‑case breakouts are useful for later **productization**, once a common journey frame and baseline/product inventories are already established.

## b) UX research terminilogy
### 1. How the terms are typically used

**User journey / journey map**
- End‑to‑end narrative from trigger → steps → touchpoints → outcome.
- In service design: often multi‑actor (frontstage user + backstage systems/teams).
- Used to build a **shared mental model** of “what actually happens.”
- Common practice: multiple teams or breakouts work on **the same journey** so everyone stays anchored in one storyline.

**Use case**
- A more focused scenario: *actor + goal + context* (e.g., “provincial planner prepares a climate‑risk budget request”).
- In software/UML, formalized as “actor interacts with system to achieve goal”; in UX/product, often more informal but similar.
- Typically treated as a **slice inside a journey**—you zoom in on one segment of the larger path.

**MVP (Minimum Viable Product)**
- The smallest product that:
  - delivers real value, and
  - is sufficient to test a hypothesis / learn from real usage.
- Often implements **one or a small cluster of use cases** taken from the journey.

So in industry language:
- **Journey** = shared backbone story.
- **Use case** = one concrete slice of that story for a specific actor/goal.
- **MVP** = a productized implementation of one or more of those use cases, small enough to ship and learn from.

### 2. Are journeys usually shared while use cases/MVPs are slices?

Yes, that pattern is very common and considered good practice:
- Workshops anchor everyone on **one shared journey map** per round (to keep context and synthesis coherent).
- Teams then derive **use cases** and **MVP ideas** as slices within that journey.

This matches the way you’re framing the CRDB workshop design: using a common journey to align actors, then using use‑case/MVP thinking when you move into productization and implementation decisions.
