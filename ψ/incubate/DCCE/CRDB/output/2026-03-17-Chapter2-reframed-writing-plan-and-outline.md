## Updated scope decision for the interim report

This note supersedes the broader Chapter 2 framing for the **interim report only**.

Boss confirmed that the broader Chapter 2 outline remains useful for the **final draft report** in early June, but the interim report must be narrower in scope. The interim-report version of Chapter 2 should focus only on:

1. **reviewing the current data product landscape**;
2. **summarizing interview evidence** on what data stakeholders generate, how they collect it, and how frequently they update it;
3. **identifying which datasets stakeholders say they need**; and
4. **summarizing the use cases of climate risk and impact information**.

## Why Chapter 2 needs recalibration

For the interim report, Chapter 2 should no longer try to carry the full architecture of a baseline inventory chapter. Instead, it should function as a **disciplined landscape review + interview synthesis** that helps the reader understand three things clearly:

1. **what products and data systems already exist** across DCCE and partner agencies;
2. **how stakeholder agencies currently generate and manage relevant data**; and
3. **what the evidence says about unmet dataset needs and use cases** for climate risk and impact information.

This narrower scope keeps Chapter 2 useful, evidence-based, and proportional to interim-report expectations.

## Writing rules for Chapter 2

### Tone and voice

- Use formal Thai that reads as part of the real report, not as a background working note.
- Prefer concrete institutional wording such as **บัญชีรายการข้อมูลอ้างอิง**, **คลังรายการผลิตภัณฑ์ข้อมูล**, **เจ้าของข้อมูล**, **ความถี่ในการปรับปรุง**, **ข้อจำกัดของการใช้งาน**, and **เส้นทางการเข้าถึงข้อมูล**.
- Reduce product jargon unless it helps precision. When technical terms are necessary, explain them through their operational role.

### Framing rules

- Do not present Chapter 2 as if the project has already completed a national baseline inventory in full.
- Do not make Chapter 2 carry too much of the governance and architecture burden already established in Chapter 1.
- Frame the chapter as an **evidence-led review of the current product landscape and stakeholder information practices**.
- Keep emphasizing that CRDB's role in this chapter is to **observe, synthesize, and organize what exists and what is needed**, rather than to claim completed integration.
- Use the interview summaries to show institutional reality: different agencies produce different kinds of data, at different scales, with different update cycles and constraints.

### What the chapter should make visible

The reader should be able to see, by the end of the chapter:

1. What kinds of climate-related data products and systems already exist across DCCE and partner agencies.
2. What data different stakeholders currently generate, how they collect it, and how often they update it.
3. What datasets stakeholders say they need but do not currently have in usable form.
4. What the main use cases are for climate risk and impact information across different institutional settings.
5. Where the evidence is already strong, and where additional analysis is still needed before later report stages.

## Revised narrative strategy for Chapter 2

Chapter 2 should move in the following sequence:

1. **Start with TOR intent and practical purpose**
   - Explain that TOR 5.3.1-5.3.2 requires the project to review the current product and data landscape, not merely to compile a technical list.

2. **Describe the current data product landscape**
   - Show which products, dashboards, portals, catalogs, reports, and systems already exist across DCCE and related agencies.

3. **Move into stakeholder data-generation practice**
   - Summarize what each interviewee group generates or manages, how data is collected, and how often it is updated.

4. **Then surface unmet data needs**
   - Show what each agency still needs in order to use climate risk and impact information more effectively.

5. **Conclude with use cases and implications**
   - Summarize what these patterns imply for the use of climate risk and impact information in policy, planning, budgeting, emergency response, social protection, and sector-specific analysis.

## Chapter 1 to Chapter 2 linkage points

To maintain flow and avoid repetition, Chapter 2 should connect back to specific Chapter 1 subsections as follows:

- **Chapter 1.2** should remain the place where interview evidence is introduced as a methodological source for design criteria. Chapter 2 should not repeat the full design-criteria argument; it should use the interviews as evidence about products, data practices, needs, and use cases.
- **Chapter 1.6** is now the merged analytical subsection on current-state implications from the website and product landscape. Chapter 2 should carry the fuller descriptive landscape review.
- **Chapter 1.10** in the revised Chapter 1 now explains the locked design choices and navigation model. Chapter 2 should not restate sitemap governance logic beyond what is needed to understand current products and use cases.
- **Chapter 1.9** should stay focused on workflow patterns and MVPs as the service translation of the architecture. Chapter 2 should not repeat MVP logic; it should provide the product and interview evidence that helps explain why certain use cases matter.
- **Chapter 1.10** and **Chapter 1.11** should remain architecture-facing and framework-facing. Chapter 2 should use FGD and interview findings only insofar as they reveal data realities, expressed needs, and use cases.

## Current evidence gaps and weak points

1. **No single comparison artifact yet exists across interviews** for: data generated, collection method, update frequency, granularity, expressed needs, and use cases.
2. **Update frequency is unevenly documented**. Some interviews give clear cadence clues, while others only imply periodicity or project-stage status.
3. **Current data product landscape evidence is dispersed** across [`ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/FGD 1 result.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/FGD%201%20result.md) and the interview summaries rather than consolidated in one comparison view.
4. **Use cases are rich but scattered** across interview summaries and [`ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/NCAIF_Use_Cases.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/NCAIF_Use_Cases.md), creating risk of repetitive writing unless clustered first.
5. **Some agencies are primarily infrastructure or governance actors rather than data producers**. Their treatment in Chapter 2 needs clear framing to avoid forcing them into the wrong analytical bucket.

## Revised drafting stance for Chapter 2

For the interim report, Chapter 2 should now be understood as:

- **not** a full baseline inventory chapter;
- **not** a repeated governance chapter;
- **not** a second architecture chapter;
- but a **structured review of the current product landscape and interview evidence about data practices, missing datasets, and uses of climate risk and impact information**.

## Recommended drafting stance for Chapter 2

If Chapter 1 established that NCAIF needs a hidden organizing logic and a visible user-facing structure, then the interim-report version of Chapter 2 should establish that **the current data product landscape and stakeholder information practices are the practical ground conditions that this architecture must respond to**.

In other words:

- **Chapter 1** = why the architecture and sitemap had to change
- **Chapter 2** = what product landscape, data practices, unmet needs, and use cases the architecture must respond to

## Suggested note on what to avoid in Chapter 2

1. Do not open with spreadsheet/catalog mechanics.
2. Do not repeat the Chapter 1 website-gap or risk-map argument in full.
3. Do not list interview findings agency by agency without synthesis logic.
4. Do not describe every external system as if it is already integrated.
5. Do not promise a completed national baseline inventory if registration is still in progress.
6. Do not make Chapter 2 sound more technical than Chapter 1; keep it formal, structured, and visibly useful for management decisions.
