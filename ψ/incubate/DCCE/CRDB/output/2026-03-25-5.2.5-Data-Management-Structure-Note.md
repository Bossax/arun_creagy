# TOR 5.2.5 – Data Management Structure Note (Sources → CDM → NCAIF → Products)

This note rebuilds the **data‑management structure narrative** for TOR 5.2.5 under a strict **PDF‑first discipline**. It is anchored primarily in the interim‑report v1 PDFs:

- Main report: **Chapter 1**, especially §§1.1–1.3 (TOR interpretation), §§1.8–1.10 (CDM, draft structure, governance), and Chapter 2 (current data landscape and constraints).
- Appendices: **Appendix ก** (CDM conceptual model), **Appendix ข** (draft NCAIF structure and sitemap), and **Appendix จ** (FGD synthesis on expectations for NCAIF and governance).

Helper notes such as [`2026-03-05-Feature-Driven Data Governance Strategy v3.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-05-Feature-Driven%20Data%20Governance%20Strategy%20v3.md) and [`2026-02-26-Detailed_CDM_Design.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-02-26-Detailed_CDM_Design.md) are used only where they **interpret or package what is already present in v1**, and are treated explicitly as draft design aids rather than canonical sources.

## 1. Purpose and scope of the data‑management structure

TOR 5.2.5 requires the project to describe **how data will be managed and governed**, not only how information will appear on the website. In the v1 main report, this requirement is framed as part of a broader shift:

- from viewing the project as "ปรับปรุงหน้าเว็บไซต์หรือการรวบรวมเอกสารเผยแพร่" (just a website and document collection) to designing a **"โครงสร้างบริการสารสนเทศด้านการปรับตัวระดับชาติ"**;
- and from thinking only about content menus to specifying a **"โครงสร้างการบริหารจัดการข้อมูล"** with clear roles, access rules, and minimum standards (Ch.1 §1.1–1.3, §1.10).

Within that framing, the data‑management structure has to connect four layers that already appear in the v1 PDFs:

1. **Source systems and datasets** – climate, risk, loss, socio‑economic, and adaptation data held by DCCE and partner agencies (Ch.1 §1.6; Ch.2 §§2.1–2.4; Appendix ง).
2. The **Conceptual Data Model (CDM)** – the semantic backbone that organises these datasets into subject areas and entities (Ch.1 §1.8; Appendix ก).
3. The **NCAIF information framework and sitemap** – the way information is organised and presented to different user groups (Ch.1 §1.9; Appendix ข).
4. **User‑facing products and packs** – briefing materials, risk and area profiles, and post‑event syntheses that rely on the above layers (Executive summary; Ch.2 §2.6–2.7; Appendix ค use cases).

The rest of this note follows that flow: sources → CDM → catalog and registry → NCAIF content → products, with governance rails and gates cutting across all tiers.

## 2. Source layer – what exists before NCAIF

### 2.1 Diversity of existing data and products

Chapter 2 of the main report emphasises that Thailand already has a **rich but fragmented ecosystem** of datasets and products (Ch.2 §§2.1–2.4, §2.7):

- Within DCCE: risk‑map products, policy reports, and web content that already expose some climate‑risk information but follow project‑ or division‑specific logics.
- Beyond DCCE: disaster records, socio‑economic statistics, infrastructure datasets, and sector‑specific dashboards, each with its own cadence, spatial detail, and legal constraints.

The key message is that the problem is **not data scarcity**, but lack of shared organising principles, metadata, and access routes. The data‑management structure must therefore respect this diversity and avoid assuming that all data can be pulled into a single new database.

### 2.2 Sources as governed assets, not raw "inputs"

In v1 Ch.1 §1.10, the report states that NCAIF cannot work unless there are clear agreements on:

- which datasets are suitable for publication on the platform;
- who is responsible for their content and recency; and
- how limitations and caveats are communicated to users.

This implies that source datasets are viewed as **governed assets** owned by line agencies and DCCE divisions, with NCAIF acting as a **consumer and organiser**, not a replacement owner. Appendix ง reinforces this, describing how each interviewed agency already maintains its own systems, update cycles, and constraints.

The data‑management structure therefore treats the source layer as:

- **multi‑institutional** (many owners and custodians);
- **heterogeneous in cadence and granularity** (from event‑driven disaster logs to decadal projections and multi‑year surveys);
- **governed by existing sectoral mandates**, which NCAIF must align with rather than override.

## 3. CDM layer – how meaning is aligned

### 3.1 Four subject areas and the risk/impact spine

The CDM is introduced in Ch.1 §1.3 and elaborated in §1.8 and Appendix ก. It groups entities into four subject areas:

1. **Climate drivers and conditions** – covering climate drivers, scenarios, and hazardous events.
2. **Risk and impact assessment** – linking hazards, exposure, vulnerability, and resulting risk metrics and composite indices.
3. **Resilience assessment** – covering frameworks and indices that measure adaptive capacity and resilience.
4. **Adaptation planning and implementation** – representing options, projects, and results.

Within subject area 2, the report and Appendix ก emphasise that the **risk and impact assessment group** is the **central connector**. It joins climate‑hazard information with exposure, vulnerability determinants, and observed loss and damage (Appendix ก §§1–2.4). This is the structural spine that subsequent governance decisions build on.

### 3.2 CDM as the constraint layer for NCAIF

The v1 report explicitly states that without a CDM, TOR 5.2.5 tasks such as structuring basic data inventories, loss‑and‑damage content, or adaptation‑planning information would risk being:

- inconsistent between sections;
- tied to one‑off project taxonomies; or
- difficult to extend to new use cases without schema changes (Ch.1 §1.3, §1.8).

The **data‑management structure** therefore assumes that:

- any dataset that will be surfaced in NCAIF must be **mappable to CDM entities** (e.g. `DISASTER_RECORD`, `LOSS_DAMAGE_RECORD`, `EXPOSED_ASSET`, `RISK_ASSESSMENT`, `ADAPTATION_PROJECT`), as described in [`2026-02-26-Detailed_CDM_Design.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-02-26-Detailed_CDM_Design.md);
- CDM subject areas define **which pieces of information belong together** in each NCAIF section (e.g. risk and area profiles draw from hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and risk metrics; adaptation sections draw from `ADAPTATION_PROJECT` and `INTERVENTION_RESULT`).

In Phase 1, the CDM operates primarily as a **logical and semantic layer** – a set of diagrams, definitions, and mapping rules – rather than a fully implemented physical database.

## 4. Governance rails and gates – how data flow safely

### 4.1 Three rails from v1 narrative

Ch.1 §1.10 presents a pragmatic view: the project should **reuse existing national digital‑government infrastructure** rather than build its own data‑exchange stack. This is echoed and elaborated in the governance strategy v3.

The structure assumes three main **publishing rails**:

1. **Open data (Rail A)** – datasets that can be openly published via agency catalogs and harvested to a central open‑data portal.
2. **G2G / non‑open data (Rail B)** – datasets shared between agencies via government data‑exchange infrastructure, based on legal mandate and bilateral agreements.
3. **Internal‑only data (Rail C)** – datasets kept within DCCE or partner agencies until governance and legal conditions allow broader sharing.

These rails are not inventions beyond v1; they are a **structured restatement** of the distinctions between public information, inter‑agency information, and sensitive internal information that appear throughout the v1 narrative and interviews (Ch.1 §1.10; Appendix ง on digital‑government and PDPA constraints).

### 4.2 Five minimum gates (G1–G5)

To avoid NCAIF becoming "เพียงพื้นที่รวมลิงก์" (just a link repository) or a visually attractive but ungoverned website, Ch.1 §1.10 argues that each dataset or product entering the system must satisfy basic conditions. Governance strategy v3 translates this into **five gates**:

1. **G1 – Data classification and publishing path**  
   Decide, for each asset, whether it is internal, G2G, or open; record the legal basis and the rail to be used.

2. **G2 – Catalog metadata and preview standard**  
   Ensure that every asset in scope for NCAIF has minimum metadata: name, description, owner, steward, spatial unit, update cadence, sensitivity level, and limitations. Where possible, provide a preview or summary so users understand the asset before downloading.

3. **G3 – Recommended baseline endorsement**  
   For key variables (e.g. provincial flood baseline), record which dataset is recommended, for which purpose, and at what scale. The v1 narrative (Ch.1 §1.10) refers to the need for a **ทะเบียนชุดข้อมูลอ้างอิงที่แนะนำให้ใช้** (baseline registry).

4. **G4 – Boundary and crosswalk governance**  
   Explicitly identify canonical boundaries for reporting and treat crosswalks between boundary systems as governed assets with owners, versions, and limitations (following Ch.2 discussion of spatial granularity and cross‑agency boundary issues).

5. **G5 – Event/impact schema governance**  
   For disaster and loss‑and‑damage data, require timeliness flags, validation status (e.g. preliminary, validated, revised), and visible revision history, so that users understand the reliability of post‑event analyses (Ch.2 §2.4; Appendix ง and Appendix ค cases on loss‑and‑damage reporting).

In Phase 1, these gates are applied **manually and procedurally** (through checklists, templates, and review processes) rather than through fully automated pipelines.

## 5. NCAIF, catalog, and registry layers – how structure and governance meet

### 5.1 Sitemap and content structure from v1

Appendix ข sets out the draft NCAIF structure in concrete sitemap terms:

- a **homepage** with risk highlights, key maps, and quick entry points;
- a **Policy Maker Centre** with summary indicators and status of national adaptation efforts;
- sections aligned with the **adaptation cycle** (climate science, risk and impacts, loss and damage, planning, implementation, monitoring);
- **risk and area profiles** that present provincial and area‑based risk summaries; and
- a **Knowledge / Tools / Data Services** section that contains tools, data catalogs, methods, and glossaries.

Ch.1 §1.9 explains that this structure is not arbitrary: it is designed so that users can enter along different paths (policy, geography, workflow) while still relying on the same underlying CDM subject areas.

### 5.2 Catalog as the first organisational layer

Under this sitemap, the data‑management structure introduces a **catalog layer** that sits between sources and pages:

- It lists datasets and products relevant to NCAIF along with metadata specified in G2.
- It indicates each asset’s classification (internal, G2G, open) and access path (open‑data URL, G2G route, internal request point).
- It tags datasets by CDM subject area and by their role in products (e.g. "primary hazard map", "baseline socio‑economic determinant").

This catalog does not need to physically host all datasets. For many assets, it acts as a **pointer with structured description**, as described in Appendix ข §3.6 (บัญชีข้อมูล การดาวน์โหลด และบริการเชื่อมต่อข้อมูล).

### 5.3 Recommended baseline registry (MVP‑3)

On top of the catalog sits a more selective **recommended baseline registry**. This registry addresses a recurrent problem documented in v1 (Ch.2 §2.4, §2.7; Appendix ง):

- different users select different datasets to answer the same question, leading to inconsistent numbers and narratives.

The registry responds by recording, for each high‑demand question (e.g. "flood risk for provincial planning", "baseline vulnerability index"), a **recommended dataset** or combination, with:

- spatial and temporal scope;
- level of validation and known limitations;
- appropriate and inappropriate uses; and
- the roles that endorsed the choice.

This is the governance "engine" behind the **MVP‑3 baseline registry** described in the governance strategy v3 and the CRDB plan.

### 5.4 Traceability from sources to content

Combining the catalog and registry, the data‑management structure aims to make every NCAIF page and product:

- traceable back to **specific catalog entries and baselines**;
- explainable in terms of **CDM entities** (e.g. which `RISK_ASSESSMENT` and `EXPOSED_ASSET` combinations underlie a profile); and
- auditable through governance records (which gate was passed, by whom, and when).

This traceability is the practical expression of the "ไม่ให้แพลตฟอร์มใหม่ย้อนกลับไปเป็นเพียงคลังเอกสาร" warning in Ch.1 §1.6 and §1.10.

## 6. Product layer – how users experience the structure

### 6.1 Types of products described in v1

Across the executive summary, Chapter 2, and Appendix ค, v1 describes multiple **use‑case‑driven products** that the system should support, even in a draft form:

- **Risk and area profiles** for provinces and sub‑areas, using risk maps and socio‑economic context (Ch.2 §2.6–2.7; Appendix ค cases on provincial planning and LAO budgeting).
- **Post‑event summaries and loss‑and‑damage briefs**, combining event records, exposure, and loss data (Appendix ค cases on post‑disaster assessment).
- **Briefing packs for policy‑makers**, summarising key risks, hotspots, and status of measures in a concise, exportable format (executive summary; Appendix ค policy‑oriented use cases).
- **Thematic explainers**, such as cascading‑impact narratives or vulnerable‑group insights (Appendix ค §2.7, §2.10–2.12).

These products are not yet fully automated in v1, but the narrative makes clear that they are the **intended direction** of NCAIF.

### 6.2 Products as structured recombinations of governed assets

Under the data‑management structure, each product is treated as a **structured recombination** of governed assets:

- the **data** it uses must be traceable to catalog entries and, where applicable, recommended baselines;
- the **narrative** must acknowledge limitations and appropriate uses, in line with G2 and G3;
- the **spatial coverage** must respect boundary and crosswalk governance (G4);
- and, for event‑related products, the **timing and validation status** must observe G5.

This framing allows products to be generated in an export‑first way (e.g. PDF, briefing slides), while preserving **links back to evidence** so that updates and corrections can be managed.

## 7. How this structure reflects v1 and TOR 5.2.5

### 7.1 Alignment with v1 main report

The structure described above does not introduce new conceptual tiers beyond what v1 already implies:

- **Source diversity and constraints** come directly from Chapter 2 and Appendix ง.
- **CDM subject areas and risk‑centric integration** come from Ch.1 §1.8–1.9 and Appendix ก.
- **NCAIF sitemap and content hierarchy** come from Ch.1 §1.9 and Appendix ข.
- **Governance emphasis** – roles, access levels, baselines, and status flags – comes from Ch.1 §1.10 and Appendix จ.
- **Use‑case‑driven products** come from the executive summary and Appendix ค.

Where governance strategy v3 and the detailed CDM design add terminology (e.g. naming G1–G5 explicitly), they are used only as **shorthand labels** for patterns already present in v1.

### 7.2 Responding to TOR 5.2.5

Within TOR 5.2, tasks 5.2.1–5.2.4 establish the **foundations** – benchmarks, DCCE landscape, NCAIF structure, and consultation feedback. TOR 5.2.5 then asks the project to set out the **revisions and data‑management structure** based on that foundation.

The data‑management structure described here does so by:

1. **Making CDM central** – ensuring that all NCAIF content is grounded in a coherent data model, not ad‑hoc fields.
2. **Using existing infrastructure** – aligning hosting and exchange with national open‑data and G2G platforms instead of duplicating them.
3. **Defining minimum governance gates** – so that Phase‑1 products, even if manually assembled, can be relied on and audited.
4. **Clarifying tiers from sources to products** – as summarised in the companion table [`2026-03-25-5.2.5-Data-Management-Table.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-25-5.2.5-Data-Management-Table.md).

In v1 language, this is the difference between having **"เว็บไซต์ที่สวยงาม"** and having a **sustainable information and data‑management architecture** that DCCE and its partners can operate beyond the life of the project.

### 7.3 Use in drafting Section 1 §1.10 / TOR 5.2.5

For the interim report, this note can be used as a **ready‑to‑drop narrative backbone** for Section 1’s discussion of the data‑management structure:

- the **tiered description** in Section 2–6 supports a concise in‑text explanation (1–2 pages) of how data flow from sources → CDM → NCAIF → products;
- the **governance rails and gates** provide language for summarising what "minimum viable governance" looks like in Phase 1;
- and the **alignment paragraphs** in Section 7 make it straightforward to reference relevant v1 sections and appendices without reconstructing the argument.

Any future refinements (e.g. after additional workshops or implementation experience) should be added as **superseding notes** that keep this v1‑anchored structure intact as the baseline for TOR 5.2.5.
