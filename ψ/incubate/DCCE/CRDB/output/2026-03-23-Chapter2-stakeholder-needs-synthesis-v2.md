## Expanded synthesis by cluster

### 1. Long-term projections and forward-looking scenarios

This remains one of the clearest recurring needs, but the newer interviews sharpen its downstream meaning. It is not only about national projections; it is also about planning-grade rainfall inputs for drainage design ([DPT](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/Interview%20summary%20notes/Interview%20Summary%20-%20DPT.md)), 20-year water-balance planning for industry ([FTI](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/Interview%20summary%20notes/Interview%20Summary%20-%20FTI.md)), and city-scale heat/flood preparation.

### 2. Authoritative baselines and a trusted reference point

The problem is not simply “no data,” but **multiple competing datasets without an endorsement mechanism**. FTI and UDDC show that this affects private-sector planning and local budget justification as much as national policy work.

### 3. Metadata, owner pathways, and discoverability

Metadata is now clearly tied to trust and usability, not only catalog completeness. FTI’s complaint about broken websites and unreachable contacts, together with DPT’s difficulty receiving digital GIS-ready data, shows that discoverability must include owner pathways, usable interfaces, and clear contactability.

### 4. Operationally relevant granularity

The newer interviews make granularity more differentiated. The gap is no longer only province-versus-tambon. It now spans neighborhood heat management, sub-city planning, intervention-site NbS design, and industrial/facility risk analysis.

### 5. Machine-readable interoperability and GIS-ready access

This cluster becomes much stronger with BMA, UDDC, and DPT. Cataloging alone is insufficient if operators still scrape websites manually, planners still receive PDFs, or modelers cannot obtain processable data.

### 6. Planning-grade and engineering-ready datasets

UDDC and DPT introduce a stronger planning-and-design perspective into the synthesis. They need data that can support land-use planning, drainage design, and local intervention design rather than only high-level maps.

### 7. Economic loss, business interruption, and budget justification

NESDC and TBA already showed the macroeconomic and financial sides of the issue. FTI and UDDC now add the business-continuity and local-budget-defense sides, making this a more general translation problem from hazards into investable evidence.

### 8. Vulnerability and resilience indicators

The new contribution here is UDDC’s explicit “double vulnerability” framing, which shows how social vulnerability indicators must be operationalized alongside localized hazard maps for real local planning decisions.

### 9. Tiered service design and trustworthy communication

This cluster should now be treated as a core design requirement rather than a usability extra. The interviews show repeated demand for different service layers: raw data for analysts/operators, dashboards and briefing products for executives, and accessible explanations for broader users.

### 10. Access, publishing, and governance pathways

Access problems remain institutional, but the FTI interview adds a practical trust dimension: even when data exist, poor stewardship, high turnover, and unclear support pathways can make the system feel unusable and unreliable.

## Strongest drafting takeaway

After adding FTI, UDDC, BMA, and DPT, the Chapter 2 demand-side backbone is no longer only about better data. It is about:

1. **trusted baselines**,
2. **planning- and operations-ready granularity**,
3. **machine-readable and GIS-ready access**,
4. **tiered service design with credible communication**, and
5. **translation of hazards into budget, business, and engineering decisions**.

These clusters should drive subsection **2.5** and sharpen the design implications discussed in subsection **2.7**.
