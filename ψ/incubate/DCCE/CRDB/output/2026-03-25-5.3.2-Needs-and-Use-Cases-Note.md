## 2. Baselines and indicators

### 2.1 What stakeholders are asking for

Across Appendix ง interviews and Chapter 2 §2.5, agencies repeatedly emphasise that the core problem is not simply “no data”, but **multiple, inconsistent baselines without a trusted reference point**.

- **NESDC (INT‑05; Appendix ง‑11–12)** needs standard indicators and baselines that can support macroeconomic planning, SDG reporting, and appraisal of large infrastructure projects. Current loss data and socio‑economic baselines come from many sources with different definitions and time frames.
- **NSO (INT‑06; Appendix ง‑13–15)** already maintains official census and survey baselines, but highlights constraints on environmental statistics coverage and spatial resolution. NSO stresses the need for clear tagging, metadata, and agreed indicator lists so that statistical products can be re‑used coherently for climate‑risk assessment.
- **NXPO (INT‑07; Appendix ง‑16–17)** notes the absence of any authoritative climate and resilience baseline that higher‑level policy processes can rely on. In their view, many agencies produce overlapping indicators, but no one is mandated to declare which indicators constitute the “national reference”.
- **DGA (INT‑01; Appendix ง‑2)** reinforces this from an infrastructure angle: their catalogs and GDX exchange layer can host metadata and references, but sector ministries must decide which datasets are official baselines.

Chapter 2 §2.5 therefore lists “ค่าฐานและตัวชี้วัดมาตรฐานกลางที่เป็นที่ยอมรับร่วมกัน” as one of the main demand clusters. Interviews make clear that stakeholders want both **numbers** and **interpretation rules** (which number to use, in which context, with what caveats).

### 2.2 Implications for NCAIF / CRDB components

The v1 narrative suggests that the NCAIF and CRDB baseline registry should:

- maintain a **register of endorsed baseline datasets and indicators**, with clear descriptions of:
  - intended use (e.g. national reporting, local planning, macroeconomic appraisal),
  - spatial coverage and resolution,
  - update cycle and responsible owner.
- implement the “ทะเบียนชุดข้อมูลอ้างอิงที่แนะนำใช้” concept referenced in Appendix ค (Section 4) and Chapter 2 §2.5.
- expose these baselines through:
  - **baseline registry pages** within the data‑services layer; and
  - **policy‑maker briefing packs** that explain how to use the indicators in decisions.

Relevant interviews and anchors: **INT‑01, INT‑05, INT‑06, INT‑07**; Chapter 2 §§2.3–2.5; Appendix ง‑2, ง‑11–15; Appendix ค‑2.9, 2.11.

## 3. Spatial scale and area profiles

### 3.1 What stakeholders are asking for

Chapter 2 §2.4 emphasises that **data usefulness depends strongly on the match between spatial scale and decision scale**. Appendix ง interviews provide concrete examples:

- **DLA (INT‑02)** reports that local administrations must justify budgets and design projects at municipality or sub‑district level, but often only have access to provincial risk indices or fragmented hazard data from multiple websites.
- **MSDHS (INT‑04)** works with vulnerable‑group registries at individual/household level, yet can only publish dashboards at sub‑district level. They need hazard and climate context at least at the same sub‑district / municipality granularity to plan services and relocation options (Appendix ง‑9–10).
- **OTP (INT‑08)** needs corridor‑ and asset‑level information for major transport routes. As Chapter 2 §2.4 notes, existing national risk maps are valuable for provincial comparison but are too coarse for detailed design. OTP commissions high‑resolution hydrological models to fill this gap.
- **Thai Bankers’ Association and commercial banks (INT‑09)** require hazard information at individual asset or parcel level for stress testing and portfolio analysis (Appendix ง‑20–21). Provincial averages are explicitly described as unusable for that purpose.
- **UDDC (INT‑10)**, working at city scale, uses high‑resolution elevation data and drone imagery to design ecosystem‑based measures and to understand “ความเปราะบางซ้อนทับ” at neighbourhood level (Appendix ง‑22–23).

Chapter 2 §§2.4 and 2.6 summarise this pattern as a mismatch between **current data scales (often province)** and **operational decision scales (municipality, corridor, asset, vulnerable‑group cluster)**.

### 3.2 Implications for NCAIF / CRDB components

Taken together, the interviews imply that NCAIF and CRDB should:

- organise **risk and area profiles** around decision‑relevant spatial units:
  - provincial overviews for national planning and communication;
  - LAO / municipality / sub‑district profiles for local planning and social protection;
  - corridor / asset‑scale summaries for infrastructure and financial use cases.
- maintain clear metadata and guidance on **where down‑scaling is legitimate and where it is not** (as emphasised in Chapter 2 §2.4 on the limits of using provincial indices for sub‑district interventions).
- treat spatial **crosswalk datasets** (e.g. between administrative units, grid cells, transport corridors, and statistical enumeration areas) as first‑class assets, consistent with the data‑management guidance in [`ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-12-Interim Report Writing Plan.md`](ψ/incubate/DCCE/CRDB/output/2026-03-12-Interim%20Report%20Writing%20Plan.md).

Relevant interviews and anchors: **INT‑02, INT‑04, INT‑08, INT‑09, INT‑10, INT‑11**; Chapter 2 §§2.3–2.4, 2.6; Appendix ง‑5–6, ง‑9–10, ง‑18–19, ง‑20–21, ง‑22–23; Appendix ค‑2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.5, 2.8, 2.12.

## 4. Update frequency and timeliness

### 4.1 What stakeholders are asking for

Chapter 2 §2.4 distinguishes between **event‑driven data**, **continuous system data**, and **periodic survey/census data**. Interviewees highlight both strengths and limitations of each type:

- **DDPM (INT‑11)** operates an event‑driven disaster‑reporting chain from village to province to central level. This provides highly current information during events but suffers from delays, misclassification, and inconsistent detail, especially when local staff are under pressure (Appendix ง‑24–25).
- **NSO (INT‑06)** produces annual or multi‑year census and survey data with strong statistical discipline, but stresses that such products cannot be updated at operational time scales without large budget increases (Appendix ง‑13–15). This limits their use for near‑real‑time monitoring.
- **MSDHS (INT‑04)** runs continuous registries but has limited forward‑looking hazard inputs, making it difficult to anticipate where vulnerable groups will be exposed in the next 2–5 years.
- **OTP (INT‑08)** uses historical hydrological data and long‑term projections to model risk for 50–100‑year horizons, but faces institutional resistance when those projections are used to argue for higher up‑front investment.

Across agencies, the v1 report observes that users want **clarity about update cadence and data “freshness”** in order to select appropriate datasets for long‑term planning, annual budgeting, or post‑event response.

### 4.2 Implications for NCAIF / CRDB components

For CRDB and NCAIF, this implies that:

- every **product entry and dataset in the baseline registry** should carry explicit fields for:
  - update frequency category (continuous/system‑driven, event‑driven, monthly, annual/periodic, project‑based),
  - date of last update,
  - typical lag between real‑world change and data availability.
- the **risk and area profiles** and **loss‑and‑damage pages** should explicitly differentiate between:
  - stable baseline indicators (e.g. census‑based socio‑economic variables),
  - recent event records and post‑event assessments, and
  - projections and scenarios.
- NCAIF should provide guidance on **which data types are appropriate for which decisions**, echoing the caution in Chapter 2 §2.4 about using event‑driven, uncertain, or lagged data for budget or regulatory decisions.

Relevant interviews and anchors: **INT‑04, INT‑06, INT‑08, INT‑11**; Chapter 2 §§2.3–2.4; Appendix ง‑10, ง‑13–15, ง‑18–19, ง‑24–25.

## 5. Loss, damage, and economic‑risk information

### 5.1 What stakeholders are asking for

The v1 interim report (§§2.4–2.6) and Appendix ง show a strong, recurring concern that **current systems record impacts but do not translate them into economic loss and risk metrics that are defensible in macroeconomic, budgeting, or financial‑sector contexts**.

- **DDPM (INT‑11)** records detailed counts of affected people, assets, and relief payments, but does not systematically classify these into loss categories aligned with Sendai or IPCC‑style loss‑and‑damage frameworks. Appendix ง‑24–25 notes difficulties in valuing indirect losses and cross‑sector impacts.
- **NESDC (INT‑05)** is developing methodologies for “ความสูญเสียและความเสียหายด้านเศรษฐกิจที่แท้จริง”, explicitly distinguishing them from relief payouts. However, current work focuses mainly on direct agricultural losses; data for indirect and long‑term economic effects remain scarce (Appendix ง‑11–12).
- **Commercial banks (INT‑09)** seek damage functions that relate hazard attributes (e.g. flood depth, duration) to financial loss at asset level. Appendix ง‑20–21 notes that they currently rely on external studies and lack nationally endorsed functions.

Appendix ค §§2.1, 2.10, 2.12 and Chapter 2 §2.6 cluster these into use cases for post‑event assessment, macroeconomic evaluation, and financial stress testing.

### 5.2 Implications for NCAIF / CRDB components

NCAIF and CRDB can respond by:

- defining a **loss‑and‑damage data structure** in the baseline registry and area profiles that clearly separates:
  - recorded physical impacts (counts, damaged assets, relief payments),
  - estimated direct economic loss, and
  - estimated indirect / systemic economic loss where available.
- documenting the **methods and limitations** of any economic‑loss estimates, in line with the transparency expectations in Chapter 2 §2.4 and Appendix ค Section 4.
- providing **standard report templates and summary views** for:
  - post‑event impact reports (DDPM, MSDHS, local authorities),
  - macroeconomic loss‑and‑damage syntheses (NESDC),
  - financial‑sector risk briefs (banks, supervisors).

This would translate the interview‑derived need for “ข้อมูลความสูญเสียทางเศรษฐกิจที่แท้จริง” into concrete NCAIF components: loss‑and‑damage pages, downloadable structured datasets, and decision‑ready briefing materials.

Relevant interviews and anchors: **INT‑05, INT‑09, INT‑11**; Chapter 2 §§2.4–2.6; Appendix ง‑11–12, ง‑20–21, ง‑24–25; Appendix ค‑2.1, 2.10, 2.12.

## 6. Vulnerability and resilience information

### 6.1 What stakeholders are asking for

While many interviews can describe hazards and exposure, several emphasise that **vulnerability, resilience, and adaptive‑capacity information is comparatively under‑developed**.

- **MSDHS (INT‑04)** seeks indicators that distinguish different vulnerable sub‑groups (children, older people, persons with disabilities, pregnant women, diverse gender groups) and link them to specific hazards and service pathways (Appendix ง‑9–10).
- **DDPM (INT‑11)** notes difficulty in quantifying local capacity to manage disasters; current systems rely on narrative after‑action reviews more than measurable adaptive‑capacity indicators.
- **NSO (INT‑06)** points to existing socio‑economic indicators (indebtedness, asset ownership, land tenure, employment) that could serve as vulnerability determinants if linked appropriately.
- **NXPO (INT‑07)** looks for resilience or carrying‑capacity metrics that can inform national adaptation strategies and research agendas.
- **UDDC (INT‑10)** explicitly uses the concept of “ความเปราะบางซ้อนทับ”, where socio‑economic vulnerability interacts with physical exposure at neighbourhood level.

Chapter 2 §2.5 summarises this as a demand for “ข้อมูลด้านความเปราะบางและขีดความสามารถในการปรับตัว” that goes beyond hazard‑only indices.

### 6.2 Implications for NCAIF / CRDB components

This pattern reinforces several design choices already embedded in the CDM and NCAIF:

- the **vulnerability and resilience indicator layer** should:
  - use NSO and other baseline providers as primary sources for social and institutional determinants;
  - define **vulnerability frameworks and indices** that can be transparently derived from those determinants (consistent with Appendix ก and the CDM description in the main report);
  - support both **national‑scale indices** (for macro policy) and **localised vulnerability profiles** (for MSDHS, DLA, UDDC use cases).
- NCAIF content should include **explanatory pages and dashboards** that:
  - make clear that risk = hazard × exposure × vulnerability, and
  - show how vulnerability and capacity indicators modify risk patterns across areas and groups.

Relevant interviews and anchors: **INT‑04, INT‑06, INT‑07, INT‑10, INT‑11**; Chapter 2 §§2.3, 2.5–2.6; Appendix ก (CDM – vulnerability structures); Appendix ง‑9–10, ง‑13–15, ง‑16–17, ง‑22–23, ง‑24–25; Appendix ค‑2.5, 2.8.

## 7. Decision‑support and briefing needs

### 7.1 What stakeholders are asking for

Appendix ค clusters interview‑derived use cases into six main groups (emergency response, local planning, social protection, macroeconomic planning, infrastructure resilience, financial‑sector risk). Across these, a common pattern appears in Chapter 2 §2.6: **users want decision‑ready packs, not only exploratory tools**.

Examples include:

- **Emergency response and post‑event assessment (Appendix ค‑2.1; INT‑11, INT‑04):** DDPM and MSDHS need standard post‑event summaries – which event, which area, who was affected, and what relief or follow‑up is required – with exportable reports rather than raw tables alone.
- **Local planning and budget justification (Appendix ค‑2.4; INT‑02, INT‑10):** DLA and UDDC emphasise that local governments must attach concise evidence packs to budget proposals. They need risk and area profiles, key indicators, and narrative explanations that can be attached directly to “คำของบประมาณ”.
- **Social protection and vulnerable‑group targeting (Appendix ค‑2.5; INT‑04):** MSDHS requires overlays of hazard and vulnerable‑group locations with guidance on prioritisation and service design.
- **Macroeconomic planning (Appendix ค‑2.10; INT‑05, INT‑07):** NESDC and NXPO need summary products that translate hazard and exposure information into economic narratives and indicator sets suitable for plans and national reports.
- **Infrastructure resilience planning (Appendix ค‑2.8; INT‑08, INT‑10):** OTP and UDDC look for corridor‑level or city‑level briefs that combine hazard projections, asset exposure, and indicative risk metrics to support design choices.
- **Financial‑sector risk analysis (Appendix ค‑2.12; INT‑09):** banks need standardised stress‑test input packs (hazard layers, damage functions, usage notes) rather than heterogeneous third‑party products.

### 7.2 Implications for NCAIF / CRDB components

For NCAIF and CRDB, these demands translate directly into product components that Section 2 can foreshadow:

- **Policy‑maker centre and briefing packs**
  - curated country‑level and provincial‑level summaries of risk, vulnerability, and adaptation progress, aligned with NESDC/NXPO needs.
- **Risk and area profile pages**
  - province, LAO/municipality, and sector profiles designed to be exportable as PDF or print‑ready packs for DLA, MSDHS, and local actors.
- **Emergency and post‑event views**
  - standardised event dashboards and printable summaries drawing on DDPM and MSDHS data structures.
- **Infrastructure and financial‑sector inserts**
  - targeted briefings and reference pages that compile the baseline hazard, exposure, and uncertainty information required by OTP and banks, with explicit caveats and recommended interpretation.

These components are already anticipated in the NCAIF sitemap and CDM‑derived product set described in Chapter 1 and Appendix ข / ก; the interviews in Appendix ง / ค provide the demand‑side justification.

Relevant interviews and anchors: **INT‑02, INT‑04, INT‑05, INT‑07, INT‑08, INT‑09, INT‑10, INT‑11**; Chapter 2 §2.6; Appendix ค‑2.1–2.8, 2.10, 2.12.

## 8. Summary for Section 2 – 5.3.2

Synthesising the interview and use‑case evidence from Chapter 2 §§2.3–2.6 and Appendices ง/ค, Section 2 – 5.3.2 can credibly state that:

1. **Baseline and indicator demand** centres on the need for trusted, endorsed reference datasets and indicators that can be reused across planning, reporting, and evaluation.
2. **Spatial‑scale and granularity gaps** emerge where provincial summaries are used to support municipal, corridor, or asset‑level decisions; stakeholders explicitly request profiles and crosswalks aligned to their decision scales.
3. **Update frequency and timeliness** are as important as the data themselves; agencies need clarity on cadence and “freshness” to choose appropriate inputs for long‑term planning, annual budgets, or event response.
4. **Loss, damage, and economic‑risk information** is a shared weak point: current systems record impacts and relief, but do not yet deliver widely accepted economic‑loss metrics for macroeconomic and financial decisions.
5. **Vulnerability and resilience information** lags behind hazard information; agencies want stronger, subgroup‑sensitive and institutional indicators that can be integrated into risk assessments.
6. **Decision‑support and briefing needs** cut across sectors: users repeatedly ask for exportable, narrative‑plus‑indicator packs linked to their workflows, rather than raw data alone.

These patterns provide the demand‑side backbone for Section 2 – 5.3.2 and directly motivate CRDB components such as the baseline registry, risk and area profiles, loss‑and‑damage module, vulnerability and resilience indicators, and decision‑support/briefing products in NCAIF.
