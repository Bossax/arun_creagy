## Expanded synthesis by cluster

### 1. Long-term projections and forward-looking scenarios

This is one of the clearest recurring needs. Agencies that must plan budgets, infrastructure, relocation, or financial exposure repeatedly report that historical records are not enough. The need is not only for more data, but for **accepted forward-looking evidence** that can survive policy and budget scrutiny.

### 2. Authoritative baselines and a trusted reference point

Several interviews reveal that the problem is not simply “no data,” but **multiple competing datasets without a trusted endorsement mechanism**. This is especially strong in NXPO and NESDC, but it also appears in sectoral planning contexts such as OTP.

### 3. Metadata, dictionaries, and discoverability

Metadata is not a technical side issue. It is a practical operating need. MSDHS, NSO, DGA, and FGD1 all point to the same problem: users waste time searching, calling source agencies, or guessing how to interpret fields.

### 4. Operationally relevant granularity

The interviews show a repeated mismatch between currently available scales and decision scales. Provincial summaries may work for national overviews, but many real users need municipality, sub-district, corridor, or asset-level relevance.

### 5. Economic loss and damage

The strongest pattern here is that present systems often capture **counts, compensation, or indices**, but not defensible estimates of true loss and damage. This makes it difficult to support macroeconomic planning, post-event assessment, and risk-finance applications.

### 6. Vulnerability and resilience indicators

Many agencies can describe hazards more easily than they can describe vulnerability or adaptive capacity. Chapter 2 should therefore show that current demand is not only for hazard information, but for richer social, institutional, and subgroup-sensitive indicators.

### 7. Access, publishing, and governance pathways

The interviews also show that unmet need is partly institutional. Some datasets are difficult to share not because they do not exist, but because agencies fear privacy breaches, accountability exposure, or unclear publication rules.

## Strongest drafting takeaway

Across agencies, the most consistent unmet needs are:

1. **authoritative projections**,
2. **trusted baselines**,
3. **usable metadata and discoverability**,
4. **finer operational granularity**, and
5. **better translation from raw impact data to meaningful planning and budgeting evidence**.

These clusters should drive subsection **2.5** and also provide the demand-side backbone for subsection **2.7**.
