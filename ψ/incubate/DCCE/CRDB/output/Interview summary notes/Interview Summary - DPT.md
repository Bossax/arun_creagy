**Department:** Multi-office DPT team across planning, engineering, analysis/evaluation, and policy support functions

### 1. Role and Responsibility of the Agency

- **Key duty and roles:** DPT described its mandate across four broad areas, with the most climate-relevant functions in this interview being policy-level planning, town and country planning, land-use and urban development planning, public-works engineering support, and technical / regulatory guidance related to buildings and infrastructure.

- **Institutional role in climate adaptation:** DPT functions primarily as a regulator, planner, and technical integrator rather than a frontline disaster-response agency. Its role is to bring climate-risk considerations into land-use planning, urban form, drainage planning, flood protection, coastal protection, and design guidance for built environments.

- **Scale of intervention:** The agency works across multiple planning scales—from national, regional, basin and provincial planning to urban plans and more detailed local planning. This makes DPT a key user of hazard, exposure, infrastructure, and socio-economic data at different resolutions.

### 2. Highlighted Key Projects

- **River-basin drainage planning and flood mapping work:**

    - _Status & Scope:_ Ongoing and historically substantial. DPT described long-running work on drainage planning across multiple river basins and the production of flood-related planning maps.

    - _Technical role:_ Rainfall and related hydrological inputs are translated into mapped flood-prone areas that become base information for urban planning and engineering decisions.

    - _Climate relevance:_ DPT explicitly noted that older statistical approaches based only on historical rainfall are becoming less adequate under climate change.

- **Urban-Act / climate-resilient urban planning collaboration:**

    - _Status & Scope:_ Active collaboration with GIZ and university partners. The discussion referenced work in locations such as Chiang Mai and Phuket to produce risk-informed planning outputs.

    - _Outputs under development:_ Risk maps, planning guidance, and project concepts related to drainage, public open space, and urban adaptation measures.

    - _Strategic direction:_ The longer-term aim is to convert these pilots into guidance or design frameworks that can inform broader planning practice.

- **Green building / green urbanism guidance and urban form concepts:**

    - _Status & Scope:_ Existing and evolving. DPT referred to building-related guidance such as energy-conserving or green-building approaches, as well as planning concepts such as sponge-city and green-city thinking.

    - _Use in practice:_ These ideas are being incorporated into planning logic, but the data foundation remains uneven across hazards and scales.

### 3. Current Workflow & Data Usage

- **Hazard scope:** Flooding is the most developed hazard area in DPT’s current workflow. The interview also referenced drought, coastal erosion, and—in a more limited way—heat. Flood risk is used more directly in planning and engineering work than heat at present.

- **DPT-generated data:** DPT produces some data itself, especially land-use and building-use information gathered through its own planning work and field surveys. It also develops flood-related analyses and planning maps in selected projects. Currently, they do not request data collected at municipality levels.

- **Externally sourced data:** Much of DPT’s work depends on requesting data from other agencies, including GISTDA, ONWR / national water-planning bodies, DDPM-type hazard sources, Land Development Department, and other sectoral custodians. The agency also integrates socio-economic and statistical data from relevant sources.

- **Planning workflow:** DPT combines physical, social, economic, land-use, and hazard information to inform urban plans, zoning logic, and infrastructure-related planning. The agency emphasized that it often has to gather, translate, and reformat fragmented datasets before they become usable in planning.

- **Hydrological and engineering workflow:** For drainage and flood-related work, DPT either develops analyses itself or works with consultants / partner entities, depending on the project scale and internal capacity. The agency is trying to strengthen its own modeling capability over time.

- **Multi-scale planning challenge:** DPT repeatedly highlighted that planning needs differ by scale. Basin-scale and provincial data may exist, but city-scale or sub-city-scale planning often requires finer geospatial and engineering detail than what is currently available.

### 4. Climate Data Needs & Gaps

- **Climate-adjusted rainfall projection gap:** DPT identified climate-change-adjusted rainfall and related hydrological information as one of its most urgent unmet needs. Historical rainfall alone is no longer sufficient for drainage and flood-planning work.

- **Fine-scale urban heat data gap:** For urban planning below provincial scale, DPT reported that it effectively does not yet have usable heat data. This limits the integration of heat risk into urban plans and detailed area planning.

- **Digital / GIS-ready data gap:** One of the clearest recurring problems is that many agencies still provide data as paper-based copies, PDFs, or non-structured map outputs. DPT wants digital, spatially referenced datasets that can be reused directly in planning and analysis.

- **Geometric and engineering-detail gap:** The interview surfaced needs for higher-resolution terrain and infrastructure information—such as road elevations, drainage-system geometry, station density, and other parameters needed for better flood modeling and design.

- **Data-access and interoperability gap:** DPT currently relies heavily on ad hoc requests to other agencies. This slows work and creates uneven data quality. A more systematic inter-agency data-sharing environment would significantly improve planning efficiency.

- **Capacity gap for advanced modeling:** DPT indicated that internal capacity is improving, but high-end hydrological / hydraulic simulation still requires additional staff capacity and, at times, consultant support.

### 5. Next Steps & CRDB Integration

- **Map to implementation plan:** DPT strongly validates the need for a CRDB architecture that supports multi-scale geospatial use, engineering-ready climate inputs, and cross-agency discoverability. For DPT, a catalog is only useful if the underlying datasets are sufficiently digital, documented, and spatially actionable.

- **Seed use cases for WP3 Conceptual Data Model (CDM):**

    - _Scenario 1:_ “Using climate-adjusted rainfall projections, flood-prone area maps, and local infrastructure geometry to support drainage and flood-protection planning in urban areas.”

    - _Scenario 2:_ “Overlaying flood, drought, coastal, land-use, and socio-economic data to guide urban plans, land-use controls, and resilient city form.”

    - _Scenario 3:_ “Applying finer-scale urban heat information to support detailed planning decisions on green space, open space, and heat-resilient urban design.”

- **Design implications for CRDB / NCAIF:** DPT’s case shows that CRDB should not stop at thematic hazard maps. It should progressively support planning-grade data services, including better metadata, machine-readable spatial formats, minimum required datasets for risk assessment, and eventual integration of climate projections into sector design workflows.

- **Planned participation:** The interview indicates DPT should be treated as a core stakeholder for the April workshop and later design feedback, particularly on the needs of planning and engineering users who work below broad national map scales.
