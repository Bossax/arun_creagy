The life cycle of the climate service development process can be structured using the **Design Study Methodology**, which outlines a sequential, yet iterative, process composed of nine stages grouped into three main phases: **Precondition, Core, and Analysis**.

**1. Precondition Phase (Problem Characterisation with Human-Centred Design)** This 'front end' phase focuses on building a cross-disciplinary understanding of the project, identifying promising collaborations, and defining team roles. It is heavily grounded in human-centred design to ensure the final service is useful, usable, and tailored to the end-users. The key steps in this phase include:

- **Knowledge exchange:** Fostering a shared understanding between climate scientists and designers regarding the primary purpose of the project and the main domain and informational challenges.
- **Understanding the problem space:** Engaging with industry stakeholders through workshops, surveys, and interviews to explore the industry landscape, user needs, and potential applications for climate predictions.
- **Defining the role of design:** Determining specific contexts of use, defining primary objectives, and developing a clear design brief for the climate service prototype.
- **Understanding data and user capacity:** Analyzing the complex, probabilistic climate prediction data alongside the specific questions users need to answer.
- **Characterizing user requirements:** Gathering user focus groups to precisely characterize the end-users and confirm how the information will actually be utilized in their day-to-day decision-making (e.g., for planning, maintenance, or investment decisions).

**2. Core Phase (Visualisation Design and Evaluation)** This phase moves into the active design, implementation, and deployment of the climate service system.

- **Design and Implementation:** An interdisciplinary team—often including design researchers, climate scientists, and data designers—collaborates to translate probabilistic data into an accessible user interface. This involves rapid experimentation with visual design vocabularies and interface prototypes to balance scientific comprehensiveness with clear, aesthetic communication.
- **Deployment and Evaluation:** Early versions of the prototype are formally evaluated with a subset of potential end-users. Developers observe how users freely explore the tool and ask them to perform realistic tasks to gauge the product's usefulness, user-friendliness, and ability to cover their daily information needs.

**3. Analysis Phase (User Engagement and Reflection)** The final phase focuses on reflection, writing, and outward-facing validation.

- **Multiple channels of communication:** Stakeholder engagement is treated as an integral component, utilizing diverse communication channels such as web applications, interactive exhibition modules, media coverage, and industry conferences to reach different target audiences.
- **Reflection:** The team reflects on the design process and user feedback to suggest future improvements, refine how probabilities are communicated, and identify new ways to increase the service's trustworthiness.

While these phases provide a structured framework, **the development process is not strictly linear and can loop back to any of the preceding stages** to refine the service based on new insights, user feedback, or data constraints.

# Design challenges in climate services
## Domain challenge
The primary domain (or sectoral) challenge in climate services is to make innovative information and services useful and usable in a complex landscape of users that are unknown or hard to access and for a not yet established market ([Buontempo et al., 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405880716300814?__cf_chl_tk=O1O_jF9_apGGaHQkd0yBFmYVs4L2K76nY9Jgwvu3S8c-1777425263-1.0.1.1-bW4kAFdIPJod_S8m4NA8upNjRneWg_qCxGQmL0zUnRU#b0020)). The objective of this research was to stimulate a currently embryonic climate services market. The climate services science domain is new and its application in the industry sector is still emerging. As a consequence, many factors may be unknown, such as 
- users and their decision framing
- decision-making practices
- use cases
- tasks
- data

Moreover, the quality (or, more generally, the _skill_ attributes) of the predictions can be uneven and in some cases low ([Brunet et al., 2010](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405880716300814?__cf_chl_tk=O1O_jF9_apGGaHQkd0yBFmYVs4L2K76nY9Jgwvu3S8c-1777425263-1.0.1.1-bW4kAFdIPJod_S8m4NA8upNjRneWg_qCxGQmL0zUnRU#b0005), [Weisheimer and Palmer, 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405880716300814?__cf_chl_tk=O1O_jF9_apGGaHQkd0yBFmYVs4L2K76nY9Jgwvu3S8c-1777425263-1.0.1.1-bW4kAFdIPJod_S8m4NA8upNjRneWg_qCxGQmL0zUnRU#b0245)). This can present a barrier to engaging potential end users, who face pressures of time and cost/benefits balance, and so remain sceptical to participate in a process when benefits to them may be unknown, poorly demonstrated or remote ([Bruno Soares and Dessai, 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405880716300814?__cf_chl_tk=O1O_jF9_apGGaHQkd0yBFmYVs4L2K76nY9Jgwvu3S8c-1777425263-1.0.1.1-bW4kAFdIPJod_S8m4NA8upNjRneWg_qCxGQmL0zUnRU#b0010)). This presented a challenging problem in the design process; the objective and readiness level created a requirement to develop the problem space and the [solution space](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/solution-space) at the same time.
## Informational challenge

The primary informational challenge in climate services concerns the probabilistic nature of climate predictions. Ensemble based predictions such as seasonal predictions are probabilistic in nature, meaning that they give the probability of occurrence of certain outcomes, as projected by the model, rather than deterministic, single future prediction.

Another informational challenge in climate services is the requirement to communicate and present complex scientific information to decision makers in industry. To popularise and broaden the use of climate services and to realise wider impact it is necessary to spread innovative information and products beyond the community of developers and engaged users. Because of its probabilistic nature, climate predictions are often perceived as untailored, unintuitive, hard to understand, and thus difficult to apply in a decision making context, particularly taking into account the varying levels of expertise among users ([Bruno Soares and Dessai, 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405880716300814?__cf_chl_tk=O1O_jF9_apGGaHQkd0yBFmYVs4L2K76nY9Jgwvu3S8c-1777425263-1.0.1.1-bW4kAFdIPJod_S8m4NA8upNjRneWg_qCxGQmL0zUnRU#b0010), [Dilling and Lemos, 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405880716300814?__cf_chl_tk=O1O_jF9_apGGaHQkd0yBFmYVs4L2K76nY9Jgwvu3S8c-1777425263-1.0.1.1-bW4kAFdIPJod_S8m4NA8upNjRneWg_qCxGQmL0zUnRU#b0055)).
